# 06 — Nutrients (Organic)

Organic feeds the soil microbiome, which feeds the plant. This is a fundamentally different mental model from synthetic fertilization (`06-nutrients-synthetic.md`), and the two should not be mixed mid-grow — switching directions kills the biology that organic systems depend on. This file is the catalog of *inputs*: dry amendments, mineral additions, liquid ferts, KNF/JADAM ferments, compost teas, and bottled organic lines. The system context (no-till, soil web, mix-building) lives in `04-medium-living-soil.md`. Cross-reference with `05-water-quality.md` (chlorine and chloramine kill the workforce — dechlorination is mandatory), `07-deficiencies.md` (organic deficiencies present and resolve differently — slower fix, can't shotgun a foliar at the problem), `06-nutrients-synthetic.md` (the contrast and the warning against blending models).

## How to Use This File

1. **Pick your model first** — true living soil (no-till, microbe-driven), container organic (bottled or amended), or hybrid (synthetic base + biological top-up). Each uses this file differently.
2. **Front-load the soil** — amend at mix-build with slow-release nutrients sized for the grow length. The soil should carry most of the food.
3. **Top-dress per release rate** — fast amendments (blood, fish hydrolysate, FPJ) for week-of demand; slow (bone, langbeinite, rock dust) for season-long.
4. **Layer liquid inputs** for signaling and biological top-up — kelp for hormones, ACT for biology, LAB for protection.
5. **Never use chlorinated water on living soil** — see `05-water-quality.md` for dechlorination methods.
6. **Patience** — organic fixes take 5–10 days to show vs 24–48 hours for synthetic. Don't double-dose if results are slow.

## Critical Concept — "Feed the Soil, Not the Plant"

Synthetic nutes deliver mineral salts directly to the root zone in immediately-available form. The plant takes what's in solution; the medium is just structural. Organic inputs are different — they're food for the soil biology (bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes) which then mineralize the inputs into plant-available form on the plant's demand schedule.

| Aspect | Synthetic | Organic |
|---|---|---|
| Form delivered | Mineral salt ions in solution | Carbon-based amendments + bio-active compounds |
| Speed of availability | Hours | Days to weeks |
| pH adjustment of inputs | Mandatory every mix | Not applicable in living soil; biology buffers |
| Runoff EC tracking | Critical metric | Mostly irrelevant in living soil |
| Sensitivity to chlorine | Low | High — chloramine sterilizes |
| Foliar response to deficiency | Visible in 24–48h | Visible in 5–10 days |
| Relationship to PPFD | Tight (EC scales with light) | Looser — soil buffers |
| Salt accumulation risk | High in coco/hydro | Low, biology cycles excess |

**Practical implications:**
- Don't measure runoff EC in living soil and panic — it will read low because plants are pulling minerals slowly through biological release. The metric is plant appearance, not EC.
- Don't pH-adjust water before watering living soil. The biology buffers around 6.3–6.8 in well-built soil. Tap pH 7.5 → soil pH still 6.5.
- A "deficiency" appearing in week 1 of flower in living soil is often microbial lag — the system is shifting from N to P/K demand and the biology hasn't caught up. Wait 5–7 days before adding fast-release amendments.
- Chlorinated water does measurable harm. Use rainwater, dechlorinated tap (24h aeration for chlorine; ascorbic acid or catalytic carbon for chloramine), or RO with light remineralization.

## Critical Concept — Release Rate Spectrum

Organic amendments don't release all at once. Where they sit on the speed spectrum dictates how to time top-dressing relative to plant demand.

| Speed | Amendments | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **Fast (days–1 week)** | Blood meal, fish hydrolysate, FPJ, ACT, urea-bearing manures, insect frass, bat guano (high-N) | Use to address active demand or specific stage shift |
| **Medium (1–4 weeks)** | Feather meal, alfalfa meal, kelp meal, neem cake, crab meal, EWC, bottled organic liquids | Top-dress 1–2 weeks ahead of demand window |
| **Slow (1–3 months)** | Bone meal, fish bone meal, langbeinite, gypsum, soft rock phosphate | Mix into base soil; top-dress at flip if needed |
| **Very slow (3+ months / amendment-only)** | Rock dust (basalt, glacial, azomite), oyster shell flour, dolomite lime, greensand | Mix into base soil only; remineralization, not feeding |

**Diagnostic shortcut for top-dress timing:**
- Veg-to-flower transition (high N pull-back, P/K ramp): top-dress fast P/K (insect frass) at flip + medium-release (kelp, alfalfa) week before flip
- Mid-flower bulk (week 3–5): top-dress medium-release K-leaning (langbeinite, kelp, BAS Build-A-Bloom)
- Late-flower (week 7+): no top-dress; let stored soil charge fade naturally

## DRY AMENDMENTS — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Blood meal (12-0-0)

**Role:** Fastest organic N source. Excellent for catching N deficiency in veg or boosting heavy-feeding strains.

**Pros:**
- Cheap, widely available
- Acts within days
- High protein, also feeds soil bacteria

**Cons:**
- "Hot" — easy to burn plants if applied fresh and uncomposted
- Strong smell during release (some growers report rodent attraction)
- Some sourcing concerns (slaughterhouse byproducts; choose grass-fed if available)
- Acidifies soil slightly over time

**Dose:** 1/4 – 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix; 1–2 tbsp per 5-gallon top-dress.

**Pitfalls:** Top-dressing near the stem can burn root crown. Always scratch into top inch and water in, away from stalk.

### Feather meal (13-0-0)

**Role:** Slower-release N alternative to blood meal. Less burn risk, longer feeding window.

**Pros:**
- Slow steady N release over 2–4 weeks
- Less burn risk than blood meal
- Lower odor

**Cons:**
- Sourcing varies — feedlot poultry feathers may carry antibiotic / hormone residues. Look for organic-certified sources
- Slower to break down than blood meal — won't fix an active deficiency fast

**Dose:** 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix; equivalent for top-dress.

### Bone meal (3-15-0) and Fish bone meal (4-17-0)

**Role:** Slow phosphorus + long-term calcium. Soil-build amendment, not corrective.

**Pros:**
- Reliable P source for the entire grow if mixed in at start
- Adds Ca slowly without pH disturbance
- Fish bone meal slightly faster and smaller particle than animal bone meal

**Cons:**
- Slow — won't catch an active P deficiency in flower (top-dressing P-type guano is faster)
- Vegan growers: skip bone meal, use soft rock phosphate + insect frass + comfrey

**Dose:** 1 cup per cubic foot in mix.

### Kelp meal (1-0-2)

**Role:** Trace minerals + plant hormones (cytokinins, auxins, gibberellins). Not a primary feeder — a *signaling* and *resilience* amendment.

**Pros:**
- 60+ trace minerals
- Drought, heat, and cold tolerance enhancement (peer-reviewed across non-cannabis crops)
- Cytokinin content stimulates lateral growth and bud-set
- Pairs with everything

**Cons:**
- Not a primary NPK source — supplement, not base
- Slow release in dry form (liquid kelp is faster)
- Sourcing matters — wild-harvested Atlantic (Ascophyllum nodosum) is the standard

**Dose:** 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix; 1 tbsp per 5-gallon top-dress every 2–4 weeks.

### Alfalfa meal (3-1-2)

**Role:** Balanced low-NPK feeder + triacontanol (a natural plant growth regulator). Excellent veg amendment.

**Pros:**
- Triacontanol stimulates root growth and chlorophyll content (Tier 1 — non-cannabis research, generally accepted)
- Cheap and widely available (livestock feed grade is fine)
- Builds soil organic matter

**Cons:**
- Heats up if over-applied (can compost in pot)
- Some sources contain pesticide residue (livestock feed grade may be sprayed) — organic is preferred

**Dose:** 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix; 1 tbsp per 5-gallon top-dress in veg / early flower.

### Neem cake (6-1-2)

**Role:** Nitrogen feed + IPM benefit. The single highest-value amendment for combined nutrition and pest suppression.

**Pros:**
- Azadirachtin and limonoid content suppress soil-borne pests (root knot nematodes, thrips pupae, fungus gnat larvae)
- Steady N release over 2–3 weeks
- Pairs with karanja cake for broader IPM coverage

**Cons:**
- Strong smell during initial breakdown (1–2 weeks)
- Sourcing — Indian-origin cold-pressed is the gold standard

**Dose:** 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix; 1/4 cup per 5-gallon top-dress. Pair 50/50 with karanja for synergy.

### Crustacean / crab meal (4-3-0)

**Role:** Chitin-rich amendment that triggers plant chitinase production — a natural anti-fungal defense. Also feeds chitin-eating bacteria that compete with fungal pathogens.

**Pros:**
- Real IPM contribution (chitin-mediated immune signaling, well-documented in agricultural literature)
- Adds calcium
- Slow steady release

**Cons:**
- Specific sourcing — must be cold-processed crab/shrimp meal, not random "shellfish dust"
- Can smell during initial breakdown

**Dose:** 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix.

### Insect frass (2-2-2)

**Role:** Balanced fast-release feed + chitin source. Newer to organic cannabis but rapidly becoming mainstream.

**Pros:**
- Faster release than crab meal
- Same chitin-immune-signaling benefit
- Includes microbe-associated molecular patterns that prime plant immunity

**Cons:**
- Expensive per pound vs crab meal
- Quality varies by insect source (mealworm vs black soldier fly larvae) — BSFL frass is the most-studied

**Dose:** 1/2 cup per cubic foot in mix; 1–2 tbsp per 5-gallon top-dress.

### Earthworm castings (EWC)

**Role:** The "always" amendment. Light NPK + huge biological + humic acid contribution. Buffers everything.

**Pros:**
- Adds biology (bacteria, fungi, protozoa) to every mix
- Humic acids buffer pH and improve nutrient availability
- Improves moisture retention and tilth
- Compatible with every mix and stage
- Use as base for compost teas (1 cup per 5 gallon brew)

**Cons:**
- Quality varies wildly by source — some "EWC" is mostly bedding material with few worms involved
- Buy from a reputable source; visual cue is dark, fluffy, faintly earthy-smelling, no strong odor

**Dose:** 1–2 cups per cubic foot in mix; 1/2 cup per 5-gallon top-dress every 2–4 weeks. Cannot really be over-applied.

### Mineral amendments — Gypsum, Oyster shell, Dolomite, Langbeinite

| Amendment | Use case |
|---|---|
| **Gypsum (CaSO4)** | Ca + S without pH lift. Use when soil pH is already correct and you need Ca/S. 1/2 cup/cu ft mix |
| **Oyster shell flour** | Slow Ca + slight pH lift. Pairs well with high-CEC media (coco). Long-term Ca reservoir. 1/2 cup/cu ft |
| **Dolomite lime** | Ca + Mg + significant pH lift. Use ONLY if soil pH is below target. Risky — can over-correct. 1/2 – 1 cup/cu ft |
| **Langbeinite (K-Mag, sulpomag)** | Slow K + Mg + S. The late-flower workhorse, especially in living soil. 1/4 – 1/2 cup/cu ft |
| **Glacial rock dust / Basalt** | Broad trace mineral remineralization. Very slow. 2–4 cups/cu ft mix only |
| **Azomite** | 67+ trace elements. Similar role to rock dust but smaller particle, slightly faster release. 1 cup/cu ft |
| **Greensand (glauconite)** | Slow K + Fe + soil structure. 1 cup/cu ft mix only |

## LIQUID ORGANIC INPUTS

### Fish hydrolysate (cold-processed)

**Role:** Fast-acting liquid N + amino acids + fish oil. Distinct from fish emulsion (heat-processed) — hydrolysate retains proteins, hormones, and oils that emulsion loses.

**Difference vs fish emulsion** (Tier 2 — multiple commercial sources confirm):
- **Hydrolysate**: enzymatic cold processing, preserves fats/oils/amino acid chains/hormones; mild odor; feeds fungi (oils stay in)
- **Emulsion**: heat-processed, oils removed, broken-down protein; stronger odor; bacterial-favoring

**Why it matters:** living soil and KNF setups depend on fungal-bacterial balance. Hydrolysate supports fungi; emulsion biases bacterial. Use hydrolysate as the default.

**Brands:** Neptune's Harvest (widely available), True Organic, Alaska Fish Fertilizer (which is technically an emulsion — read labels)

**Dose:** 1–2 tbsp per gallon, weekly to biweekly through veg and early flower. Foliar at half strength.

### Liquid kelp / kelp extract

**Role:** Hormone signaling, stress reduction, foliar-friendly trace minerals.

**Use:** Foliar spray (1 tbsp/gallon, every 2 weeks); root drench (same dose); in compost tea recipes.

**Brands:** Neptune's Harvest Kelp, BAS Liquid Kelp, generic Ascophyllum nodosum extracts.

### Compost tea / Aerated Compost Tea (ACT)

Full section below.

### EWC tea (worm tea)

Gentler version of ACT — soak EWC in dechlorinated water (no aeration required for non-ACT version), 12–24 hours, dilute and apply. Beginner-friendly biological boost without ACT's brewing complexity.

**Dose:** 1 cup EWC per 5 gallon water, soak 12–24h, apply at full strength as drench.

### Molasses (unsulfured blackstrap)

**Role:** Carbon source for microbes. Used in tea recipes (1–2 tbsp per 5 gallon brew) and occasionally as standalone soil drench (1 tbsp/gallon).

**Caveat:** sulfured molasses kills microbes — must be unsulfured. "Blackstrap" means the dark high-mineral grade.

### Bokashi leachate

**Role:** Anaerobic ferment liquid from bokashi composting. Highly acidic (pH 3–4) and concentrated.

**Use:** Heavily diluted (1:100 minimum) as soil drench. Suppresses pathogens, adds beneficial anaerobes.

## KNF / JADAM INPUTS

Korean Natural Farming (KNF, founded by **Cho Han-Kyu**, b. 1935, Janong Natural Farming Institute) and JADAM (developed by his son **Cho Youngsang**) are integrated organic systems based on indigenous microorganisms (IMOs) and homemade ferments. The system context lives in `04-medium-living-soil.md` — this section is the input catalog. (Tier 3 — Cho Han-Kyu, *Cho's Global Natural Farming*; Cho Youngsang, *JADAM Organic Farming*.)

### FPJ (Fermented Plant Juice)

**Inputs:** Young, vigorous plant tips (mugwort, comfrey, dandelion, bamboo shoots — anything growing fast) + brown sugar.

**Recipe:**
- Plant material chopped roughly
- Brown sugar at 1/3 the weight of plant material (up to 1/2 for low-moisture plants)
- Layer in jar, weight down, cover with cloth (NOT airtight)
- Ferment 5–7 days at room temperature
- Strain liquid; store dark, cool

**Use:** 2 ml/L water in vegetative stage, 1 ml/L in pre-flower transition. Cannabis-specific application: terpene boost via foliar feed at 1 ml/L (Tier 3 — Research Gardens / KNF cannabis growers).

**Shelf life:** 6–12 months in cool dark storage.

### FFJ (Fermented Fruit Juice)

**Inputs:** Ripe, sweet, sugar-rich fruit (banana, papaya, mango, melons) + brown sugar.

**Recipe:** Same as FPJ but brown sugar at 3/4 to 1:1 weight of fruit. Ferment 5–7 days.

**Use:** 1 ml/L pre-flower, 2 ml/L through flower. K-leaning support during bud development.

**Shelf life:** 6–12 months cool/dark.

### LAB (Lactic Acid Bacteria Serum)

**Inputs:** Rice wash water + raw milk.

**Recipe:**
- Wash rice with water (use the cloudy starchy water)
- Cover, ferment 5–7 days until separates
- Pour off middle layer, mix 1:10 with raw milk
- Ferment 5–7 days more
- Strain off curd; liquid (whey) is LAB serum
- Stabilize with brown sugar (1:1 by weight) for storage

**Use:** 1–2 ml/L as foliar (anti-fungal, mildew suppression); 1 ml/L as soil drench (digestion support); odor reduction in compost; broiler-house and cleanroom applications outside cannabis.

**Shelf life:** 6+ months stabilized with sugar.

### OHN (Oriental Herbal Nutrient)

**Inputs:** Garlic, ginger, angelica (2 parts), cinnamon, licorice root + Korean rice wine or 40%+ ethanol.

**Recipe:**
- Each herb extracted separately in alcohol for 7–14 days
- Then combined in equal parts (except 2× angelica)
- Stored as concentrated tincture

**Use:** 1 ml/L as foliar plant-immune signaling; 0.5 ml/L as soil drench. Stress recovery, disease resistance.

**Shelf life:** Indefinite (alcohol-preserved).

### JMS (JADAM Microbial Solution)

**Inputs:** Indigenous microbes from local leaf mold + boiled potato + sea salt + dechlorinated water.

**Recipe (Tier 3 — Cho Youngsang):**
- 50 g leaf mold (from base of trees in healthy forest near grow location)
- 200 g boiled potato (mashed)
- 1.5 tbsp sea salt
- In 5 gallons rainwater or dechlorinated water
- Anaerobic ferment 2–3 days (less in summer, more in winter)
- Use immediately — no shelf life

**Use:** Dilute 1:10 for soil drench, 1:20 for foliar. Application is primary microbial inoculant for any organic system.

**Why it's "JADAM"**: anaerobic, free, indigenous — opposite philosophy from aerated tea, claims equivalent or better results at zero cost.

### JLF (JADAM Liquid Fertilizer)

**Inputs:** Wild grass, crop residues, kitchen scraps, weeds — whatever organic plant material is available locally.

**Recipe:** Submerge in water in a sealed barrel, anaerobic ferment 1–6 months. Longer ferment = higher concentration.

**Use:** Dilute 1:100 as soil drench.

**Pros:** Free, uses waste material, builds long-term fertility.

**Cons:** Strong smell during ferment. Outdoor or well-ventilated location only.

### JADAM Wetting Agent (JWA)

**Inputs:** Soap nut (Sapindus mukorossi) or similar saponin-rich plant + water.

**Recipe:** Boil soap nuts in water, strain. Concentrate.

**Use:** Spray adjuvant — improves leaf coverage of foliar applications. 1–2 ml/L mixed with foliar input.

### JADAM Sulfur

**Inputs:** Sublimed sulfur + sodium hydroxide + water.

**Use:** Homemade fungicide (powdery mildew, bud rot suppression) and IPM tool. Sprayed at low concentrations during veg only — sulfur on flower can affect terpene profile.

**Caveat:** sodium hydroxide handling requires safety gear. Several pre-made commercial alternatives (Regalia, Eagle 20, Greenshield) exist for growers who want sulfur-class IPM without making their own.

## BOTTLED ORGANIC NUTRIENT LINES

Shorter profiles — these are commodity products, less unique per item than dry amendments or KNF.

### Build-A-Soil (BAS) — Craft Blend, Build-A-Bloom, Top Dresses

**Profile:** Modern living-soil-focused company; sells dry amendment blends, liquids, soil mixes, mycos, EWC. Educational content from Jeremy Silva is among the best in the industry.

**Craft Blend NPK:** 3-5-2; balanced amendment blend designed for re-amend AND top-dress AND tea.

**Doses (Tier 2 — BAS official):**
- Re-amend used soil: 2–4 cups per cubic foot
- Top-dress: 1/4 – 1 cup per plant per top-dress event
- Tea: 1/4 – 1 cup per 5 gallons

**Best fit:** intermediate-to-advanced organic growers, especially no-till. Documentation is excellent.

### Gaia Green All-Purpose 4-4-4 + Power Bloom 2-8-4

**Profile:** Canadian dry amendments, very widely used in NA organic cannabis community. Two-product simplicity: All-Purpose for veg, Power Bloom for flower.

**Schedule (Tier 2 — Gaia Green official):**
- Early veg: 3 tbsp All-Purpose per gallon medium
- Early flower: 3 tbsp at 50/50 (All-Purpose / Power Bloom) per gallon
- Mid flower: 3 tbsp at 25/75 (AP/PB) per gallon
- Late flower: 2 tbsp 100% Power Bloom per gallon
- Top-dress every 4 weeks; supplement liquid every 2 weeks

**Best fit:** beginner to intermediate organic in containers. The simplest reliable two-product organic system.

**Pitfall:** Gaia full schedule is hot for some strains — start at 2/3 dose and ramp up.

### Down to Earth (DTE)

**Profile:** US-widely-available dry amendments. Single-input bags (blood meal, kelp meal, neem cake, etc.) at fair prices. The "buy one bag at a time" company.

**Best fit:** DIY soil-builders following a Coots-style or custom recipe.

### Roots Organics

**Profile:** Complete bottled organic line designed for coco/soil. Trinity, Buddha Grow, Buddha Bloom, Terp Tea. Easier than DIY, less granular control than Coots-style.

**Best fit:** intermediate growers in coco wanting a bottled organic experience.

### General Organics GO Box (Bio-Thrive)

**Profile:** GH's organic line — six bottles in a starter kit (BioThrive Grow, BioThrive Bloom, plus accessory bottles). Entry-level bottled organic.

**Best fit:** first-time organic growers wanting the GH-style mfg-feed-chart experience.

### Espoma

**Profile:** Garden-store dry amendments (Plant-Tone, Bio-Tone, Holly-Tone). Beginner-friendly, found at any garden center in US.

**Best fit:** Casual hobby growers, outdoor + greenhouse grows.

### Dr. Earth

**Profile:** Boxed dry amendments at retail — All-Purpose, Bud & Bloom, Tomato/Vegetable. Wide retail presence, organic-certified.

**Best fit:** Casual hobby growers; less granular than Down to Earth single-input bags but easier to source.

### Nectar for the Gods

**Profile:** Calcium-heavy bottled organic line (Greek-mythology naming — Athena, Zeus Juice, etc.). Premium-priced, niche following.

**Best fit:** growers who specifically want a calcium-leaning organic stack; expensive vs alternatives.

### Earth Juice

**Profile:** Older bottled liquid line — Grow, Bloom, Catalyst. Works but feels dated; superseded by BAS/Roots/General Organics for most growers.

## Top-Dressing Strategy

**When to top-dress:**
- 1 week before flip (load up for stretch demand)
- Every 4 weeks in flower (or 2 weeks for shorter-cycle autoflowers)
- Whenever a deficiency appears AND microbial lag explanation has been ruled out (5+ days of waiting)

**How much:**
- General: 1/4 to 1 cup per 5-gallon container, depending on amendment intensity
- High-N fast amendments (blood meal, frass): 1–2 tbsp only
- Bulk amendments (EWC, compost): 1/2 to 1 cup acceptable
- Mineral amendments (langbeinite, gypsum): 1 tbsp per 5 gal

**How:**
1. Lift mulch layer if present
2. Sprinkle amendment evenly over soil surface, away from main stem
3. Scratch into top 1/2 to 1 inch with hand or rake
4. Replace mulch
5. Water in with dechlorinated water (or LAB-amended water for biology boost)

**Mulch:** straw, leaf litter, BAS coco husk, dried comfrey leaves. Mulch keeps surface biology alive — without it, the top inch dries and biology dies.

**Stage-specific top-dress recipes (Tier 3 — Coots Mix consensus + BAS schedule):**

| Stage | Top-dress mix per 5-gal pot |
|---|---|
| Pre-flip (1 week before) | 1/2 cup EWC + 1 tbsp neem cake + 1 tbsp kelp meal |
| Early flower (week 1–2) | 1/2 cup EWC + 1 tbsp insect frass + 1 tsp langbeinite |
| Mid flower (week 3–4) | 1/2 cup EWC + 2 tbsp Gaia Power Bloom (or BAS Build-A-Bloom) + 1 tbsp kelp |
| Late flower (week 5–6) | 1/2 cup EWC + 1 tsp langbeinite (K only) |
| Week 7+ | None — let stored charge fade |

## Compost Tea (ACT) — Brewing and Use

(Tier 1 — Dr. Elaine Ingham, Soil Foodweb Inc.)

**Recipe (5-gallon brew):**
- 5 gallons dechlorinated water (rainwater, RO, or aerated tap)
- 1/2 lb (about 1 cup) high-quality EWC or thermophilic compost
- 2 tbsp unsulfured blackstrap molasses (bacterial food)
- 2 tbsp liquid kelp or kelp meal in mesh bag (fungal food)
- 1–2 tsp fish hydrolysate (fungal food)
- Optional: 1 tsp humic acid

**Brewing:**
- Strong air pump (>2.5 CFM for 5-gal brew)
- 24–36 hours at 65–85°F
- Dissolved oxygen target: >6 ppm throughout
- Stir or agitate every few hours if possible

**Hard rule — shelf life:** 3–4 hours after aeration stops. Biology dies fast in stagnation. **Brew in the morning, apply same afternoon. Never store overnight.**

**Application:**
- Soil drench: full-strength, 1–2 cups per 5-gal pot
- Foliar: filter through fine mesh, dilute 1:1 with dechlorinated water, spray under canopy first thing in morning
- Frequency: every 2–4 weeks through veg and early-mid flower

**When NOT to use ACT:**
- Late flower (mold risk, especially foliar)
- If the brew smells bad (rotten, sulfurous) — went anaerobic, dump it
- If you don't have time to use it within 4 hours of stopping aeration
- On bone-dry soil — water lightly first, then drench

**Larger Ingham scale (25-gallon brew):** 1/2 lb compost + 2 tsp humic + 1–2 tbsp fish hydrolysate + 1/2 cup kelp solution, otherwise same protocol.

**Microscope verification (advanced):** Ingham recommends 400× microscope inspection to verify biology before applying. Visible flagellate protozoa, motile bacteria, and fungal hyphae = brew is good. Mostly ciliates = went anaerobic, dump it.

## Hybrid Organic + Synthetic

Hybrid setups can work, but the two systems compete in subtle ways:

**What works:**
- Synthetic base feed (Jack's 321 lite, Athena lite) + EWC top-dress + monthly kelp foliar
- Coco with synthetic-driven base + light biological inoculation
- Mineral-amended soil + occasional synthetic boost when deficiency catches you out

**What fails:**
- Mixing organic + synthetic in the same res (synthetic salts kill biology in concentrate)
- ACT applied right after a synthetic feed (chlorine carryover + salt environment kills tea biology)
- Trying to switch from organic to synthetic mid-flower (microbial die-off causes nutrient cascade)
- Over-additive sprawl — bottled organic line + synthetic base + 5 separate additives = unpredictable interactions

**Decision rule:** if hybridizing, keep the systems sequenced (synthetic feed → wait 24h → biological top-dress) rather than concurrent.

**Sustainable middle path:** low-EC synthetic base (0.8–1.2 EC) + EWC + monthly kelp foliar + occasional ACT in veg. Captures most of the upside of both.

## Quick Reference — Organic Strategy Decision Tree

```
START → What's the model?

TRUE LIVING SOIL (no-till, 30+ gal containers)?
├── Soil build → Coots Mix or BAS Living Soil
├── Top-dress → BAS Craft Blend / Gaia AP+PB / DTE single inputs
├── Liquid → ACT every 2-4 weeks + KNF stack
└── See 04-medium-living-soil.md for full system

CONTAINER ORGANIC (5-15 gal, conventional pots)?
├── Pre-amended bag (FFOF) → plain water 2-4 wks → top-dress at flip
├── Custom mix → Down to Earth single inputs + EWC + slow minerals
├── Bottled liquid → Roots Organics, BAS, General Organics
└── Beginner-friendly → Gaia AP + PB schedule

BEGINNER ORGANIC?
├── First grow → Pre-amended FFOF + Gaia AP + PB top-dress

HYBRID (mostly synthetic, some organic)?
├── Synthetic base + EWC top-dress + monthly kelp foliar
├── Sequence — synthetic feed, wait 24h, biological top-up
└── Don't mix in res

KNF/JADAM PURIST?
├── Soil → IMO collection + base mix per 04-medium-living-soil.md
```

## Common Misdiagnosis Traps

| Looks like | Actually | How to tell |
|---|---|---|
| "Deficiency" in living soil week 1 of flower | Microbial lag during stage transition | Wait 5–7 days; if it self-resolves, it was lag |
| "pH crashed" in living soil | Measuring water pH, not soil pH (irrelevant) | Test soil pH directly; in living soil, water pH doesn't matter |
| Plant burning after top-dress | Hot fresh blood meal too close to stem | Check application location; pull back, water heavily |
| ACT smells bad | Went anaerobic — biology is dead/wrong | Smell test: should smell earthy/yeasty, not rotten/sulfurous |
| Synthetic worked, organic doesn't | Switched mid-grow, killed soil biology | Wait 2–3 weeks for biology to rebuild before evaluating |
| Amendment "didn't work" | Release rate slower than demand window | Check release rate table; fast amendments take 3–7 days |
| Organic plants smaller than synthetic | Possibly true — synthetic ~20% yield bump in trials | Quality often higher (terpene/cannabinoid expression) — tradeoff |
| Mold or mildew after foliar tea | Late-flower foliar in high RH | Don't foliar tea past week 4 of flower |
| Stunted seedlings in living soil | Soil too hot for seedlings | Plant clones/seedlings in less-amended soil first, transplant up |
| Chlorinated water "killed my tea" | Confirmed — chlorine sterilizes | Always dechlorinate; see `05-water-quality.md` |

## Verification Questions Before Acting

1. What's the model — true living soil, container organic, hybrid, or KNF?
2. Container size and total medium volume?
3. What's already amended into the soil (if known)?
4. When was the last top-dress and what was applied?
5. Is the water dechlorinated? How?
6. Is the plant in microbial-lag-prone stage transition (pre-flip, week 1 of flower)?
7. Pot moisture — is biology even active? (Bone-dry soil = inactive biology)
8. Is mulch layer present?
9. Recent ACT or KNF input applied? When?
10. PPFD, temp, RH — is the plant capable of taking up more nutrients, or is environment limiting?

If 4+ unknown → don't recommend organic input changes. Get the data first.
