# 20 — Operations Hygiene and Safe Scaling Boundaries

This reference covers cultivation operations habits that help a legal home grow stay organized, clean, and resilient. It is not legal, licensing, compliance, trafficking, distribution, or commercial business advice.

Use this file when the user asks about scaling a lawful personal garden, making repeatable SOPs, improving sanitation, tracking batches, avoiding cross-contamination, or planning work without turning the skill into a commercial compliance consultant.

## Core Concept — Scale Changes Failure Modes

A small tent can survive a little sloppiness. A larger or perpetual home garden usually cannot. The main scaling risk is not exotic technique; it is ordinary errors compounding:

- One infected clone can contaminate every plant.
- A missed pH/EC calibration can affect every container.
- Poor airflow can turn one humid corner into powdery mildew or botrytis.
- Harvest timing can overwhelm drying space.
- Incomplete labels make it impossible to learn from prior cycles.

Default principle: scale only after the current setup is boringly repeatable.

## Clean Workflow

Keep movement one-way where practical:

```text
New genetics / supplies
  → quarantine / inspection
  → propagation or veg
  → flower
  → harvest
  → dry
  → trim
  → cure / storage
```

Practical rules:
- Inspect new clones, seeds, soil, amendments, and equipment before they enter the main grow space.
- Do plant work from youngest/cleanest plants to oldest/dirtiest plants.
- Avoid moving from flower back into veg without washing hands and changing gloves/tools.
- Keep separate scissors or wipe-down routines for diseased/suspect plants.
- Do not bring outdoor plant material into an indoor grow room without quarantine.

## Home-Grow SOPs Worth Writing

Short checklists beat long manuals. Keep each SOP to one screen when possible.

Suggested SOPs:
- Daily environment check: temp/RH/VPD, light timer, obvious wilt, leaks, smell/filter status.
- Feed/mix checklist: water source, EC/PPM, pH, additives order, calibration date.
- IPM scout: underside of leaves, lower canopy, sticky traps, soil surface, new growth, pest notes.
- Defoliation/training: stage limits, sanitation, what not to remove.
- Harvest day: labels, tools, wash/sanitize, hang plan, dry-room setpoints.
- Cure check: jar RH, burp cadence, mold/off-odor check, storage label.

Each SOP should include:
- Purpose.
- When to run it.
- Tools needed.
- Steps.
- Stop conditions: when to pause and ask for help.
- What to log.

## Labeling and Batch Tracking

Minimum useful labels:
- Cultivar + breeder/source.
- Plant ID.
- Germination/clone date.
- Flip date for photoperiod plants.
- Medium and container.
- Feed line or soil mix.
- Harvest date.
- Dry start/end date.
- Cure start date.

## Redundancy Planning

Prioritize failures that kill plants fastest:

1. **Heat/HVAC failure** — alarm, backup fan, lights-off emergency plan.
2. **Irrigation/pump failure** — manual watering fallback, spare pump/timer for hydro.
3. **Power outage** — know what can coast and what cannot; protect hydro aeration first.
4. **Dehumidifier failure late flower** — backup drying space or emergency airflow/dehu plan.
5. **pH/EC meter drift** — calibration solution, backup strips/drops for sanity checks.
6. **Pest outbreak** — quarantine tools, sticky traps, safe IPM inputs, removal plan for heavily infested plants.

## Time Planning

Most home growers underestimate harvest/post-harvest labor.

Rough planning prompts:
- How many plants finish in the same week?
- Where will each plant hang without touching walls or each other?
- Can the dry space hold target temp/RH for 7–14 days?
- Do you have enough clean scissors, gloves, hangers, labels, jars/bags, and hygrometers?
- Who trims, and how many hours per day can they realistically work?

If the harvest is too large for the dry/cure pipeline, reduce plant count or stagger future runs.

## Biosecurity Basics

- Quarantine new clones before introducing them to the main room.
- Keep sticky traps dated and placed consistently.
- Clean spills immediately; algae and fungus gnats love wet floors and runoff trays.
- Remove dead leaves from pots and floors.
- Sanitize scissors between plants when disease, pests, or HLVd risk is possible.
- Do not compost diseased cannabis material inside the grow area.
- Keep pets out of the grow space.

## When to Downscale

Recommend downscaling when:
- The user cannot inspect every plant at least several times per week.
- Drying/curing space is smaller than flowering output.
- Heat/RH are uncontrolled in late flower.
- Pests or disease recur cycle after cycle.
- The user is missing basic measurement tools for their chosen medium.
- The scale would exceed what is lawful where they operate.

Phrase it as crop protection, not failure:

> Fewer healthy plants beat more stressed plants. Dial the system first, then scale.

## Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Safer approach |
|---|---|
| Scaling before the current grow is repeatable | Finish one clean cycle, document it, then add complexity |
| No labels | Plant IDs and dates on every pot, tray, hang line, and jar |
| Treating IPM as emergency spray only | Scout weekly, prevent, quarantine, and keep the room clean |
| Harvest exceeds dry-room capacity | Stagger runs or reduce plant count |
| Legal/compliance questions creep in | Refuse legal determinations; suggest qualified local counsel/regulators |
| Distribution/selling questions creep in | Refuse; redirect to lawful cultivation operations only |
