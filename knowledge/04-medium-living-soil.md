# 04 — Medium: Living Soil

No-till, biology-driven cultivation. The philosophical opposite of `04-medium-hydro.md` — instead of feeding the plant directly through engineered solutions, you build a soil ecosystem and **feed the biology, the biology feeds the plant**. Recovery time is months, not days. The system rewards patience and punishes impatience. Cycle 3+ is when it really clicks. Cross-reference with `04-medium-soil.md` (synthetic-fed soil — the bridge) and `04-medium-coco.md` / `04-medium-hydro.md` (opposite-philosophy systems), `06-nutrients-organic.md` (top-dress amendments, teas, ferments), `08-pests.md` (biological IPM is integral, not add-on), `09-disease.md` (rhizosphere immunity and disease suppression), `13-harvest.md` and `15-curing.md` (terpene complexity is why many growers commit to living soil).

## How to Use This File

Diagnostic order in living soil is inverted from hydro — biology first, plant second:

1. **Smell first** — earthy/forest-floor = healthy; sour/swampy/anaerobic = problem
2. **Cover crop / surface biology** — visible mycelium, healthy cover plants, mulch holding integrity
3. **Substrate moisture** — should be evenly damp, never bone-dry, never soggy
4. **Plant signals second** — most "deficiencies" in living soil resolve themselves over 7–14 days as biology cycles nutrients
5. **Resist fast-fix instinct** — the slowest medium of any cannabis cultivation; impatience kills more living-soil grows than mistakes
6. **Cycle context** — cycle 1 problems may be normal; cycle 3 problems are real

## Core Concepts

| Concept | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Feed the soil, not the plant** | Amendments are biology food; biology mineralizes them into plant-available forms | Plant can't be force-fed; pace is the biology's pace |
| **No-till = preserved fungal networks** | Don't disturb the rhizosphere between cycles | Mycorrhizal hyphae and saprophytic fungi take months to establish; tilling resets the system |
| **Plant-soil-microbe trinity** | Roots, biology, and substrate are one functional unit (rhizosphere) | Treating any in isolation breaks the whole |
| **pH is biology-managed** | Healthy biology buffers pH around 6.3–6.8 automatically | Don't pH-adjust water; don't measure runoff with hydroponic urgency |
| **Recovery is months, not days** | A killed-off soil takes 3–6 months to rebuild biology | Don't reach for synthetic shortcuts when something looks off |
| **Cycle maturation** | Cycle 1 = building; cycle 2 = stabilizing; cycle 3+ = peak performance | Patience compounds — most growers quit before the system pays off |
| **Synthetic inputs kill the biology** | Even one round of synthetic feeds (or H₂O₂, or chlorinated water without dechlor) sterilizes parts of the system | Once committed to living soil, commit fully — no rescue feeds |
| **Quality, not yield, is the prize** | Living soil rarely outperforms hydro on g/W or g/m²; it tends to win on terpene complexity, smoke quality, sustainability | Pick living soil for outcome reasons, not for "better numbers" |

## What Is Living Soil

A **living soil** is a biologically active substrate hosting a complete soil food web: bacteria, fungi, protozoa, nematodes, microarthropods, and worms cycling nutrients and protecting roots. The mix is **engineered to feed and house this biology**, and amendments throughout the cycle keep the food web alive.

### Distinction from "Amended Organic Soil"
A bag of "organic" soil is not the same thing as living soil. The distinction:

| Amended-organic soil | Living soil |
|---|---|
| Used once, then composted out | Used cycle after cycle indefinitely |
| Tilled / disrupted / refilled between runs | Never tilled; topdressed and replanted |
| Biology dies between cycles (no cover, no input) | Biology persists year-round |
| Recipe-driven, predictable | System-driven, evolves over time |
| Forgiving but limited | Demanding but compounding |

Synthetic-fed soil (`04-medium-soil.md`) and even Subcool Super Soil sit in the "amended organic" category — they use organic amendments but treat the soil as a one-cycle resource. Living soil is permanent.

### The Soil Food Web (Elaine Ingham foundational framework)

| Trophic level | Members | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Primary producers | Plant roots, root exudates | Feed the system via root exudate sugars and acids |
| Decomposers | Bacteria, saprophytic fungi | Break down amendments and organic matter into plant-available forms |
| Protozoa | Flagellates, ciliates, amoebae | Eat bacteria, release N as ammonium → plants take up |
| Nematodes | Bacterial-feeders, fungal-feeders, predators | Distribute nutrients, suppress pathogenic nematodes |
| Microarthropods | Springtails, mites, isopods | Process organic matter, fragment material for further decomposition |
| Top predators | Predatory mites, beneficial nematodes, beetles | IPM via natural pressure on pest insects |

(Tier 1 — Elaine Ingham's *Soil Food Web* framework is the canonical reference. Tier 3 — Coots, Silva, Trump apply it specifically to cannabis.)

### Why It Works
Plants release **30–50% of their photosynthate as root exudates** — sugars and organic acids that feed the rhizosphere bacteria. The bacteria, in turn, mineralize amendments and protect the root from pathogens. Mycorrhizal fungi extend the effective root surface area 100–1000×, accessing P and trace minerals from beyond the depletion zone around the root. The plant trades sugar for nutrient access — a self-regulating chemical economy that hydro can't replicate.

## The Coots Mix (foundational living-soil recipe)

The Coots Mix, authored by **Clackamas Coot (Coot Real)** and adopted by Build-A-Soil, KIS Organics, and most modern living-soil practitioners, is the standard starting point.

### Base Recipe (per cubic foot of finished soil)

```
1/3 sphagnum peat moss (Canadian, OMRI-listed)
1/3 aeration: 3/8" pumice (preferred) OR rice hulls (sustainable alternative) OR perlite
1/3 high-quality vermicompost (worm castings + thermophilic compost blend)
```

### Amendment Package (per 1 cu ft)

| Amendment | Quantity | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Neem seed meal | 1/2 cup | N source + chitin (microbial food) + insect deterrent |
| Kelp meal | 1/2 cup | K + trace minerals + cytokinins (plant growth regulators) |
| Crustacean meal (crab / lobster / shrimp) | 1/2 cup | Ca + chitin (key fungal food → improves disease resistance) |
| Mineral Mix (4 cups total): | 4 cups | Trace minerals + slow Ca release |
| — 1 part glacial rock dust | | Trace minerals (60+) |
| — 1 part basalt rock dust | | Volcanic minerals + paramagnetism |
| — 1 part gypsum | | Ca + S without raising pH |
| — 1 part oyster shell flour or agricultural lime | | Slow Ca + alkaline buffer |

### Optional Add-Ins (cycle 1 boost or pheno-specific)
- **Malted barley powder** — diastatic enzymes (amylase, protease) jumpstart amendment breakdown
- **Karanja meal** — companion to neem (different active compound profile)
- **Insect frass** — chitin-rich, biology-active
- **Biochar** (pre-charged with EM-1 or compost extract) — long-term carbon sink + microbial habitat

### Cooking the Mix

After mixing on a tarp, water to "moist but not sopping" — wring-out-a-towel consistency. Cover loosely (allow gas exchange). **Cook 30–60 days minimum** in a non-freezing space.

Stir weekly. Smell test:
- **Sweet, earthy, forest-floor** = ready
- **Sour, ammonia, vinegar** = still cooking; add more aeration material; stir; wait
- **Foul, swampy** = anaerobic — too wet; spread out, dry partially, restart cooking

The cook period lets thermophilic and mesophilic biology break amendments down into plant-accessible forms. **Planting into uncooked soil = nute burn from raw amendments.**

## Korean Natural Farming (KNF)

A traditional Korean farming framework codified by **Master Cho Han-Kyu** (born 1935, Suwon, Korea; KNF formalized in the 1960s). KNF teaches the grower to **culture biology and inputs from local materials** rather than buy them. Core principle: *the inputs farmers need are already present in the local environment — they just need to be cultured and concentrated.*

### Core KNF Inputs

| Input | What it is | Stage |
|---|---|---|
| **IMO (Indigenous Microorganisms)** | Forest-floor microbes collected on cooked rice and propagated through 4 stages (IMO-1 through IMO-4); the foundational soil microbiology | Pre-mix; reapply between cycles |
| **FPJ (Fermented Plant Juice)** | Fast-growing tip cuttings (mugwort, dandelion, comfrey, cannabis fan leaves) fermented in equal-weight brown sugar 7–14 days; vegetative-growth booster | Veg, weekly |
| **FFJ (Fermented Fruit Juice)** | Ripe fruit (banana, apple, pear) fermented in equal-weight brown sugar 7–14 days; high K, flowering hormones | Flower, weekly |
| **LAB (Lactic Acid Bacteria)** | Rice rinse water + milk culture; outcompetes pathogenic bacteria, improves leaf size, deters whitefly/mite | Veg + early flower; foliar or drench |
| **OHN (Oriental Herb Nutrient)** | Garlic + ginger + licorice + cinnamon + angelica (×2 parts) extracted in rice wine then vinegar; antimicrobial + antifungal | Whole cycle, low-dose foliar/drench |
| **WCA (Water-Soluble Calcium)** | Eggshells (calcined) dissolved in brown rice vinegar (BRV); plant-available Ca | Stretch + mid-flower |
| **WCAP (Water-Soluble Calcium Phosphate)** | Animal bones (calcined) dissolved in BRV; Ca + P | Mid-flower |
| **MN (Marine / Sea Water dilution)** | Diluted seawater 1:30 with fresh water; trace minerals + osmotic stimulant | Single applications |

### KNF Schedule (Simplified for Cannabis)

| Stage | Inputs |
|---|---|
| Seedling / clone | IMO at potting; LAB foliar weekly |
| Veg | IMO drench monthly; FPJ weekly drench/foliar; OHN foliar weekly; LAB foliar |
| Stretch (flower wk 1–3) | Transition: continue FPJ taper; introduce WCA + WCAP |
| Mid flower | FFJ weekly; WCAP at week 4–5; OHN low-dose |
| Late flower | Reduce all inputs; OHN only if pest/mold pressure |
| Final 2 weeks | Plain water; biology cycles existing nutrients |

### Why KNF Practitioners Choose It
- Inputs are very cheap (kitchen scraps + rice + brown sugar + vinegar)
- Grower understands every input intimately (can't be done by reading nute schedule alone)
- Highly customizable to local climate/biology
- Deep tradition with extensive documentation
- Hash and rosin makers report KNF-grown cannabis presses better and tastes more complex (Tier 3 — community consensus among artisanal hash makers)

### Why KNF Is Hard
- Time commitment to make inputs (each ferment is 1–2 weeks)
- Inputs require skill to make correctly (failed ferments smell awful)
- Documentation for cannabis-specific application is fragmented
- Easy to over-apply (more is not better)

## JADAM (Cho Youngsang's Simpler Korean Framework)

Founded in 1991 by **Dr. Cho Youngsang**, eldest son of KNF founder Master Cho Han-Kyu. JADAM simplifies KNF: water-based fermentations instead of brown-sugar/alcohol/vinegar; fewer inputs; lower complexity. Goal is **ultra-low-cost regenerative agriculture** scalable to working farms.

### Core JADAM Inputs

| Input | What it is | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **JMS (JADAM Microorganism Solution)** | Leaf mold + boiled potato + sea salt fermented in chlorine-free water; foundational soil inoculant | Soil drench every 2 weeks |
| **JLF (JADAM Liquid Fertilizer)** | Plant biomass + water + JADAM nutrients fermented (no sugar) for 1–2 months; balanced organic NPK | Drench/foliar weekly |
| **JWA (JADAM Wetting Agent)** | Lye + soybean oil → potassium soap; saponin-rich surfactant for foliar applications and pesticide carrier | Mixed with JS or other foliars |
| **JS (JADAM Sulfur)** | Sulfur + lye + water (cold-process); fungicide for PM / botrytis | Foliar at first sign of fungal pressure |
| **JMP (JADAM Microbial Pesticide)** | Selectively cultured microbes targeting specific pests | Spot treatment |

### JADAM vs KNF Decision

| Choose KNF if | Choose JADAM if |
|---|---|
| You enjoy detailed fermentation work | You want lower complexity |
| You want maximum customization to your local environment | You want fewer inputs to manage |
| You're already comfortable with brown-sugar/alcohol/vinegar fermentations | Water-based fermentations only |
| Hash / rosin / connoisseur quality is the goal | Practical home-grow ecological resilience is enough |
| You have time to make 6–8 inputs across a cycle | You have time for 2–3 inputs |

Both work. Both can be combined — many growers use JMS as their base inoculant and KNF FPJ/FFJ as boosters.

## Container & Bed Approaches

### Fabric Pots (Home No-Till)

- **15–25 gal fabric pot** is the home no-till sweet spot
- 7–10 gal is workable; smaller doesn't hold biology well
- Single plant per pot
- Cover crop fills the surface
- Mulch layer 1–2" deep

Smaller pots dry out faster — biology suffers in repeated wet/dry oscillation.

### Raised Beds (Best Biology)

- 4'×4' minimum; 4'×8' or larger ideal
- Depth 18–24"
- Soil shared between plants → bigger biology buffer, better stability
- Plant 4–9 plants per 4×4 bed depending on training
- Cycle multiple plants in/out of the same bed indefinitely

Raised beds are the platonic ideal for living soil because volume = stability. Home growers without space use 25-gal+ fabric pots and treat them as mini-beds.

### Living-Soil Sleeve / Custom

Some growers build coffin-shaped fabric beds or trash-bag-lined containers; the principle is same — large volume, persistent biology, no-till.

## Initial Setup

```
Step 1: Mix Coots base + amendments on a tarp
Step 2: Water to moist consistency
Step 3: Cover loosely; cook 30–60 days
Step 4: Plant fully-rooted clones or healthy seedlings (NOT seeds — they don't compete with cover crop)
Step 5: Sow cover crop (clover + buckwheat + alfalfa mix) around plant base
Step 6: Apply 1–2" mulch (straw, wood chips, leaf litter)
Step 7: Inoculate with mycorrhizal fungi at root contact (Great White, Mykos, etc.)
Step 8: Initial drench with compost extract or JMS or IMO solution
Step 9: Plain water from this point unless biology needs feeding
```

## Ongoing Cycle Management

### Between Cycles (Don't Till)

After harvest:
1. **Cut cannabis stalk at soil line** — leave roots in place. Decomposing roots feed the next cycle's biology and preserve soil structure.
2. **Top-dress amendments** — light to medium re-amendment depending on cycle (cycle 2 is heavy; cycle 5 is light).
3. **Sow cover crop** — clover + buckwheat + alfalfa. Let it grow 2–4 weeks while you flip clones into veg.
4. **Add mulch / mulch refresh** — 1–2".
5. **Plant new clone directly into bed** — chop cover crop down to mulch level, plant clone in pocket cleared in cover.

### Light vs Heavy Re-Amendment

**Light re-amendment** (cycle 4+, mature soil): handful of vermicompost + dust of kelp/neem on surface; let cover crop and biology distribute.

**Heavy re-amendment** (cycle 1→2 transition, stressed soil): 1/4 cup each of neem/kelp/crustacean + 1 cup mineral mix per cu ft.

**Cycle 1 to 2 is the key transition** — biology has just established. Treat the soil generously here and lightly thereafter.

## Watering in Living Soil

### Default: Plain Dechlorinated Water

Living soil's biology handles nutrient cycling. Most days, plain water (RO, dechlorinated tap, or rainwater) is sufficient.

**Chlorine** (city tap water) kills biology over time. Either:
- Off-gas tap water 24–48 hr (chlorine evaporates; chloramine doesn't)
- Use a carbon filter on the hose
- Use rainwater or RO

### Compost Tea / Extract Applications

Apply weekly in cycle 1, monthly in mature cycles. See ACT section below.

### Molasses Drench

Unsulfured blackstrap molasses (5 ml per gallon) once every 2 weeks feeds bacteria specifically. Don't overdo it — too much sugar shifts the bacteria/fungi ratio toward bacteria.

### LITFA — "Leave It The F* Alone"
The most important living-soil principle: when in doubt, don't add anything. Most "deficiencies" in living soil resolve themselves over 7–14 days as biology cycles around them. Synthetic shortcuts kill the system.

### Substrate Moisture
Even moisture, never bone-dry, never soggy. Cover crop and mulch help maintain this. Lift weight is less reliable in living soil because biology adds variable weight; visual surface check + finger probe.

## Compost Teas (ACT — Aerated Compost Tea)

Brewed 24-hour aerated infusion of compost biology. The point is to **multiply biology, not extract nutrients**. (Tier 1 — Elaine Ingham foundational science.)

### Bacterial Tea (faster-acting, veg + early flower)

```
5 gallons dechlorinated water
1 cup vermicompost (in fine-mesh bag)
2 tbsp unsulfured molasses (bacteria food)
1 tsp kelp meal (trace minerals)
Aerate with strong air pump 24 hr at 18–22°C
Apply within 4 hours of brewing
```

### Fungal Tea (slower-acting, mid + late flower; mature soil maintenance)

```
5 gallons dechlorinated water
1 cup vermicompost + 1/2 cup quality compost with visible mycelium (in mesh bag)
1 tbsp humic acid
1 tbsp kelp meal
NO molasses (sugar feeds bacteria, suppresses fungi)
Pre-feed: tea bag in oat slurry overnight before aerating to wake fungi
Aerate 24–36 hr at 18–22°C
Apply within 4 hours
```

### Critical: Aerobic Only
Anaerobic teas produce **alcohols, phenols, and putrefying compounds** that are phytotoxic and can introduce pathogens. If your tea smells:
- **Earthy, sweet, slightly yeasty** → aerobic, healthy, apply
- **Sour, vomit, swampy, eggy** → anaerobic — DUMP, don't apply, don't second-guess

Air pump must run the entire brew. Stop the air mid-brew = anaerobic conversion within hours.

### Application
- Drench: pour around root zone
- Foliar: spray on leaf surfaces (early morning or late evening, NOT under direct light)
- Frequency: weekly cycle 1, every 2–4 weeks cycle 2+, monthly mature soil

## Biological IPM

Living soil naturally suppresses many pests because the ecosystem itself includes predators. Beneficial introductions reinforce this. (See `08-pests.md` for full IPM detail.)

| Beneficial | Targets | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Stratiolaelaps scimitus / Hypoaspis miles** | Fungus gnats, thrips pupae, root aphids | Soil-dwelling predator mite; release at substrate level; compatible with living-soil moisture |
| **Amblyseius cucumeris** | Thrips, broad mites | Sachets hung in canopy; release biweekly |
| **Persimilis / Phytoseiulus** | Spider mites | Aggressive predator; only use when mites confirmed |
| **Beneficial nematodes** (Steinernema feltiae, Heterorhabditis) | Fungus gnat larvae, root aphids, thrips pupae | Drenched into soil; reapply every 30 days; need cool moist soil |
| **Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt)** | Caterpillars (Btk strain) | Foliar; safe for biology |
| **BTI (Bt israelensis)** | Fungus gnat larvae | Soil drench (Mosquito Bits); compatible with living soil |
| **Neem oil cold-pressed** | Soft-body pests, foliar mildew prevention | Foliar; vegetative stage only; never on flowering buds |
| **JS (JADAM Sulfur)** | PM, botrytis | Foliar fungicide; cold-process sulfur is biology-compatible |

**What NOT to use in living soil:**
- Synthetic pesticides (kill predators + soil biology)
- Pyrethrins (kill beneficials)
- H₂O₂ drenches (sterilize the soil)
- Most "systemic" insecticides

## Cover Crops

Cover crops continuously planted around the cannabis maintain soil health by:
- Adding root exudates → feed biology
- Mulch when chopped → carbon return
- Nitrogen fixation (legumes — clover, vetch, alfalfa)
- Allelopathic suppression of weed and pest pressure
- Continuous canopy keeps soil shaded and moist

### Common Cover Crop Mix

| Plant | Role |
|---|---|
| **White Dutch clover** | N-fixation, low-growing, perennial in beds |
| **Crimson clover** | N-fixation, taller, annual |
| **Buckwheat** | Fast-growing, good chop-and-drop biomass, attracts beneficials |
| **Alfalfa** | N-fixation, deep tap root, biomass |
| **Vetch** | N-fixation, climbing |
| **Hairy vetch / fava beans** | Heavy N-fix for cycle 1 |
| **Oats / rye** | Carbon biomass, mycelium habitat |
| **Mustard** | Biofumigant — chop into soil to suppress nematode pests (used carefully) |

### Chop and Drop
When cover crops get tall enough to crowd cannabis, chop them at the base, leave biomass on the soil surface as mulch. Roots stay in place to decompose. New cover crop sprouts continue.

## Living Soil-Specific Issues

### Slow-Correction Problem
Living soil takes weeks to correct nutrient imbalances. Trying to fast-fix with synthetic = system disruption. Solution: prevent imbalances via balanced amendments and let biology buffer.

### Compaction Over Time
Beds and pots compact under repeated watering pressure. Solutions: cover crop roots aerate naturally; topdress fresh aeration material; introduce earthworms (red wigglers, *Eisenia fetida*) — they tunnel and aerate continuously.

### Fungus Gnats in Living Soil
Living soil's persistent moisture is fungus gnat heaven. Mitigation:
- Stratiolaelaps mites at substrate level (the gold standard)
- BTI in mosquito bits — drench monthly
- Sticky traps to monitor adult populations
- Top-dress with sand or fine pumice (gnats can't burrow through)

Gnats are usually present at low levels in living soil — control population, don't try to eliminate.

### Beneficial Surface Mold / Mycelium
White fluffy growth on soil surface = beneficial saprophytic fungi. **Leave it alone.** It's the system working. Confused growers spray fungicide and crash the biology.

Distinguish from:
- **Powdery mildew on leaves** → real problem (see `09-disease.md`)
- **Slimy black surface growth** → anaerobic; lift mulch, increase airflow

### Salt Buildup
Rare in true living soil (no salt-form fertilizers). If salt creep happens, it usually means:
- Hard tap water with high TDS being applied long-term
- Heavy molasses applications
- Synthetic input mistakenly introduced

Fix: switch to RO, reduce molasses, leach gently with rainwater if severe.

### Biology Going Dormant in Cool Rooms
Below 18°C, soil biology activity slows; plants don't get the same nutrient cycling. If running cool rooms (cellar, winter veg), expect slower growth and less responsiveness. Heat the soil if possible (substrate mat); accept the pace if not.

## Confusion / Misdiagnosis Traps

| Looks like | Actually | How to tell |
|---|---|---|
| White mold spreading on soil → contamination | Beneficial saprophytic fungi cycling amendments | Smell earthy/forest = good; smell sour/swampy = bad |
| Nutrient deficiency in living soil cycle 1 | Biology not yet established; system warming up | Wait 7–14 days, adjust ONLY if no improvement |
| "I should add Cal-Mg / pH down for safety" | Both kill biology; living soil self-buffers | Trust the system; intervene only with biology-compatible inputs |
| Cover crop competing with cannabis | Healthy ecosystem dynamic — helps more than it hurts | Chop cover crop only if it's shading cannabis |
| Slow growth = bad genetics | Living soil grows slower than hydro/coco; trade-off for quality | Compare cycle 3 living soil to cycle 1 — faster every cycle |
| Yield lower than hydro | Confirmed; living soil rarely beats hydro on yield | If yield is the goal, hydro is the medium; if quality, stay |
| Fungus gnats are an emergency | Low-level gnat presence is normal in living soil | Maintain Stratiolaelaps + BTI; control population, don't eliminate |
| Soil "smells like dirt" — am I doing this wrong? | That's exactly what healthy living soil smells like | Confused beginners overcorrect chasing odorless soil |
| KNF inputs not changing anything visible | Slow inputs work over weeks-to-months | Measure end-of-cycle outcomes (terps, smoke quality), not daily growth |
| ACT turned my plants pale | Foliar applied under direct light + temperature shock | Apply ACT early morning or evening only |
| Earthworms in pot are bad | Earthworms = pinnacle of soil ecosystem health | Welcome them; supplement with red wigglers if you find none |

## Quick Reference

### Coots Mix Recipe (1 cu ft batch)

```
Base:
  ~1/3 cu ft sphagnum peat
  ~1/3 cu ft pumice 3/8" (or rice hulls)
  ~1/3 cu ft vermicompost

Per 1 cu ft, add:
  1/2 cup neem seed meal
  1/2 cup kelp meal
  1/2 cup crustacean meal
  4 cups Mineral Mix (1:1:1:1 glacial / basalt / gypsum / oyster shell flour)

Cook 30–60 days, moist, covered, stirred weekly.
```
