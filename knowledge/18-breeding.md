# 18 — Breeding

How cannabis genetics actually behave across generations, how to pheno-hunt a pack, how pollen and reversal work, and how to stabilize new lines without falling for the "S1 stabilization" myth. Covers regular seed, feminized, autoflower, and backcross programs.

This file is educational and cultivation-focused for legal home growers and small personal breeding projects. It does not provide legal, commercial distribution, or seed-sales advice. ## How to Use This File

1. Identify your goal (keeper hunt, feminized seed run, IBL stabilization, backcross, hybrid)
2. Read core concepts — most breeding mistakes come from misunderstanding F1/F2 vs IBL
3. Match your goal to the protocol section below
4. Apply selection criteria from the rubric — be selective and cull weak, diseased, intersex-prone, or off-goal plants
5. Document everything; lineage you can't trace is worthless to anyone else

## Core Concept — Genotype vs Phenotype

- **Genotype** — the underlying genetic code; fixed at fertilization
- **Phenotype** — what the plant expresses physically given that genotype + environment
- Two clones (identical genotype) in different rooms = different phenotypes
- Two seeds from the same parents = different genotypes = different phenotypes even in same room

Breeders select on phenotype but inherit on genotype. The key question every breeder asks: *is this trait genetic, environmental, or epigenetic?* Run the same plant in different conditions before declaring a trait "the pheno's."

## Core Concept — Polygenic Traits and Hidden Recessives

Most cannabis traits worth selecting for are **polygenic** — controlled by many genes acting together:
- Yield, terpene profile, bud density, flowering time, height, branching habit — all polygenic
- THC/CBD ratio is closer to single-locus (B locus, T allele vs C allele) but still has modifiers
- Sex is XX/XY but expression overrides via stress/chemistry are common (hermaphrodites, intersex). Autosomal modifier genes influence intersex tendency — this is why some lines herm under stress and others don't even when stressed identically.

Polygenic traits do NOT segregate cleanly in F2. You won't see neat 3:1 Mendelian ratios. You'll see a **continuum** with the trait emerging in some, disappearing in others.

**Recessive traits hide.** A flavor or color you love may be recessive — it shows in P1, hides in F1, reappears in 1/4 of F2. If you breed only F1 forward you may permanently lose the trait without realizing.

## BREEDING TERMINOLOGY

Memorize these — most online "strain breeders" misuse them, and the difference between an IBL and an F1 marketed as one is the difference between predictable seeds and pheno-hunt chaos.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **P1** | True parent — usually a stabilized IBL or landrace |
| **F1** | First filial — cross of two parents. Uniformity and heterosis are strongest when parents are stable and genetically distinct |
| **F2** | F1 × F1 sibling cross. Genetically chaotic — recombination scatters traits. Where pheno-hunting "real" hybrid finds happen |
| **F3+** | Continued sibling crosses, drifting toward homozygous. Stabilizes traits over generations |
| **IBL** | Inbred Line — stabilized over many F generations (typically F5+), produces predictable phenos |
| **BX (Backcross)** | F1 × original P1 — used to recover or amplify a specific parent trait |
| **BX1, BX2, BX3** | Number of backcrosses; each one doubles the contribution of the recurrent parent |
| **Cubing** | Repeated backcrossing (BX3+) to "cube" toward the recurrent parent |
| **S1** | Self-pollinated offspring of one plant via reversal — homozygous at all loci where mother was homozygous |
| **S2, S3** | S1 selfed again, then again. Increases homozygosity but only of mother's genome |
| **Polyhybrid** | Cross of two hybrids (e.g. F1 × F1 of different lineages) — chaos, no prediction possible |
| **Landrace** | Geographically associated, locally adapted population; often diverse within the population, not automatically uniform or pure |
| **Heirloom** | Old cultivar maintained through generations |
| **Clone-only** | Genetics existing only as cuttings, never reliably reproduced from seed |

## Core Concept — Hybrid Vigor (Heterosis) and Why F1 Dominates

When two stable, genetically distinct lines cross, the F1 may outperform the parents in vigor, yield, or uniformity. This is **heterosis**. It is common, but not guaranteed for every trait or cross.

Why F1s dominate the seed market:
- Predictable for the buyer (uniform if parents are true IBLs)
- Vigor sells
- F1 seed lots are reproducible only by repeating the original parent cross; saving F2 seed will segregate and vary

Why F2 chaos is real:
- F1 is heterozygous at most loci where parents differed
- F1 × F1 (= F2) reshuffles those heterozygous alleles
- At each segregating locus: ~25% AA, ~50% Aa, ~25% aa (classic 1:2:1 Mendelian ratio).
- Multiplied across hundreds of loci → wide phenotypic variation
- F2 is where the **hidden recessives** show up

## The "S1 Stabilization" Myth

A common breeder claim: "I S1'd the keeper to stabilize it." Truth:

- S1 fixes loci where the mother was already homozygous
- At loci where mother was heterozygous (Aa), S1 produces 1AA : 2Aa : 1aa — *more* variation, not less, at those loci
- Hidden recessives masked in mother appear in S1 progeny
- S1 of a clone-only F1 hybrid is NOT an IBL of that hybrid — it's a feminized seed line with significant variation
- Stabilization requires multiple generations of **selection + sibling crossing**, not one selfing event

S1 has a legitimate use: feminized seeds of a single mother for those without male access. Just don't call it stabilization.

## PHENO-HUNT PROTOCOL

The systematic search for a keeper plant from a pack of seeds.

### Step 1 — Pop the right pack size

- Goal: keeper for personal use → 5-10 regular seeds (or 5-10 fems)
- Goal: keeper for breeding → 20-50+ regs, ideally with both sexes for breeding stock
- Goal: serious line evaluation → larger populations improve selection confidence if legal plant-count limits allow
- Why regular over fem for breeding: regular seeds let you evaluate both male and female contributors; feminized lines can still be useful when used intentionally

### Step 2 — Uniform veg conditions

- All plants in identical containers, medium, light, feed
- Veg for 4-6 weeks to express full structure
- Label every plant: pack code + plant number
- Train minimally — avoid heavy topping during hunt; let natural structure show

### Step 3 — Take cuttings BEFORE flip

**Critical:** take 2-3 cuttings of every plant before flowering. Lose them and you lose your future winner forever (and breeders who skip this step lose phenos every season).

- Label cuttings with parent plant ID
- Root and hold labeled backups in a clean veg/mother space
- Don't flower out un-cloned plants if you might want them later

### Step 4 — Flower under uniform conditions

- Same light, same feed, same environment
- Flower full term — DO NOT cut early; that's where the best terps and structure show
- Photo plants at: week 2, 4, 6, 8, harvest
- Note flowering time per plant (varies within a pack by 1-3 weeks normally)

### Step 5 — Score on rubric

Build a rubric weighted to your goal. Example weighting for production keeper:

| Trait | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Yield | 25% | g/plant, dry weight |
| Bag appeal | 15% | density, calyx-to-leaf, color |
| Smell at break | 20% | terpene loudness, character |
| Flowering time | 10% | shorter = more cycles/year |
| Stretch / structure | 10% | manageable in your space |
| Mold / herm resistance | 10% | observed late-flower |
| Cured aroma/flavor | 10% | Evaluate consistently after cure where legal/appropriate |

Different weighting applies for personal keepers, breeding stock, and outdoor/humid-climate selections.

### Step 6 — Evaluate cured flower consistently

- Cure long enough for a fair comparison before final score
- If legal and appropriate, evaluate aroma/flavor consistently; avoid medical claims and do not drive or work impaired
- The pheno that smelled best wet may not be best dry

### Step 7 — Keep 1-2, cull rest

Be selective. Most pheno hunts lose focus by hoarding every interesting plant. Keep only plants that clearly match the goal and are healthy, stable, and worth preserving.

### Step 8 — Verify with second run

A "winner" run once may be lucky. Run the keeper alone the next cycle to verify yield, terps, and timing match. Test for HLVd before declaring a true keeper.

## POLLINATION & SEED PRODUCTION

### Identifying males early

Male preflowers appear 1-2 weeks before female pistils. Look at the node where branch meets stem:

- **Male preflower:** small football/spade-shaped sac, no white hair
- **Female preflower:** small pointed calyx with one or two white hairs (pistils) emerging
- **Hermaphrodite:** female plant developing male flowers (sacs) somewhere on the plant; remove or cull depending on use

Selecting males is harder than females — the trait you smell in females is silent in males. Selection criteria for breeding males:

- Vigor and stem strength
- Branching habit, internode spacing
- Stem rub: rub stem at node, smell — males have terpenes too, often louder
- Pre-flower density and timing
- Resistance traits (skip stressed, sickly males)
- Pollen production volume and timing

### Pollen collection

- Quarantine the male in a separate room with no airflow to females (pollen travels)
- Collect when sacs split: tap branch over wax paper, parchment, or glass plate
- Sift through coffee filter or 75µ mesh to remove debris
- Store in airtight container with desiccant pack
- **Viability:**
  - Room temp: 1-3 weeks
  - Refrigerated, dry: 2-3 months
  - Frozen, dry, airtight: 12+ months (some viability past 3 years reported)

### Pollination methods

**Whole-plant pollination:**
- Apply dry pollen with a brush or controlled dusting; water is used after pollination to deactivate stray pollen, not as the main carrier
- Whole plant goes seedy
- Highest seed yield, lowest selection control

**Single-branch (most common):**
- Bag one branch with paper bag (not plastic — needs airflow)
- Dust pollen into bag with brush
- Tie bag at base for 24-48h
- Lightly mist the bag exterior before removing to reduce stray pollen drift
- Rest of plant produces sinsemilla; isolated branch produces seeds

**Single-flower:**
- Paint specific calyx with brush
- Allows multiple crosses on one plant (label each branch)
- Risk of cross-contamination if pollen drifts

### Timing

- Pollinate weeks 2-4 of flower for best seed development time
- Seeds need 4-6 weeks post-pollination to mature
- Mature seeds: dark, tiger-striped, hard, some weight; immature seeds: pale, soft, lightweight
- Dry pollinated branches normally; let seeds fully ripen on plant if possible

### Seed handling

- Dry seeds 1-2 weeks at 60% RH after harvest
- Store airtight with desiccant in fridge or freezer
- Properly stored seeds remain viable 5-10+ years
- Label: parents, cross date, parent IDs, generation

## REVERSAL (Feminization)

Producing pollen from a genetically female plant by inducing male flowers. The resulting pollen lacks a Y chromosome, so crosses to a female plant produce mostly/all XX seeds when the parent stock is stable.

### Method 1 — STS (Silver Thiosulfate)

- Common and reliable for experienced breeders
- Involves silver salts and should be handled with PPE, labels/SDS, dedicated equipment, and good ventilation
- Use a vetted horticultural protocol rather than improvising concentration or timing
- Treat sprayed plants as non-consumable and keep them isolated from the main grow

### Method 2 — Colloidal Silver (CS)

- Easier to source but less reliable than STS
- Still a silver treatment: sprayed material is non-consumable, and runoff/overspray should be handled responsibly
- Use only on isolated target branches/plants with a vetted protocol

### Method 3 — Gibberellic Acid (GA)

- Older, less common method
- Less reliable for cannabis specifically
- Largely replaced by STS

### Reversal cautions

- Reversed plant pollen + reversed plant flower = double-reversed seeds = high herm rate
- Stable feminized line: reversed female × non-reversed female (preferred)
- Reversed plants stress more easily — keep apart from the main grow
- Silver-treated material is non-consumable — do not smoke, ingest, wash into flower, or compost into food/cannabis beds
- S1 = take one female, reverse a clone, pollinate the original or a sister clone

## STABILIZATION (Building an IBL)

Goal: produce a line where seeds reliably express the parent's traits.

### How long it takes

- F2: phenotypic chaos
- F3-F4: first traits start fixing if selecting hard
- F5: most lines considered "working IBLs" by breeders
- F7-F8: widely homozygous, predictable seeds
- 4-6 years minimum at one cycle/year (faster if running multiple cycles indoors)

### Process per generation

1. Grow large F-population (50-200 plants minimum)
2. Select for the target trait — both males and females
3. Cross selected siblings
4. Repeat next generation

### Selection pressure

- High pressure (keep top 1-5%) = faster fixing but bottlenecks gene pool
- Lower pressure (keep top 10-20%) = slower fixing but maintains vigor
- Best practice: select on multiple criteria, not just one

### Inbreeding depression

- Multiple generations of sibling crossing reduces vigor
- Symptoms: slower growth, reduced yield, weaker disease resistance
- Counter by occasional outcross to unrelated line carrying same goal trait
- Some landraces are stable IBLs from natural geographic isolation — high vigor proves stabilization is possible without depression if done right

### Backcrossing as alternative

If starting from a clone-only keeper, true F-stabilization is impossible (no males). Workaround:

1. Reverse the keeper or a selected related female to make feminized pollen
2. Grow S1 or related progeny and select the best stable female individuals
3. Reverse a selected female and pollinate the original keeper or a sister clone → fem BX-style generation
4. Repeat selection and reversal across generations if the goal is a seed line resembling the keeper
5. Expect variation; this is an approximation, not a true clone replacement

This produces a feminized self/backcross-style line, not a true IBL and not an exact clone replacement.

## AUTOFLOWER BREEDING

### What auto means genetically

- Day-neutral flowering trigger — flowers based on age, not photoperiod
- Trait is recessive at one major locus (Auto1) with polygenic modifiers affecting flowering speed and final size.
- Auto × photo F1 = all photoperiod (auto trait hidden)
- F2 segregates: ~25% auto, ~75% photo (some photos with shifted flowering time from modifier genes — there is no true "semi-auto" in heterozygotes since auto is recessive).

### Auto-only breeding

- Cross two autos = all auto offspring
- Selection within auto pop: earliest, biggest, most cannabis-looking, terps
- Yield improvement: stack auto x auto across generations
- Modern autos approach photo yields and potency; gap closing yearly

### Auto × photo to "de-auto" (if you want to introduce photo trait)

- F1 = all photo
- F2 = ~25% auto, hunt within autos
- Repeat to combine photo trait + auto behavior
- Multi-year process

### Reversal in autos

- STS works on autos — produces feminized auto seeds
- Timing tighter — auto flowers fast, narrow reversal window
- Most modern auto seedbanks sell fem autos via this method

## POLYPLOIDY (Briefly)

Normal cannabis is **diploid** (2n). Polyploid cannabis (3n triploid, 4n tetraploid) is a niche area:

- Induced in research/specialist settings via mitotic inhibitors such as colchicine or oryzalin
- **Triploid (3n):** sterile, claimed larger trichomes, debated in literature; companies marketing as premium clones
- **Tetraploid (4n):** fertile, larger overall plant, possible breeding stock for triploid production (4n × 2n = 3n)
- Industry hype around triploid claims (yield, potency, bigger trichomes) is partially research-backed, partially marketing
- Colchicine and similar mitotic inhibitors are hazardous; do not recommend home polyploid induction protocols

Tier 1 work on cannabis polyploidy is emerging but limited — Parsons, J. L. et al. (2019) — *Frontiers in Plant Science* — colchicine-induced polyploidy in Cannabis; Mansouri & Bagheri (2017) — chromosome doubling protocols; Hesami et al. review covers polyploid biotech context. Most market claims outpace published data.

## HLVd AND BREEDING (Critical)

Hop Latent Viroid is a major clean-stock issue for clone-based breeding material.

- HLVd transmission through infected propagation/breeding material has been reported; pollen/seed risk should be treated conservatively
- Infected breeding stock can compromise a line
- RT-qPCR or validated lab testing is the standard way to confirm status
- Symptoms in breeding plants may be subtle — slow, low-yield, "dudding"
- Some clone-only genetics may carry HLVd silently — recovery, when worthwhile, requires specialist clean-stock work (see `19-tissue-culture.md`)

**Breeding hygiene:**
- Test high-risk or valuable breeding stock for HLVd before making seed
- Do not breed from confirmed HLVd-positive plants
- Prefer fresh blades or validated disinfection/contact time between plants
- Quarantine new genetics before breeding with them
- Keep records of test dates and results per plant

## SELECTION CRITERIA RUBRIC

Adjust weights by goal. Score each plant 1-10 per criterion.

| Criterion | What to look for | Weight examples |
|---|---|---|
| Yield (g/plant, dry) | Final dry weight in your setup | Goal-dependent |
| Flowering time | Days from flip to harvest | High for short-season |
| Bud density | Compaction, calyx-to-leaf ratio | High for bag appeal |
| Trichome coverage | Frosting, foxtailing pattern | High for hash/rosin |
| Smell at break | Terpene loudness when bud is broken | High for connoisseur |
| Cure-up aroma/flavor | Day-30+ sensory notes where legal/appropriate | Goal-dependent |
| Cannabinoid % (if tested) | THC, CBD, CBG, ratios | Goal-dependent |
| Stretch ratio | Flower height vs veg height | High for limited space |
| Internode spacing | Tight = production; long = sativa-style | Goal-dependent |
| Branching habit | Bushy vs columnar | High for SCROG/SOG |
| Stem strength | Self-supports buds without trellising | Always positive |
| Mold resistance | Observed late-flower under stress | High for outdoor/humid |
| Heat tolerance | Performance under stress | High for hot climates |
| Herm tendency | Any preflower or late-flower nanners | Always negative |
| Pest resistance | Visual/observed under typical pressure | Always positive |
| HLVd status | Tested negative | Required for breeding |

## DECISION TREE — WHICH BREEDING APPROACH?

```
GOAL?

→ Find a personal keeper from a pack
   └── PHENO-HUNT 5-10 fems (or 10-20 regs to also screen males)

→ Make feminized seeds of a clone-only winner
   └── S1 via STS reversal (acknowledge this is fem seeds, not IBL)

→ Cross two winners I have access to
   ├── Both clone-only fems → reverse one → cross to other
   ├── One has reg seeds with males → use natural male × winner clone
   └── Result is F1 hybrid; expect uniform if parents are stable, chaotic otherwise

→ Stabilize a new line over years (build IBL)
   └── F1 → F2 → F3 → ... select hard each generation, 5+ generations min

→ Recover/amplify a specific parent trait
   └── BACKCROSS — F1 × P1, repeat 2-3 times (BX2 / BX3)

→ Make a seed-reproducible version of a clone-only keeper
   └── Fem self/backcross-style program: reverse selected females across generations; expect variation

→ Breed autoflowers
   ├── Within autos → straightforward, select earliest/biggest
   └── Add photo traits → auto × photo → F2 hunt for autos with new traits

→ Heirloom preservation
   └── Open-pollinated grow with broad selection (10%+ kept) to preserve gene pool diversity
```

## COMMON BREEDING MISCONCEPTIONS

| Belief | Reality |
|---|---|
| "I S1'd it so it's stable now" | S1 stabilizes only loci where mother was homozygous; introduces variation at heterozygous loci |
| "F1 hybrid vigor means F1 is the best generation" | F1 is uniform and vigorous; F2 is where new combinations appear and where hidden traits emerge |
| "Polyhybrids are just as stable as IBLs" | False. Polyhybrid = chaos. Marketing language. |
| "I picked the best mother, so the seeds will match" | You also need to pick the best father. Father contributes 50% of every seed. |
| "Reversed plants are unstable forever" | The reversed plant is stressed; its offspring are not necessarily unstable IF reversed × non-reversed cross. Reversed × reversed = high herm rate. |
| "Clone-only means it can't be reproduced from seed" | It can sometimes be approximated with self/backcross-style breeding, but seed offspring are not exact clones. |
| "All landraces are pure / IBL" | Most are heterogeneous within a region but stable as a population. Individual plants vary. |
| "If I see a herm I should kill that plant only" | A herm pheno may be triggered or genetic; check siblings; if genetic, line is suspect |
| "Fem seeds always herm" | Modern fem seeds from STS-reversed × non-reversed mothers are stable. Only S1 of stress-prone genetics herm regularly. |
| "Auto x photo gives autoflowers" | F1 is all photo. Autos appear in F2 (~25%). |
| "Bigger pack = better odds of keeper" | True, but with diminishing returns past ~50 unless line is very heterogeneous |

## RECORDKEEPING (Essential)

A breeding program with poor records produces seeds nobody can verify or trust. Minimum recordkeeping per cross:

```
Cross ID: [unique code, e.g. 2026-001]
Date pollinated: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Mother: [strain name + plant ID + clone source + HLVd status]
Father: [strain name + plant ID + lineage + HLVd status]
Pollen batch: [batch ID + date collected + storage]
Method: [whole plant / branch / single flower]
Generation: [F1 / F2 / BX1 / S1 / etc.]
Goal: [what trait combination targeted]
Mother's pheno notes: [structure, terps, yield]
Father's pheno notes: [structure, vigor, smell, branching]
Expected harvest of seeds: [date]
Notes: [anything unusual]
```

For a pheno hunt, score every plant on the rubric, photograph at standard intervals, and save consistent cured-flower notes where legal/appropriate.

For any public or shared breeding work, traceability matters: parent IDs, dates, generation, and health status prevent confusion and accidental misrepresentation.
