# 08 — Pests

Pests are a common cause of crop loss after watering, pH, nutrition, and environment problems. Identify before treating — wrong-target sprays can damage plants, leave residues, harm beneficials, and feed resistance. Cross-reference with `07-deficiencies.md` (pest damage often mimics deficiency), `09-disease.md` (pest injury can open entry points for mold and bacteria), `02-environment.md` (environment affects pest pressure), `04-medium-living-soil.md` (soil biology can suppress some soil pests), `06-nutrients-organic.md` (soil-amendment IPM boundaries), and `workflows/diagnose-from-photo.md` (leaf-underside and microscope photos).

## How to Use This File

1. **Inspect under leaves first** — most pests live abaxial (leaf underside). A whole-plant photo from above misses the entire scouting target.
2. **Use magnification** — jeweler's loupe (30×) for spider mites and aphids, USB microscope (60–100×) for russet/broad mites. Naked-eye diagnosis fails for the most damaging species.
3. **Identify before treating** — wrong-target sprays cost time, money, and flower residue. Get an ID under magnification.
4. **Check life stage** — many controls hit only one stage. Eggs and pupae often survive single applications; follow the product label and pest lifecycle when planning repeat treatments.
5. **Default to soft-and-biological** — start with cultural and biological controls. Escalate only when those plateau.
6. **Avoid foliar sprays once flowers are forming** unless the product label explicitly allows it for edible/consumable crops and the risk is lower than crop loss. Prefer prevention, hand removal, biologicals, and environmental correction in flower.

## Critical Concept — The IPM Hierarchy

The five-tier ladder. Start at prevention/monitoring, escalate only when identification and severity justify it.

| Tier | Action | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Prevent** | Stop pests entering | Quarantine clones (14–21 days), screened intakes, clean tools, inspect incoming plants under magnification, avoid unnecessary plant sharing, consider beneficial nematodes/predatory mites when appropriate |
| **2. Monitor** | Detect early before population explodes | Yellow + blue sticky traps, weekly leaf-flip inspections, microscope checks of new growth, scouting routes |
| **3. Identify** | Pest, life stage, severity, location | Microscope ID, photo to extension service, peer ID forums |
| **4. Spot-treat** | Smallest effective intervention | Alcohol on q-tip for scale, hand-pick caterpillars, foliar wash on isolated branch, predator sachet on hot zone |
| **5. Reset** | Full corrective protocol | Defoliate, repeat treatments at 5/10/15-day intervals, biological + botanical stack, quarantine, in worst case harvest early |

**Escalation rule:** if prevention and monitoring are consistent, severe outbreaks should become rare. If every cycle needs rescue treatments, the setup has an entry, sanitation, environment, or quarantine problem to fix.

## Critical Concept — Above-Soil vs In-Soil

The organizing framework — parallel to mobile/immobile in `07-deficiencies.md`. Where the pest lives dictates how to find it and what kills it.

| Location | Pests | Detection method |
|---|---|---|
| **Foliage / canopy** | Spider mites, hemp russet mite, broad mite, thrips, aphids, whiteflies, caterpillars, leafhoppers | Underside leaf inspection; sticky traps for adults; magnification |
| **Stems / branches** | Mealybugs, scale insects, hemp borer (caterpillar), stink bugs (outdoor) | Visual stem inspection; white waxy clusters or hard bumps |
| **Soil surface** | Fungus gnat adults, springtails (mostly beneficial), shore flies, slugs (outdoor) | Yellow sticky traps near pot rims; visible adults flying |
| **In-soil / root zone** | Fungus gnat larvae, root aphids, root knot nematodes (outdoor mostly), soil mites | Lift plant inspect roots; root-zone slime/discoloration; sudden wilt of healthy plant |

## Critical Concept — Lifecycle Awareness

Most pests have egg → larva (or nymph) → adult stages. Most products hit ONE stage — usually adults. Eggs survive sprays; new adults emerge in 3–10 days; population recovers and you've trained resistance.

| Pest | Egg-to-adult time @75°F | Treatment cadence |
|---|---|---|
| Spider mite (T. urticae) | 7–14 days | Re-treat at 5, 10, 15 days |
| Hemp russet mite | 7–10 days | Re-treat at 5, 10 days |
| Broad mite | 4–7 days (very fast) | Re-treat at 3, 6, 10 days |
| Thrips | 14–20 days | Re-treat at 5, 10, 15 days |
| Fungus gnats | 17–28 days (adult emerges from soil) | Soil drench every 7–10 days × 3–4 cycles |
| Aphids | 7–10 days | Re-treat at 5, 10 days |
| Whitefly | 18–28 days | Re-treat at 5, 10, 15 days |
| Caterpillars | Continuous (eggs laid by moths) | Weekly during moth flight season |

**Diagnostic shortcut:** if a single spray "worked" but the problem returned in 7–10 days — that's a lifecycle issue, not a product issue. Re-treat on the cadence above.

## Critical Concept — Stage Safety

Cannabis flower is sticky and can retain residues from sprays. For public-safe home-grow guidance, treat flowers as a no-spray zone unless a product label and local rules clearly support use. When unsure, do not spray consumable flower.

| Stage | Foliar safety | Soil drench safety | Acceptable treatments |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Veg** | Many labeled soft options are possible | Medium treatments possible if label-compatible | Quarantine, pruning, water spray, insecticidal soap/oil products, predators, BT/BTI, nematodes; avoid restricted pesticides |
| **Stretch (week 1–2 flower)** | Cautious — pistils forming | Label-compatible only | Predators, hand removal, BT for caterpillars if label-compatible; avoid oils/soaps on forming flowers |
| **Mid-flower (week 3–5)** | Avoid foliar products on buds | Conservative, label-compatible only | Predators, hand removal, BT for caterpillars if label-compatible, sanitation, environmental correction |
| **Late flower (week 6+)** | None on buds | Limited | Predators/hand removal; remove contaminated buds; harvest early only if otherwise safe; do not use freezing to make infested or moldy flower safe |
| **Last 14 days before harvest** | None | None unless label-safe and not contacting buds | Hand removal, remove affected material, accept losses, harvest only clean/safe material |

**Hard rules:**
- No sulfur + oil within 14 days of each other (causes leaf phytotoxicity)
- Do not recommend restricted, unapproved, or systemic pesticides for consumable cannabis flower
- Avoid broad-spectrum knockdown sprays after pre-flower; they can leave residues and kill beneficials
- BT products can be useful for caterpillars when label-compatible, but they must be ingested and are not a general pest spray

## PESTS — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Spider Mite (Tetranychus urticae) — the universal threat

**Identification:**
- 0.5 mm adults, two distinct dark spots on body (hence "two-spotted")
- Color: pale yellow to red-orange; eggs are translucent pearls on leaf undersides
- Webbing in late infestations — fine silk between leaves and along leaf veins
- Lives almost entirely on leaf underside; visible to naked eye but easier with 30× loupe

**Damage symptoms:**
- Early: tiny pinprick pale dots ("stippling") on leaf top — the cells the mites have drained
- Mid: stippling becomes confluent, leaves look bronzed/silvered, reduced photosynthesis
- Advanced: webbing visible, leaves yellow and drop, plant overall stressed; bud webbing in flower = severe

**Lifecycle:**
- Egg → larva → protonymph → deutonymph → adult (5 stages)
- 5–7 days egg-to-adult at 85°F; up to a month at 60°F (Tier 1 — UC IPM, Koppert)
- Each female lays 100+ eggs over 2–3 weeks
- Warm, dry conditions can allow rapid population growth within days

**Conditions favored:**
- Warm + dry + low airflow (ideal: 80°F, 40% RH)
- Plant stress (dehydration, K deficiency) attracts them
- Outdoor migration into indoor via clothes/clones is the main entry route

**Confusion risk:**
- Spider webs in tent corners (those are spiders, not mites — webbing in canopy is mite)
- Thrips silver streaking (thrips streak is linear; mite stippling is dot pattern)
- Heat stress bronzing (no mites on underside; mite damage shows mites)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: improve plant vigor, reduce heat/drought stress, prune heavily infested leaves, isolate hot spots, clean tools
- **Biological**: *Phytoseiulus persimilis* is a strong specialist predator for spider mites when conditions fit. Use supplier release rates; performance depends on RH, temperature, and prior sprays.
- **Botanical/soft products**: labeled neem/azadirachtin, rosemary-based products, insecticidal soap, or horticultural oil in veg only; test a small area first and avoid heat/light stress
- **Mineral/nutritional support**: potassium silicate may improve stress tolerance; sulfur products are veg/empty-room only, label-dependent, and not beginner-default
- **Soft contact products**: insecticidal soap or horticultural oil in veg only; follow label rates, ensure direct contact, and avoid combining with sulfur
- **Avoid**: abamectin, bifenthrin, spiromesifen, and similar restricted/residual products on consumable cannabis

**Stage-safe options:**
- Veg: full ladder
- Early flower: predators only safely; spot-spray with insecticidal soap if isolated
- Mid-flower+: predators only
- Research note: pest stress can alter secondary metabolites in controlled studies, but this is not a cultivation strategy; prevent and control mites because yield, plant health, and sanitation risks outweigh any speculative quality effect.

### Hemp Russet Mite (Aculops cannabicola) — cannabis-host-specific, microscope mandatory

**Identification:**
- 170–210 µm female, 160–165 µm male — about twice the width of a human hair
- Light brown to white tubular body, only 4 short legs at the head end (eriophyid mites have 4 legs, not 8)
- Invisible to naked eye and 30× loupe — needs 60–100× microscope
- Cannabis-specific — only known to establish on *Cannabis sativa* (Tier 1 — NC State Extension, USU Extension, BugGuide)

**Damage symptoms:**
- Bronzing/russetting on lower leaves first (works upward over time)
- Leaves brittle, smaller than normal, upward edge curl
- "Fine golden-brown dust" on leaf surfaces — sign of large population
- New growth distortion + reduced internodes when severe
- Buds turn pale/dusty, lose density, can outright die in flower

**Lifecycle:**
- Egg → larva → nymph → adult, 7–10 days at warm room temp
- All stages on plant — no soil dormancy
- Females overwinter in protected plant tissue

**Conditions favored:**
- Warm dry indoor / greenhouse (ideal: 75–85°F, 30–50% RH)
- Cannabis monoculture (no host alternation breaks population)
- Considered one of the most damaging indoor/greenhouse hemp pests in the US (Tier 1 — Cranshaw / CSU)

**Confusion risk:**
- Calcium deficiency (both: distorted, brittle new growth) — microscope confirms
- Light burn (light burn is symmetric across canopy top; russet mite spreads from infection site)
- Spider mite (russet has 4 legs not 8; russet is 1/4 the size; russet shows no webbing)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: aggressive defoliation of worst-hit, isolation, sanitize cycle-to-cycle
- **Biological**: *Amblyseius swirskii*, *A. cucumeris*, or *A. fallacis* at 2–10 mites/sq ft. Sachet form preferred for sustained release. Effectiveness limited at low RH (<50%)
- **Botanical**: neem + karanja stack at 1 ml/L each + 0.5 ml/L silica + soap emulsifier, every 5 days × 3 cycles in veg
- **Mineral**: sulfur products only when label-compatible, in veg/empty rooms, never near oils, and not as a beginner default
- **Biological-fungal**: *Hirsutella thompsonii* entomopathogenic fungus shows promise (Tier 1 — Plant Health Progress 2023)
- **Last resort**: cull heavily infested plants or restart clean; heat treatments are experimental and easy to execute badly

**Stage-safe options:**
- Veg: full ladder
- Flower: predators + early harvest if severe; russet mite damage in flower often unrecoverable

**Critical note:** the most-failed russet mite diagnosis is calling it a deficiency. If new growth is distorted/curled and you've ruled out Ca/B/HLVd, **you need a microscope**, not more nutrients.

### Broad Mite (Polyphagotarsonemus latus) — distorted new growth

**Identification:**
- About 1/3 the size of a spider mite — practically invisible without microscope
- Translucent oval body, slow-moving on leaf underside
- Eggs distinctive: pale, oval, with rows of white tufts (looks like white-spotted ovals under microscope)

**Damage symptoms:**
- Toxic saliva injected during feeding distorts cell function — damage appears on NEW growth even if pest population is low
- New leaves: twisted, blistered, shiny / "wet" looking, downward-curled edges
- Stunted shoot tips, "claw" appearance not unlike N tox but on new growth
- Bud damage in flower: pistils brown and die, flower stops developing
- One of the most-misdiagnosed pests — symptoms persist after pest is gone

**Lifecycle:**
- Very fast — 4–7 days egg-to-adult at warm temps
- Population can explode within a week
- Hitchhike on whitefly adults (phoresy) — clones from contaminated facility carry both

**Conditions favored:**
- Warm + humid (75–80°F, >70% RH)
- Often clone-introduced
- Plant stress + dense canopy

**Confusion risk:**
- Calcium deficiency (Ca: distortion only, no toxic-saliva "wet" look)
- N toxicity / clawing (Ntox: dark green + claw on EXISTING leaves; broad mite: NEW growth)
- HLVd (HLVd: stunting + leaf deformity across multiple plants from same mother)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: discard worst plants, sanitize, quarantine new clones 21 days
- **Biological**: *Amblyseius andersoni* or *A. swirskii* at 5–10/sq ft (Tier 2 — Koppert)
- **Botanical**: neem + karanja stack as for russet mite
- **Mineral**: label-compatible sulfur products in veg only, never near oils
- **Soft synthetic**: insecticidal soap, horticultural oil (veg only)
- **Avoid**: abamectin/spiromesifen products on consumable cannabis; cull or restart when infestation is severe and controls fail

### Thrips (Frankliniella occidentalis, Thrips tabaci, etc.)

**Identification:**
- 1–2 mm slender adults, yellow-tan to dark brown; fringed wings (the name "thrips" = singular and plural)
- Larvae: pale yellow, wingless
- Visible to naked eye; 30× loupe shows wings + form clearly
- Yellow + blue sticky traps catch adults — primary monitoring tool

**Damage symptoms:**
- Silver streaking on leaves (linear, follows leaf vein) — this is the diagnostic signature (Tier 1 — UC IPM)
- Black specks on streaks = thrips frass
- Stippling-like damage but linear, not dot-pattern
- Bud thrips: discolored / distorted pistils; concealed inside bud tissue
- Virus vector concern: thrips transmit Tomato Spotted Wilt Virus (rare in cannabis but documented)

**Lifecycle:**
- Egg (laid inside leaf tissue) → 2 larval stages → prepupa → pupa (in soil/medium) → adult
- 14–20 days egg-to-adult
- Pupal stage in soil/coco — surface-only treatment misses this stage

**Conditions favored:**
- Warm dry rooms
- Brought in on flowers, clones, or clothing
- Western flower thrips is the worst in greenhouses worldwide

**Confusion risk:**
- Spider mite stippling (mite: dot-pattern; thrips: linear streak)
- Mineral deficiency silvering (thrips: discrete streaks with black frass; deficiency: uniform pattern)
- Leafminer trails (leafminer: continuous wavy trail; thrips: short discrete streaks)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: yellow + blue sticky traps at canopy level; screen all intakes; quarantine new plants
- **Biological**: *Amblyseius cucumeris* on leaves (1 mite/sq ft preventive, 5/sq ft curative) + *Stratiolaelaps scimitus* in soil for pupal stage + *Steinernema feltiae* nematodes in soil drench. Triple-stack covers all life stages (Tier 2 — Koppert)
- **Botanical/biopesticide**: spinosad or pyrethrum only if legal/label-compatible and veg/early-stage; avoid broad-spectrum sprays in flower and around beneficials/pollinators
- **Soft synthetic**: insecticidal soap (contact only)
- **Late-flower note**: thigmotactic behavior — thrips hide deep inside flower clusters, sprays cannot reach. Predators or accept loss.

### Fungus Gnats (Bradysia spp.)

**Identification:**
- Adults: 2–5 mm small dark flies, looks like fruit fly silhouette but more delicate; weak fliers
- Larvae: translucent worms with black heads, 4–8 mm, in top inch of soil/coco
- Adults caught on yellow sticky traps near pot rims; larvae in soil require lifting medium to inspect

**Damage symptoms:**
- Adults are mostly nuisance/monitoring targets; larvae do most root damage
- LARVAE feed on roots and root hairs — slow growth, nutrient inefficiency, opens entry to root rot pathogens
- Seedlings most vulnerable — larvae can outright kill them
- In established plants: subtle stunting, sluggish nutrient response

**Lifecycle:**
- Egg (in moist soil surface) → 4 larval instars (in soil) → pupa (in soil) → adult emerges and flies
- 17–28 days total at 70–75°F
- Each female lays 100–200 eggs in moist soil
- Treatment must hit larvae — adult sprays are cosmetic

**Conditions favored:**
- Persistently wet topsoil strongly favors fungus gnats
- Organic-rich substrates with active decomposition
- Cool basement / unheated grow tent

**Confusion risk:**
- Shore flies (look similar but don't damage roots — different family entirely)
- Springtails (jumping not flying; soil-surface beneficial decomposers, leave alone)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: dry top inch between waterings; sand or DE top layer; bottom-water seedlings
- **Biological**: *Steinernema feltiae* nematodes can suppress larvae when applied to moist media at supplier rates; repeat applications may be needed
- **Biological**: *Stratiolaelaps scimitus* (predatory soil mite) — 25,000–50,000 per cubic meter; lives in top inch
- **Biological**: BTI (Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis) — Mosquito Dunks at 1/4 dunk per gallon, drench every 5–7 days × 3–4 weeks (Tier 2). Larva-specific.
- **Cultural-mechanical**: yellow sticky traps reduce adult breeding population
- **Avoid as default**: hydrogen peroxide drenches can damage roots and soil biology; use moisture management, BTI, nematodes, and predatory mites first
- **Avoid**: foliar sprays (adults are not the target)

### Aphids (Cannabis aphid Phorodon cannabis, Green peach aphid Myzus persicae)

**Identification:**
- 1–3 mm soft pear-shaped bodies; green, white, yellow, brown, or black depending on species
- Wingless OR winged; cornicles (two tube-like protrusions on rear) are diagnostic
- Cluster on stems, leaf undersides, new growth tips
- Honeydew (sticky residue) on leaves below cluster + sooty mold on honeydew

**Damage symptoms:**
- Direct feeding: yellowing, leaf curl, stunted new growth
- Honeydew + sooty mold blocks photosynthesis
- Virus vector for several plant viruses (cannabis-specific virus transmission still being studied)
- Ants farm aphids — ant trail = aphid colony likely

**Lifecycle:**
- Parthenogenetic in growing season (females clone themselves) — no males needed
- Live young (not eggs) in summer — population explodes
- 7–10 day generation time
- Sexual reproduction with eggs in cooler conditions

**Conditions favored:**
- Outdoor + greenhouse
- Soft, fast new growth (over-fed nitrogen)
- Mother plants kept long-term (gradual population accumulation)

**Confusion risk:**
- Whitefly (whitefly fly when disturbed; aphids stay put)
- Mealybug (mealybug white-waxy clustered, less mobile, no cornicles)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: knock off with strong water spray (works — most don't make it back); sanitize ant trails; quarantine clones
- **Biological**: *Aphidius colemani* parasitoid wasps (1–2 per sq m), lacewing larvae (*Chrysoperla*), ladybug larvae (avoid adults — they fly away), *Aphidoletes aphidimyza* gall midge
- **Botanical**: neem oil weekly veg, insecticidal soap contact
- **Mineral**: sulfur (veg only, last resort)

### Whiteflies (Trialeurodes vaporariorum, Bemisia tabaci)

**Identification:**
- 1–2 mm white moth-like adults; hold wings tent-like over body
- Cluster on leaf undersides; fly up in cloud when disturbed
- Larvae: flat translucent scales on leaf underside, easily missed
- Eggs: tiny white circles under leaves

**Damage symptoms:**
- Same as aphids: yellowing, honeydew, sooty mold
- Virus vector concern (Bemisia transmits begomoviruses — relevant for outdoor / greenhouse)
- Persistent and population-doubling — clone-borne issue

**Lifecycle:**
- Egg → 4 nymph instars (sessile) → adult, 18–28 days
- Eggs laid on leaf underside, larvae crawl briefly then settle
- Adults can fly between plants — spread fast

**Conditions favored:**
- Greenhouse + indoor
- Imported clones from infested mother plants

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: yellow sticky cards (high attraction); vacuum adults (sounds silly, works); sanitize between cycles
- **Biological**: *Encarsia formosa* (parasitoid wasp) — 1–3 per plant per week × 4 weeks; *Eretmocerus eremicus* for Bemisia; *Amblyseius swirskii* eats eggs
- **Botanical**: neem + karanja, insecticidal soap (under-leaf coverage essential)
- **Soft synthetic**: hort oil for thorough underleaf coverage

### Root Aphids (Rice root aphid Rhopalosiphum rufiabdominale, Pemphigus species)

**Identification:**
- 1–2 mm pale/cream/grey teardrop-shaped bodies on roots
- Pointy protrusions on hind ends (Tier 3 — community ID consensus)
- Cluster on roots, often visible as soft, tissue-like masses
- Some species winged adults migrate (pots near each other = transmission)

**Damage symptoms:**
- Roots yellow, swollen, or appear "hairy" with aphid masses
- Sudden wilt of healthy-looking plant — characteristic warning sign
- Stunted growth, slow nutrient uptake despite normal feed
- Hidden until severe — often discovered at transplant

**Lifecycle:**
- Cycles between soil-dwelling and aerial winged forms
- Generation time 7–14 days in warm root zone
- Once established, very persistent — soil-borne reservoir

**Conditions favored:**
- Recirculating systems (ebb and flow, RDWC)
- Warm damp medium
- Re-used soil/coco
- Cannabis monoculture room

**Confusion risk:**
- Springtails (springtails JUMP, are tiny 1–2 mm white, beneficial decomposers)
- Soil mites (white tiny soil mites; check shape — mites are 8-legged)
- Healthy mycorrhizae fungi (white root coating but fluffy/web-like, not clustered insects)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: isolate plant, avoid reusing contaminated media, clean containers/tools, quarantine incoming clones, and consider restarting clean if roots are heavily infested
- **Biological**: *Steinernema feltiae* nematodes at full rate (10,000/L substrate, repeat 10 days) (Tier 2 — Koppert); *Stratiolaelaps scimitus* in soil
- **Botanical**: azadirachtin products only when label-compatible for soil drench; avoid oil drenches that can suffocate roots or disrupt living soil
- **Avoid in flower**: most synthetic systemics that work are flower-incompatible
- **Severe case**: dispose of affected plants/substrate and restart clean rather than escalating to unsafe chemicals

### Caterpillars / Budworms (Helicoverpa zea — corn earworm, H. armigera — cotton bollworm, Heliothis virescens — tobacco budworm)

**Identification:**
- 1–4 cm caterpillars, color highly variable (green, brown, striped, pink)
- Adults are noctuid moths active at night — outdoor / greenhouse entry
- Frass (small black fecal pellets) is the diagnostic sign — visible in flower clusters and on leaves below

**Damage symptoms:**
- Holes chewed in leaves
- Flowers tunneled — cat enters bud, eats inside, frass exits at hole
- **Frass + bud rot is a death spiral** — caterpillar damage opens entry for *Botrytis* (gray mold), bud collapse follows (Tier 1 — Cranshaw, OSU Extension; cross-ref `09-disease.md`)
- One larva ruins multiple buds

**Lifecycle:**
- Adult moths fly at night, lay eggs on plants
- Egg hatches in 3–7 days
- Larvae feed for 14–28 days (this is the damage window)
- Pupate in soil or leaf litter
- Multiple generations per season outdoor; year-round indoor if undisturbed

**Conditions favored:**
- Outdoor / greenhouse / hoop house
- Late summer / early fall (Aug–Oct in N. hemisphere)
- Adjacent corn or cotton crops (alternate hosts)

**Confusion risk:**
- Cabbage looper (similar damage; doesn't tunnel buds as deep)
- Hemp borer (smaller, tunnels stems; less common)

**Treatment ladder:**
- **Cultural**: hand-pick at dawn/dusk with flashlight; fine mesh netting over outdoor canopy; pheromone traps for adult moth monitoring
- **Biological**: **BT — Bacillus thuringiensis kurstaki (BTK) or aizawai** — caterpillar-specific and must be ingested. Apply only according to label; outdoor applications may need repeating after rain or UV exposure.
- **Biological**: HearNPV (Helicoverpa zea Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus) — host-specific viral biopesticide
- **Biological**: *Trichogramma* parasitoid wasps (egg parasitoid, outdoor primarily)
- **Botanical**: spinosad — works, late-flower questionable
- **Stage note**: BT can be among the lower-risk caterpillar tools when label-compatible, but avoid spraying dense buds when rot/residue risk outweighs benefit.

### Mealybugs and Scale Insects

**Combined entry — both are sap-sucking, sessile-as-adults, slow-spreading.**

**Mealybugs:**
- Soft white waxy clusters on stems, leaf joints, lower stem
- 2–5 mm, slow-moving
- Honeydew + sooty mold like aphids
- Treatment: mechanical spot removal with a cotton swab, *Cryptolaemus montrouzieri* for biological control, and label-compatible soap/oil products in veg

**Scale insects:**
- Hard or soft brown bumps on stems and leaf petioles
- 2–4 mm, look almost like part of the plant once settled
- Crawler stage (emerging young) is the only mobile stage and the only easy treatment window
- Treatment: mechanical spot removal, soap/oil products in veg, and appropriate parasitoids where available

**Both: low priority unless they spread — slow life cycles allow patient cultural management.**

| **Beet armyworm / armyworms** | Skeletonize leaves outdoor | BT, hand-pick, screening |

## IPM TREATMENT TOOLKIT — by Class

### Cultural

The most-underrated and cheapest tier. Get this right and 80% of pest problems don't happen.

- **Quarantine new plants 14–21 days** in separate room; inspect daily under magnification
- **Clean tools** between plants; remove sap/debris first, then use an appropriate disinfectant for tools/surfaces
- **Screened intakes** on every air entry (50-mesh minimum for thrips)
- **Filtered intake / slight positive pressure** where practical — reduces pest entry through cracks and vents
- **Between-cycle sanitation** — remove plant debris, clean surfaces, inspect intake screens; sulfur/space treatments only if label-compatible and safe for an empty room
- **Yellow + blue sticky traps** at canopy level — yellow catches most flying pests, blue catches thrips preferentially
- **Incoming clone wash/dip**: use only products labeled for live plants. Do not dip living plant tissue in 70% isopropyl alcohol; alcohol is for tools/surfaces, not clones.
- **Mother plant inspection** weekly — clones inherit mother's pests
- **Insect screening** (mesh) on greenhouse inlets and exhaust

### Biological

Biological controls are often the best flower-safe option when started early and matched to the pest and environment. They are living organisms: temperature, RH, prior sprays, and release timing matter.

| Predator/Parasite | Targets | Release rate (preventive) | Release rate (curative) | Conditions |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| *Phytoseiulus persimilis* | Spider mites (specialist) | 1–2/sq ft | 5–10/sq ft | RH >60%, temp 65–85°F |
| *Neoseiulus californicus* | Spider mites, broader range | 1/sq ft | 4/sq ft | Tolerates lower RH than persimilis |
| *Amblyseius swirskii* | Thrips eggs/larvae, whiteflies, broad mites | 50–100/m² | 200–400/m² | RH >65%, temp >68°F |
| *Amblyseius cucumeris* | Thrips (eggs only) | 100/m² | 500/m² | Cooler-tolerant than swirskii |
| *Amblyseius andersoni* | Spider mites, broad mites, russet mites | 1–2/sq ft | 5/sq ft | Wide temp range |
| *Stratiolaelaps scimitus* | Fungus gnat larvae, thrips pupae, soil pests | 25k–50k/m³ | One-time soil application | Moist soil, 50–86°F |
| *Steinernema feltiae* (nematodes) | Fungus gnat larvae, thrips pupae, root aphids | 500k/m² | 500k/m² + repeat 10 days | Moist soil, 50–86°F, dark |
| *Encarsia formosa* | Whiteflies (parasitoid) | 1–3/plant/week | 5/plant/week | Temp >65°F |
| *Aphidius colemani* | Aphids (parasitoid) | 0.5/m²/week | 2/m²/week | All conditions |
| *Aphidoletes aphidimyza* | Aphids (predator midge) | 1/m²/week | 5/m²/week | RH >70% |
| *Chrysoperla* (lacewing larvae) | Aphids, soft pests (general) | Hot-spot release | 1k–10k larvae/grow | Temp >60°F |
| *Cryptolaemus montrouzieri* | Mealybugs (specialist) | Hot-spot release | 5/plant infestation | Temp >70°F |
| *Trichogramma* | Caterpillar eggs (parasitoid) | Outdoor primarily | 30k/acre | Moth flight season |

**Slow-release sachets**: predator-mite sachets hung early in canopy development can provide continuous release for several weeks. Use supplier rates and replace on schedule.

**Compatibility**: most biologicals are killed by recent oil/soap/sulfur sprays. Wait 7–14 days after a chemical application before releasing predators. Conversely, don't spray within a week of release.

### Botanical

Plant-derived does not automatically mean safe on consumable flower. Use label-compatible products, test on a small area, protect beneficials, and avoid spraying buds.

| Botanical | Target | Stage | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neem/azadirachtin products** | Mites, aphids, whitefly, thrips, growth-regulator effect | Veg only | Follow label; coat undersides; hot weather/light stress risks burn |
| **Karanja products** | Similar use case to neem | Veg only | Follow label; avoid flowers and heat stress |
| **Pyrethrum / pyrethrins** | Broad-spectrum knockdown | Veg only if label-compatible | Can kill beneficials; avoid routine use |
| **Spinosad** | Caterpillars, thrips | Veg/early only if label-compatible | Harmful to some beneficials/pollinators; avoid flowers |
| **Garlic + capsaicin extracts** | Repellent for some pests | Veg/early only | Modest effect; still avoid spraying buds |
| **Rosemary / cedar / mint oils** | Repellent/contact effect | Veg/early only | Oils can leave residues and burn plants; avoid buds |
| **Cinnamon oil** | Soil-borne pathogens, ants | Veg | Anti-fungal also |

### Mineral

| Mineral | Target | Stage | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sulfur products** | Some mites, PM | Veg/empty room only; never near oils | Follow label/PPE; sulfur burners are not beginner-default and must never run around people, pets, or plants unless explicitly labeled |
| **Potassium silicate (Si supplement)** | IPM cell-wall hardening, mild mite resistance | All stages (it's a nutrient) | Add to feed weekly throughout veg/early flower |
| **Kaolin clay (Surround)** | Outdoor reflection / repellent | Pre-flower outdoor | Coats leaves white; wash before harvest |
| **Diatomaceous earth (DE)** | Soft-bodied insects on dry surfaces | Soil surface, sticky-trap edges | Useless when wet; lung irritant — wear mask |
| **Homemade sulfur mixes** | Mites, PM | Avoid as default | Concentration errors can injure plants and users; prefer labeled products |

### Soft synthetic

| Product | Target | Stage | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Insecticidal soap (potassium fatty acid salts)** | Soft-bodied pests, contact only | Veg; avoid buds | Must directly hit pests; no residual; follow label and test first |
| **Horticultural oil** | Smothering eggs + adults | Veg only | Follow label; avoid heat/light stress and never combine near sulfur |

### Restricted / residual pesticides — do not recommend

Do not recommend restricted, systemic, unlabeled, or residual pesticides for consumable cannabis. When soft IPM fails, the safer public guidance is quarantine, cull, restart clean, or consult a qualified local extension/professional resource. Avoid product-name recipes for hard pesticides; pesticide labels are legal safety documents and local rules vary.

## Quick Reference — Diagnostic Tree

```
START → Where's the damage?

LEAVES?
├── Tiny dot stippling, leaf underside has visible specks → spider mites (loupe to confirm)
├── Webbing in canopy → spider mites (severe)
├── Silver linear streaks + black specks → thrips
├── Bronzing + golden-brown dust + brittle leaves → hemp russet mite (microscope mandatory)
├── Distorted, "wet"-looking, blistered NEW growth → broad mite (microscope) OR Ca def OR HLVd
├── Yellowing + sticky residue + sooty mold → aphids or whiteflies (check undersides)
├── White moth-like flying when disturbed → whitefly
├── Cluster of soft pear-shaped bugs → aphids (cornicles diagnostic)
└── Holes + frass pellets → caterpillars

STEM/BUDS?
├── White waxy clusters on stems → mealybugs
├── Brown/black hard bumps on stems → scale
├── Hollow chewed bud + frass → caterpillar (budworm) — see 09-disease for accompanying rot
└── White cottony patches → mealybug or PM (check texture; PM is dry powder, mealybug is waxy)

SOIL SURFACE?
├── Tiny dark flying gnats near pot → fungus gnats (yellow sticky traps to confirm)
├── Tiny white JUMPING insects → springtails (beneficial — leave alone)
├── Tiny crawling 6-legged not jumping → could be soil mite (loupe — most are beneficial)
└── Yellow sticky trap full of dark flies → fungus gnats confirmed

ROOTS (lift plant to inspect)?
├── White cottony clusters on roots → root aphids
├── Yellow swollen / "hairy" roots → root aphid mass
├── Galls or knots → root knot nematodes (rare indoor)
└── Slime + brown roots → root rot, see 09-disease

NEW GROWTH ONLY DEFORMED, NO VISIBLE PEST?
├── Microscope check first → russet/broad mite
├── No mites → check for HLVd (multiple plants? clones from same mother?)
└── Single plant only → could be Ca/B def, see 07-deficiencies

WHOLE PLANT WILTING DESPITE WATERING?
├── Lift plant inspect roots → root aphids likely
├── Healthy roots → check pH and feed (07 + 04)
└── Slimy roots → root rot, 09-disease
```

## Common Misdiagnosis Traps

| Looks like | Actually | How to tell |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium deficiency | Russet or broad mite | Microscope; mites move, deficiency doesn't |
| HLVd | Russet mite damage on multiple plants | Microscope; HLVd has no mites at all |
| Fungus gnat damage | Overwatering symptoms (drooping) | Lift pot; gnat larvae visible in top inch of moist soil |
| Aphid sticky leaves | Watering issue / honeydew misread | Look up the stem — aphid colony will be there |
| Spider mites | Mineral deficiency stippling | Underside check; mites move, def doesn't |
| Bud rot | Caterpillar damage | Caterpillars frass externally; bud rot starts internal — often both occurring |
| Thrips streaking | Silica mineral spots | Streaks are linear with frass; silica spots are isolated |
| Springtails | Root aphids | Springtails JUMP and are on soil surface; root aphids cluster on roots, don't jump |
| Soil mites in living soil | Bad pests | Most soil mites are beneficial decomposers — ID under loupe before treating |
| Powdery mildew | Trichome accumulation in late flower | PM spreads in colony patches; trichomes are uniform on bud |
| Fungus gnat ADULTS | The actual problem | Adults are mostly cosmetic; LARVAE in soil are the damage source |
| White-coated roots | Mycorrhizae (good) vs root aphids (bad) | Mycos are fluffy/webbing white; root aphids are clustered teardrops |

## Verification Questions Before Treating

1. Pest identified under what magnification? (Loupe / microscope / naked eye / not yet)
2. Life stage observed? (Eggs / larvae / nymphs / adults / mixed)
3. Severity? (One plant / one branch / canopy-wide / multiple plants)
4. Stage of grow? (Seedling / veg / pre-flower / mid-flower / late-flower)
5. Any treatment in last 14 days? What product, label rate, and date?
6. Recent quarantine break? (New clones, mother plant, visitor, outdoor exposure)
7. Beneficials already present in room?
8. RH and temp at canopy? (Some predators need RH >60%)
9. Re-treatment cadence planned to break lifecycle?
10. Is the plant in flower or intended for consumption soon? Any residue concern?

If 4+ unknown → don't treat yet. Get the data. Wrong-target sprays make resistance worse.
