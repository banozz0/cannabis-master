# 15 — Curing

Curing is the slow biochemistry phase where flower develops its final flavor profile, smoke smoothness, and stability. Cure can polish a good dry into excellent flower — but cure cannot rescue a bad dry. The difference between "okay" homegrow and "exceptional" flower is rarely the strain or the grow technique; it's whether the dry was patient and the cure was disciplined for 4+ weeks. Cross-reference with `14-drying.md` (this file's input is dry's output — moisture content critical going in), `16-trimming.md` (dry-trim path executes between dry and cure for some workflows), `09-disease.md` (Aspergillus and Penicillium emerge if jarring too wet — Aw >0.65 = mold risk), `13-harvest.md` (yield tracking continues through cure), `state/grow-log.md` (cure entries with weight tracking).

## How to Use This File

1. **Confirm dry completion** — snap test passed across multiple plants + 24-hour jar test (no condensation) + weight stabilized.
2. **Choose container method** — glass mason jars (standard), Grove Bags TerpLoc (terpene-protective barrier), Cannatrol storage mode (controlled-environment cabinet), or Mylar+Boveda (long-term).
3. **Load buds with proper density** — fill 70–75% of jar (airflow space; not packed tight).
4. **Execute burp schedule** — weeks 1–4 schedule below; less frequent in Grove Bags.
5. **Monitor water activity (Aw) or RH** — target Aw 0.55–0.65 per ASTM D8197 standard; or 58–62% RH as Boveda proxy.
6. **Minimum 2 weeks active cure** before evaluating; ideal 4–8 weeks; premium 6+ months.
7. **Transition to long-term storage** when RH/Aw stabilizes and burping reveals stable conditions.

## Critical Concept — What Curing Actually Does

Cure is a coordinated set of slow biochemical processes that continue from where drying left off. Understanding the chemistry tells you why the protocol matters.

**The biochemistry continuing post-dry:**
- **Chlorophyll degradation completes** — enzymatic breakdown started in dry continues; Chlorophyllase enzyme converts Chlorophyll A → Chlorophyllide; produces "hay smell → terpene-forward" final transition (Tier 3 sources cite this; biochemistry general plant science)
- **Sugar / starch enzymatic conversion** — complex sugars break down to simpler compounds; smoke smoothness improves measurably
- **Aerobic bacterial activity (controlled)** — small amount of beneficial bacteria continue working in moisture-balanced environment; consume chlorophyll-derived compounds; over-wet → mold; over-dry → halts entirely
- **Terpene profile maturation** — minor terpene compounds equilibrate; "rounded" flavor profile develops; some terpenes form from precursors during cure
- **Cannabinoid stability** — THCA/CBDA stable under proper cure conditions (cool, dark, controlled RH); slow degradation toward CBN over months at higher RH/temp
- **Moisture redistribution** — water migrates from interior bud mass to surface and equilibrates with headspace and Boveda packs (the "burping" mechanism; opening releases accumulated vapor)

**Why this matters operationally:**
- Cure transforms acidic / harsh / grass-smelling smoke into smooth / aromatic / characteristic-strain expression
- The transition takes weeks because enzymatic and bacterial processes are slow at controlled-environment temperatures
- Cure cannot reverse a rushed dry (chlorophyll breakdown stalled in dry doesn't restart in cure)
- Cure can recover from minor over-dry (re-equilibrate with Boveda) but cannot recover from case hardening or mold

(Background: Tier 1 — Caplan/Zheng general post-harvest research; Sutton et al. chlorophyll degradation kinetics; Tier 3 — Cervantes, Rosenthal cure chapters as primary practical sources.)

## Critical Concept — Water Activity (Aw): The Modern Standard

Water activity is the commercial / regulatory standard for cannabis moisture management. Different from RH or moisture content; measures water available for biological/chemical processes.

**ASTM D8197 standard** (Tier 1 — ASTM official): "Standard Specification for Maintaining Acceptable Water Activity (aw) Range (0.55 to 0.65) for Dry Cannabis Flower Intended for Human/Animal Use."
- **Aw must be <0.65** to prevent undesirable growth of microorganisms (mold, especially Aspergillus)
- **Aw must be >0.55** to prevent physical damage (breakage) in routine handling and storage
- ASTM D8196 specifies the measurement procedures

**Why Aw matters more than moisture content:**
- Two flower samples can have identical moisture content but different Aw (free vs bound water)
- Aw is what microbes actually access for metabolism — predicts mold risk directly
- Aspergillus, the highest-priority cure-stage pathogen, grows at the lowest Aw of any mold species
- Commercial state testing programs increasingly require Aw measurement, not RH

**Aw measurement equipment:**
- **Commercial labs / large operations:** Aqualab, Rotronic, Decagon meters ($300–2000)
- **Hobby growers:** RH meter inside jar serves as proxy (62% RH equilibrates buds to ~0.62 Aw at sealed equilibrium)
- **Boveda 62 packs:** automatically maintain 62% RH ≈ 0.62 Aw — the cure-target standard

**Aw range outcomes:**
| Aw range | Outcome |
|---|---|
| <0.50 | Over-dry; brittle; harsh smoke; cure stalled |
| 0.50–0.55 | Dry; cure progresses slowly; below ASTM minimum |
| **0.55–0.65** | **Optimal cure range (ASTM D8197)** |
| 0.65–0.70 | Mold risk territory; Aspergillus growth possible |
| 0.70+ | Mold risk high; Botrytis, Aspergillus, Penicillium all viable |

(Tier 1 — ASTM D8197, D8196; Tier 2 — Aroya, Cannatrol, Mobius, ARES Scientific commercial Aw monitoring guidance.)

## Critical Concept — The Burping Protocol

Opening jars (or breaking the seal in any cure container) serves two functions:
1. **Releases accumulated moisture** — water vapor migrating from bud interior to headspace gets exchanged with drier ambient air
2. **Replaces stale air with fresh oxygen** — supports aerobic bacterial activity that breaks down chlorophyll-derived compounds

**Standard burping schedule (Tier 3 — community consensus across multiple sources):**

| Phase | Frequency | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Week 1, days 1–3 | 2× daily | 5–15 minutes |
| Week 1, days 4–7 | 1× daily | 10 minutes |
| Week 2 | 1× daily | 5–10 minutes |
| Week 3 | Every 2–3 days | 5 minutes |
| Week 4+ | 1–2× per week | brief check |
| Long-term storage | Monthly inspection | brief |

**Burp by data, not just schedule:**
- **Hygrometer in each jar** (modern gold standard) — drives decisions
- If RH inside jar exceeds 65% within 24h of last burp → burp longer / more frequently
- If RH drops below 58% → burping too aggressively or buds too dry
- If RH stable at 60–62% for several days → cure is on track

**Smell evolution markers** (qualitative tracking):
1. Days 0–3: hay/grass smell dominant (chlorophyll still breaking down)
2. Days 4–7: hay fading; "plant" smell
3. Week 2: terpene notes emerging
4. Week 3–4: strain expression developing
5. Week 6–8: mature flavor profile

**Burp duration reality check:**
- Brief burps (1–2 minutes) in early cure if RH already stable
- Longer burps (15+ minutes) if RH is consistently above 65%
- "Burp by smell" — if ammonia-like aroma when opening, buds were too wet to start; longer burps + extended dry-back

**Boveda 62 packs simplify burping** — they actively manage RH, so less frequent burping needed. With Boveda + glass jar: burp daily week 1, every other day week 2, weekly thereafter.

**Grove Bags TerpLoc** — minimal burping needed; the bag's modified atmosphere film handles moisture/oxygen exchange.

## Critical Concept — The Cure Timeline Quality Curve

Quality continues improving for weeks. Plotting subjective quality against cure duration produces an asymptotic curve.

| Time | Stage | Quality progress |
|---|---|---|
| Week 0–2 | Active cure | Chlorophyll completes breakdown; hay smell fades; harshness reduces; smoothness emerges |
| Week 2–4 | Smooth cure | Terpene profile rounds; smoke quality improves significantly; "good" flower achievable |
| Week 4–8 | Mature cure | Flavor depth peaks; standard "well-cured" target; most growers stop here |
| Month 2–6 | Long cure | Terpene complexity continues developing; some specialty growers cure indefinitely; subtle improvements |
| Month 6+ | Diminishing returns | Storage stability matters more than cure progression; THC slowly degrades to CBN |
| 12+ months | Storage phase | Long-term Mylar+Boveda or vacuum/freeze; preservation, not active cure |

**Strain dependence:** heavy-resin / "exotic" strains often benefit from 3–6 month cures; commercial / commodity strains plateau at 4–6 weeks. Test on your specific genetics; track over multiple cycles in `state/grow-log.md`.

(Tier 2 — University of Arizona research on terpene/cannabinoid synergy ("entourage effect"); Tier 3 — connoisseur and breeder long-cure consensus.)

## CONTAINER METHODS — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Glass Mason Jars (the traditional standard)

**Principle:** Wide-mouth glass jars with airtight lids. Standard for hobby and small-scale cure. No flavor contamination; visual inspection through glass; well-sized for batches.

**Sizes:**
- Half-pint (8 oz): ~1/4 oz dry flower (small batches, samples)
- Pint (16 oz): ~1/2–3/4 oz dry flower
- Quart (32 oz): ~1.5–2 oz dry flower (the standard cure jar)
- Half-gallon (64 oz): ~3–4 oz dry flower (larger batches)

**Filling:** 70–75% full (airflow space critical; tightly packed = uneven cure).

**Equipment:**
- Wide-mouth jars (Ball, Kerr, Mason)
- Hygrometer (one per jar minimum; Boveda Butler or similar)
- Boveda 62 packs (sized to jar volume)
- Cool dark cure cabinet or closet

**Burp schedule:** standard (above table).

**Pros:**
- Cheapest per unit
- Reusable indefinitely
- Visual inspection through glass
- No chemical contamination
- Available everywhere
- Holds Boveda well

**Cons:**
- Heavier than alternatives (storage weight)
- Glass breakage risk
- Requires daily attention in week 1
- Doesn't shield from light (use cool dark room or wrap jars)

### Grove Bags (TerpLoc™ Patented Technology)

**Principle:** Multi-layered resin film with modified atmospheric properties. Auto-regulates humidity (58–62% RH) and gas exchange; designed to preserve terpenes and cannabinoids during cure and storage.

**Mechanism (Tier 2 — Grove Bags official):**
- Multi-layered resin film with modified atmospheric properties
- Static charge neutralized along sidewall to prevent trichome stripping
- UV protection
- Auto-regulating moisture exchange via differential film permeability

**Performance claims (Tier 2 — Grove Bags independent testing):**
- 37% more terpenoid retention vs traditional packaging
- 7% more cannabinoid retention vs traditional packaging
- THC levels 7.25% higher in TerpLoc vs other storage methods

**Sizes:** small (3.5 g) up to commercial (5 lb).

**Burp schedule:** minimal — bag's atmospheric film handles exchange. Some growers don't burp at all.

**Pros:**
- Minimal labor (no daily burping)
- Excellent terpene retention
- Light shielding built in
- Consistent moisture control
- Scales to commercial volume

**Cons:**
- Single-use (or limited reuse)
- Cost per bag higher than glass
- Buds need to enter at correct moisture content (no recovery from wet jarring)
- Requires trust in technology vs hands-on cure

**Best fit:** intermediate-to-premium home growers, commercial operations, terpene-priority strains.

### Cannatrol Storage Mode (Vaportrol Technology)

**Principle:** Controlled-environment cabinet manages temp + humidity (via dew point control) automatically. Continues from cure into indefinite storage.

**Specs (cross-ref `14-drying.md`):**
- Storage phase: 68°F / 53°F dew point
- Same cabinet handles dry → cure → storage on different program settings
- Cool Cure: 2.49 cu ft holds 4–5 lbs dry flower

**Burp schedule:** none — cabinet handles all atmospheric management.

**Pros:**
- Fully automated atmospheric control
- Combines dry + cure + storage in one unit
- Repeatable across batches

**Cons:**
- Equipment cost ($2k–10k+)
- Limited capacity per unit
- Power dependence (24/7 operation)
- Fewer "craft" feel-it-out elements

### Mylar Bags + Boveda (long-term storage standard)

**Principle:** Light-blocking foil bag with airtight seal; Boveda packs maintain RH; some passive gas exchange through seam.

**Best for:** post-cure long-term storage (after 4+ weeks active cure); 6 months to 12+ months stable storage.

**Sizes:** quarter-ounce up to commercial pound bags.

**Equipment:** Mylar bags (3–5 mil thickness), Boveda 58% or 62% packs, optional vacuum-sealer for compression.

**Pros:**
- Light-blocking (UV degrades cannabinoids)
- Airtight when sealed
- Long shelf life (months to years)
- Compact storage

**Cons:**
- Single-use unless re-sealable
- No visual inspection without opening
- Not for active cure (closed system, no burping option)

### Vacuum-Sealed Bags (long-term inventory storage)

**Principle:** All air evacuated; pauses cure progression entirely; preservation mode.

**Best for:** finished cure → long-term storage; commercial inventory rotation; preservation of finished product.

**Equipment:** vacuum sealer (FoodSaver-style; commercial chamber sealers for higher volume); thick mil bags rated for vacuum.

**Pros:**
- Maximum oxygen exclusion
- Pauses degradation
- Compact storage
- 1–2 years stable shelf life

**Cons:**
- Cure progression stops (so vacuum AFTER preferred cure level reached)
- Trichomes can crush under vacuum (use semi-vacuum settings or wrap in something protective)
- No moisture management (must be at correct moisture going in)
- Single-use bags

### Frozen Long-Term Storage

**Principle:** Freezer at 0°F (-18°C) or below; preserves cannabinoids and terpenes for years.

**Best for:** specialty long-term preservation, future hash/extract destination, genetic library samples.

**Protocol:**
1. Complete normal cure (4+ weeks active)
2. Reduce moisture content slightly (Boveda 58 for week before freezing)
3. Vacuum-seal in Mylar bags
4. Place in freezer; maintain 0°F or below; minimize freeze-thaw cycles
5. To retrieve: let bag thaw to room temperature BEFORE opening (prevents condensation); handle gently (frozen trichomes are brittle)

**Pros:**
- Multi-year preservation
- Slows all degradation
- Terpene and cannabinoid retention excellent

**Cons:**
- Trichomes brittle when frozen (handle only at room temp)
- Some terpene loss on freeze-thaw cycles (minimize)
- Requires freezer space and stable temp
- Not for everyday consumption product

### Cardboard / Paper Boxes (specialty)

**Principle:** Old-school technique; paper or cardboard wicks moisture; dark cool box for cure.

**Best for:** outdoor harvests with high volume; specialty growers preferring "natural" cure; pre-jar cure stage.

**Pros:**
- Cheap; uses existing materials
- Allows passive moisture exchange
- Light-blocking

**Cons:**
- Pest entry risk (rodents, insects)
- Smell loss (cardboard absorbs aroma)
- No moisture control (relies on ambient RH)
- Not airtight; easy contamination

**Honest framing:** specialty technique; some growers swear by it for long cures. Mainstream cure should use jars / Grove Bags / Cannatrol.

### Containers to AVOID

- **Plastic containers (Tupperware, food storage):** leach plasticizers; BPA concerns; not airtight enough for cure
- **Metal tins:** can affect flavor (especially aluminum); steel may rust; not for primary cure
- **Cigar humidors:** designed for higher RH (65–70%); too wet for cannabis
- **Ziploc / sandwich bags:** not airtight; static cling damages trichomes; not appropriate for cure

## BOVEDA / HUMIDITY PACK MANAGEMENT

**The three cannabis-relevant RH packs:**

| Pack | RH | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Boveda 58%** | 58% RH | Personal-use (joints, vapes, pre-rolls); arid environments; drier preference |
| **Boveda 62%** | 62% RH | **The cure-target standard**; preserves terpene profile; ideal for premium |
| Boveda 65% | 65% RH | Cigar humidors; rare for cannabis; too wet for most strains |
| Boveda 69% | 69% RH | Cigars only; not for cannabis |

**Pack sizing per container volume (Tier 2 — Boveda official):**
- 1g pack per 28g (1 oz) flower minimum
- 8g pack per ounce-volume container (commercial size)
- 67g pack per 1 lb storage container

**Pack replacement schedule:**
- When pack goes hard (loses pliability) → replace
- Typical lifespan 2–6 months depending on environment
- Multiple packs share workload — replace before all are exhausted
- Don't dry out an exhausted pack (rehydrate with distilled water) — they're calibrated for two-way exchange

**Two-way humidity control mechanism:** Boveda packs both ABSORB and RELEASE moisture to maintain target RH. If buds too wet, packs absorb. If too dry, packs release. The salt solution inside maintains a vapor pressure equilibrium with the surrounding air at the target RH.

**Boveda compatibility with Grove Bags:** redundant — Grove Bags self-regulate via TerpLoc; adding Boveda inside doesn't add benefit and may interfere. Pick one method.

**Boveda + Cannatrol:** redundant — Cannatrol's Vaportrol manages atmosphere; no Boveda needed.

## LONG-TERM STORAGE TRANSITION

When active cure is complete (typically 4–8 weeks), transition to long-term storage. Signals:
- RH stable at target (no daily fluctuation despite no burping)
- Smell stabilized (strain expression developed; no further evolution)
- Smoke quality smooth (test small samples)
- Burping reveals stable conditions (no headspace moisture or off-smells)

**Long-term storage environment:**
- **Temp:** 60–68°F (cool but not refrigerated)
- **RH:** 58–62% (Boveda)
- **Light:** dark (UV degrades cannabinoids)
- **Container:** airtight (Mylar + Boveda OR vacuum-sealed; jars also work)
- **Location:** cool basement, climate-controlled cellar, or dedicated cure cabinet
- **Avoid:** garages (temp swings), bathrooms (humidity), kitchens (light + heat)

**Storage duration outcomes:**
| Duration | Outcome |
|---|---|
| 0–3 months | Active cure progression continues; flavor improves |
| 3–6 months | Stable flavor; minimal degradation |
| 6–12 months | Slow THC → CBN conversion at higher RH/temp; minor terpene loss |
| 12–24 months | Noticeable potency drift; flavor changes |
| 24+ months | Significant degradation unless vacuum/frozen |

**For 12+ month storage:** vacuum-sealed Mylar + Boveda 58 in cool dark room OR frozen vacuum-sealed (freeze method above).

## CURE FAILURES & RECOVERIES

| Failure | Cause | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Mold (Aspergillus, Penicillium) in cure jar** | Aw >0.65 (jarred too wet); Aspergillus grows at lowest Aw of any mold | Discard affected; don't cross-contaminate; investigate moisture source upstream; commercial: extraction or gamma irradiation remediation possible |
| **Cure jars wet despite burping** | Case hardening from dry stage (outside dry, inside wet) | Empty jars; return buds to controlled drying environment 60% RH for 3–5 days; restart cure with proper moisture content |
| **Hay smell not fading after 4+ weeks** | Locked in from rushed dry; chlorophyll didn't break down at dry stage | Cannot recover; cure cannot reverse a rushed dry; learn for next time |
| **Brittle / over-dry / harsh** | Cure jars opened too long; ambient too dry; burped too aggressively | Cautious rehydration with Boveda 62 packs in sealed jar over 7–14 days; partial recovery only |
| **Ammonia smell when opening** | Anaerobic bacteria from too-wet jarring | Burp aggressively (1+ hours); if persists, may indicate loss; consult disease section |
| **Cure stalled (no smell evolution)** | Cure environment too cold (<50°F halts activity) | Move to 60–65°F environment; resume burp schedule |
| **Boveda packs hardening fast** | Too much moisture in flower; jar hasn't equilibrated | Add fresh packs; burp longer; investigate dry-stage moisture issue |
| **Smell weak after cure** | Terpene loss from rushed dry (not a cure issue) | Cannot recover; learn for next cycle |
| **Cure jars never stabilize RH** | Uneven moisture within buds (case hardening); or jar not sealed | Re-equilibrate at 60% RH controlled environment; check jar seal |

**The Aspergillus warning (Tier 1):** "Aspergillus is one of the most effective indoor saprophytes able to grow at the lowest water activity of any other mold species" — this is why ASTM D8197 sets Aw <0.65 as the hard upper bound. Containerizing without checking moisture is the #1 cause of cure failures (Tier 2 — Aroya, Cannatrol industry sources). Any wet pocket inside a sealed jar is an Aspergillus opportunity.

## Quick Reference — Container Selection Tree

```
ACTIVE CURE (week 0-4)?
├── Standard / hobby → Glass mason jars + Boveda 62 packs
├── Premium / minimal-burp → Grove Bags TerpLoc
├── Set-and-forget premium → Cannatrol storage mode
└── Commercial / scale → Glass jars or Grove Bags depending on labor budget

LONG-TERM STORAGE (after week 4-8)?
├── 6 months → Mylar + Boveda 58 or 62 in dark cool room
├── 12 months → Vacuum-sealed Mylar + Boveda
├── 12+ months / specialty → Frozen vacuum-sealed (thaw protocol)
└── Inventory rotation → FIFO with weight tracking; vacuum-sealed batches

BUDS TOO WET FOR CURE?
├── Snap test failed → back to dry environment 7+ more days
├── Snap test passed but jar test failed → case hardening; controlled re-equilibrate
└── Mold visible → cull affected; consult 09-disease.md for full protocol

BUDS TOO DRY?
├── Brittle from over-dry → Boveda 62 in sealed jar 7-14 days; partial recovery
├── Cure stalled → temp too cold; warm to 60-65°F
└── Cracked / shatter prone → ASTM D8197 minimum Aw 0.55 violated; rehydrate cautiously
```

## Common Misdiagnosis Traps

| Looks like | Actually | How to tell |
|---|---|---|
| **Cure jars wet** | **Often case hardening from dry stage**, not cure issue | If snap-test passed but jar wet within 24h → case hardening |
| **Smell weak after cure** | **Terpene loss from rushed dry**, not cure issue | Hay smell fading is cure progress; weak overall smell is dry-stage loss (not recoverable) |
| **Boveda not working** | Packs hardened (replace), jar not sealed, or way too much moisture in flower | Check pack pliability; verify seal; check buds with hygrometer |
| **Cure taking too long** | Strain-dependent; some need 8+ weeks | Track strain history in grow-log |
| **Brittle after cure** | Over-dry going INTO cure; cure didn't cause it | Investigate dry stage; cautious rehydration |
| **Mold in cure** | Jarred too wet (Aw >0.65); case hardening; or contamination | Restart with proper dry; check ASTM D8197 Aw range |
| **Cure in winter cold basement** | Below 50°F halts cure; not a "slow cure" | Warm to 60°F minimum |
| **Buds smell weird after long cure** | Maillard reactions (sugars + amino acids); some good ("vanilla" notes), some bad ("ammonia") | Smell test; if ammonia, anaerobic activity; longer burps or move on |
| **Trichomes look gray after cure** | Light exposure (UV degrades) | Move to dark storage; partial damage |
| **Vacuum-sealed buds compressed flat** | Vacuum too aggressive; trichomes crushed | Use semi-vacuum settings or skip vacuum for fragile genetics |

## Verification Questions Before Jarring

1. Snap test passed across multiple plants?
2. Weight stabilized (daily loss <2%)?
3. 24-hour jar test: no condensation?
4. Aw or RH measurement device available?
5. Boveda 62 packs ready (sized to volume)?
6. Container method chosen (glass / Grove / Cannatrol / Mylar)?
7. Burp schedule planned and calendar-ready?
8. Long-term storage destination prepared?
9. Strain notes captured for cure expectations (timeline, target)?
10. Disease pressure cleared (no Botrytis carry-over from harvest/dry)?
11. Cure environment ready (cool, dark, stable temp 60–68°F)?
12. Tracking system ready (weights at jarring, after 4 weeks, after long-term)?

If 4+ unknown → don't jar yet. Get the data and the plan.
