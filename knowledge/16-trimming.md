# 16 — Trimming

Trimming is the labor-intensive bottleneck of the harvest pipeline. Good trimming protects flower quality, separates usable sugar trim from waste, and keeps the handoff to cure clean. This file covers wet vs dry execution, hand-vs-machine tradeoffs, scissor and tool selection, ergonomics, trim material streams, and HLVd-conscious sanitation. Cross-reference with `13-harvest.md` (wet/dry trim fork decision happens at harvest), `14-drying.md` (dry-trim path executes between dry and cure), `15-curing.md` (post-trim handoff), `09-disease.md` (HLVd transmission via tools — every cut is a vector), and `state/grow-log.md` (trim weight tracking by stream).

## How to Use This File

1. **Confirm trim path** — wet (immediate post-harvest) or dry (after 7–14 day dry).
2. **Setup trim space + ergonomics** — proper-height table, lighting, trim tray with screens, breaks scheduled.
3. **Choose hand vs machine** — based on quality target, volume, labor budget, equipment availability.
4. **Execute with material stream separation** — 4 separate bins (A trim / B trim / fan / stem) for value recovery.
5. **Track yields** — wet trimmed weight, dry trimmed weight, trim ratio for grow-log.
6. **Sanitation between plants** — HLVd-conscious tool cleaning; glove changes.
7. **Hand off to cure** (wet path → `15-curing.md`) or storage (dry path complete).

## Critical Concept — The Trim Quality Spectrum

Trim style ranges from close and cosmetic to loose/natural. The right style depends on your intended use, dry/cure plan, and how much time you want to spend at the trim table.

| Trim style | Description | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Close / tight** | All sugar leaves removed close to calyxes; clean visual appearance; minimal sugar leaf retained | Best-looking jar flower; more time and handling |
| **Standard trim** | Most sugar leaves removed; some retained at calyx base | Balanced appearance, speed, and terpene retention |
| **Loose / "natural"** | Sugar leaves retained around calyxes; less cosmetic but may preserve more aroma during cure | Terpene-focused flower or personal preference |
| **Minimal/no trim ("popcorn")** | Lower-canopy buds left mostly as-is; large leaves removed | Solventless input, cooking input where legal, compost, or personal low-effort use |

**Trade-offs:**
- Cosmetic appeal (close trim wins) vs terpene retention (loose trim wins)
- Time cost (close trim can take much longer) vs appearance
- Intended use (jar flower → close/standard; solventless input → loose/minimal may be acceptable)

## Critical Concept — Wet vs Dry Trim Execution Differences

The wet-vs-dry decision was made at harvest (`13-harvest.md`). Each path executes differently:

| Aspect | Wet trim | Dry trim |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | Immediately after cut | After 7–14 day dry |
| Leaf state | Stiff, snip cleanly | Brittle, crumble, fall everywhere |
| Trim speed | Faster (~30–60 min/lb wet) | Slower (~60–90 min/lb dry; ~50% wet rate) |
| Material handling | Sticky resin coats scissors and gloves | Dust, tiny leaf fragments |
| Bud appearance after dry | Crisper, cleaner final look | Slightly more "natural" appearance |
| Sugar leaf separation | Easy — scissors cut at base cleanly | Harder — leaves brittle, edges crumble |
| Trim material destination | Wet/sticky — bag for bubble hash | Dry — direct edible/extract usable |

**Wet trim rates:** 30–60 min per pound wet (experienced; less for beginners)
**Dry trim rates:** 60–90 min per pound dry (~50% wet speed due to brittleness)

**Hard rule:** if Botrytis-affected plants → wet trim mandatory (cannot wait for dry; mold spreads in drying environment).

## Critical Concept — Trim Material Streams (the 4 Streams)

The trim process produces 4 distinct material streams. Separating streams helps even small home growers keep trichome-rich material away from compost/waste.

| Stream | Description | Trichome density | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A trim (sugar leaf, calyx-attached)** | Small leaves directly attached to calyxes; trichome-coated | Highest | Bubble hash, dry sift, rosin pressing where legal |
| **B trim (intermediate sugar)** | Sugar leaves slightly farther from calyx; less trichome density | Moderate | Compost, cooking input where legal, or lower-grade solventless input |
| **Fan leaves** | Large leaves from vegetation | Very low / none | Compost, mulch, or discard |
| **Stems** | Main stems and large branches | None | Compost or discard |

**Practical setup:** four bins per trim station, labeled clearly. Trim tray with kief-collection screen (100 micron typical) auto-separates trichomes during work — collect this kief weekly.

## HAND TRIM TECHNIQUES — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Wet Trim Execution

**When:** immediately after cut; chosen at harvest fork (`13-harvest.md`).

**Setup:**
- Trim space cleared, well-lit
- Trim tray with 100 micron kief screen
- 4 collection bins (A trim, B trim, fan, stem)
- 4–6 pairs of scissors ready to swap
- IPA (70%+) for scissor cleaning
- Nitrile gloves (multiple boxes; gets sticky fast)
- Drying rack ready for trimmed buds
- Scale for tracking

**Execute (branch-by-branch):**
1. Take cut branch, work over trim tray
2. **Remove fan leaves first** — large, easy to grab; pull or scissor-cut at petiole; bin in fan stream
3. **Remove stems and small branches** below the buds — bin in stem stream
4. **Trim sugar leaves around calyxes** — close trim for shake-tight, looser for craft
5. **A trim** (calyx-attached sugar) → A bin
6. **B trim** (less trichome-dense sugar) → B bin
7. Trimmed bud → drying rack within 2 hours
8. Move to next branch

**Speed:** 30–60 minutes per pound wet (experienced; ~2 grams per minute target).

**Equipment swap:** swap scissors every 30–45 minutes; soak used pair in IPA while using fresh.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Mixing material streams (lost value recovery)
- Dirty scissors (resin clogs slow cuts dramatically)
- Cutting too close to calyxes (trichome damage)
- Direct sun on trim work (terpene loss)
- Long sessions without breaks (quality drops at fatigue)

### Dry Trim Execution

**When:** after 7–14 day dry completes; snap test passed.

**Setup:** same as wet trim PLUS:
- Sharper scissors (brittle leaves crumble at dull edges)
- More care to avoid bud damage (dry buds delicate)
- Slower expectations (~50% wet trim rate)

**Pre-removal at hang time:** fan leaves often pre-removed during hang (when they're still slightly pliable) before they fully dry; saves crumbling problem.

**Execute (branch-by-branch):**
1. Take dried branch
2. Remaining fan leaves (if any) → fan bin (crumbled OK)
3. Stems and small branches → stem bin
4. **Sugar leaves trim with sharp scissors** — brittle, cleaner cut with sharper edge
5. Stream-separate A vs B trim
6. Trimmed bud → cure jar / Grove Bag / Cannatrol immediately
7. Move to next branch

**Speed:** 60–90 minutes per pound dry.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Crumbling sugar leaves (mix into bud — degrades final product)
- Buds shatter under pressure (dry trichomes brittle; gentle handling)
- Cure container not ready (don't leave dry-trimmed buds exposed long)
- Dust/fragment cleanup needed at end of session

### Close / Tight Trim

**When:** visual appearance matters and you are willing to spend more time per bud.

**Technique:**
- Trim sugar leaves flush with calyx surface
- Curved scissor tip follows calyx contour
- Sub-mm precision on visible bud surfaces
- Leave nothing extending beyond calyx outline
- Each bud individually inspected post-trim

**Speed:** ~50% slower than standard trim.

**Risk:** trichome damage from over-aggressive close cuts (calyx surface trichomes most concentrated).

**Best for:** best-looking jar flower and gifts/shared personal flower where legal.

### Loose / "Natural" Trim

**When:** terpene-focused flower, solventless input, or lower-labor personal trimming.

**Technique:**
- Remove only fan leaves and obvious large sugar leaves
- Leave smaller calyx-adjacent sugar leaves intact
- Trichomes on retained sugar leaves preserve terpenes during cure
- Less cosmetic precision; more "natural" look

**Speed:** ~50–70% of standard trim time.

**Best for:** smoke quality / terpene priority; hash/extract destination (the retained sugar will be processed anyway).

## MACHINE TRIM — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Throughput Trimmer (Continuous-Feed Tumbler)

**Principle:** Buds fed into rotating drum or belt with helical blade system; trimmed buds exit one end, trim falls into collection chamber.

**Major brands and verified specs (Tier 2):**

| Model | Wet rate | Dry rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mobius M108S | 120 lbs/hr | 60 lbs/hr | Largest commercial throughput |
| Twister T4 SE | 70 lbs/hr | 14 lbs/hr | Mid-large commercial |
| Twister T2 | 23–78 lbs/hr | 7–16 lbs/hr | Variable by speed setting |
| Twister T-Zero PRO | 150 lbs/hr (with 5 people) | — | Top-end commercial line |
| Triminator Hybrid | 40 lbs/hr | 15 lbs/hr | Flex-Knife technology |

**Quality outcome:** independent lab testing comparing T-Zero PRO machine-trimmed flower to hand-trimmed flower found average difference only **0.6% THC** and **0.005% CBD** in favor of hand trim (Tier 2 — Twister Trimmer documentation). For most commercial purposes, machine trim quality is acceptable.

**Pros:**
- Massive throughput vs hand trim (40–150 lbs/hr vs 0.25–0.5 lbs/hr per hand trimmer)
- Consistent quality across batch
- Reduced labor costs at scale
- Conveyor / processing line integration

**Cons:**
- Capital cost ($5k–50k+ depending on model)
- Slight THC/CBD potency reduction (small but measurable)
- Trichome breakage from mechanical contact
- Machine maintenance required
- Quality below hand-trim premium

**Best fit:** mid-large commercial operations; B-grade flower; high-volume processing.

### Bowl Trimmer (Small Batch)

**Principle:** Buds loaded into salad-bowl-shaped chamber; brushes inside sweep flower across rotating blade underneath.

**Examples:** Trim Pro, GreenBroz Model M, various import brands.

**Capacity:** 1–4 oz per cycle; cycle time 5–15 minutes.

**Quality:** lower than throughput trimmer; bruising and shake loss common; cheapest mechanical option.

**Pros:**
- Lowest cost ($100–500)
- Simple operation
- 1 person operates 2–4 simultaneously
- Suitable for hobby + small commercial

**Cons:**
- Quality below throughput trimmer or hand trim
- Bruising / damage to delicate buds
- Shake loss higher than tumbler
- Not for premium product

**Best fit:** hobby; small farms; B-grade flower; outdoor/large volume where speed > quality.

### Tumble Trimmer (Rotating Drum with Blade)

**Principle:** Drum rotates with internal blade system; buds tumble through drum; trimmed buds exit; gentler than blade-direct contact systems.

**Examples:** various Twister and Triminator models use tumbler architecture.

**Capacity:** comparable to throughput trimmer; varies by drum size.

**Quality:** gentler than bowl trimmer; closer to hand-trim quality with proper technique.

**Pros:**
- Gentler on flower than bowl trimmer
- Higher throughput than bowl trimmer
- Many commercial models available

**Cons:**
- More expensive than bowl
- Smaller throughput than full continuous-feed
- Drum cleaning labor

### Bucking Machine (De-Stem)

**Principle:** Pulls buds off branches mechanically; pre-trim step in commercial workflow.

**Major brand specs (Tier 2 — verified):**

**Mobius MBX Bucker:**
- 150 lbs/hour capacity
- Cuts stems into smaller pieces during bucking (integrated stem chipper)
- 1 bag chipped stems = 20–25 bags unchipped (waste reduction)
- Industry's only fully-guarded cannabis bucker; CSA-compliant

**GreenBroz Original / 215:** comparable bucking capacity; manual feed.

**Workflow integration:** cut → buck → trim line → pack. Bucker removes branches efficiently before trim; trim line then processes only buds.

**Pros:**
- Massive throughput for de-stem step
- Reduces stem disposal volume (chipper)
- Frees hand labor for actual trim
- Required for commercial-scale workflow

**Cons:**
- Capital cost ($3k–15k)
- Requires structured workflow (cut → buck → trim sequence)
- Maintenance schedule
- Loud operation

**Best fit:** commercial operations >50 plants per harvest; trim-line-integrated facilities.

## SCISSOR / TOOL SELECTION

**Primary trim tool:** curved-tip pruning shears for fine work; straight-blade for general.

**Brand consensus among professional trimmers (Tier 2/3):**

**Chikamasa B-500SF (the gold standard):**
- Straight stainless steel blades with fluorine coating (sap-resistant)
- 155mm total length, 35mm blade, 42g weight
- Elastomer handles
- Field-tested by US OSHA inspectors
- Users report 3.5 hours of trimming before thorough scissor cleaning required (vs ~30–45 min for non-coated)
- Sharper and more durable than competing brands
- "Highest quality secateurs in agricultural/horticultural industry" reputation

**Other brands:**
- Fiskars Micro-Tip (good budget option)
- Felco pruners (general use; less precise than Chikamasa)
- Generic curved-tip trim scissors (cheap, replace frequently)

**Razor blades / single-edge knives:** for stem work and fine precision (some pros use these for the entire trim).

**Sharpening + maintenance:**
- Keep blades sharp; resin buildup slows cuts dramatically
- Soak in IPA 5–15 min when resin builds
- Wipe with clean cloth; dry before reuse
- Replace dull scissors (sharpening rarely worthwhile for cheap brands)

**Spare scissors workflow:**
- Rotate every 30–45 minutes
- Soak used pair in IPA while using fresh
- Have 4–6 pairs ready per trim station
- Chikamasa's fluorine coating extends rotation interval to 3–4 hours

## ERGONOMICS & WORKFLOW

**The carpal tunnel reality** (Tier 1 — CDC/NIOSH evaluation, 2015): a 2015 NIOSH evaluation of a Washington state cannabis farm concluded that the grip used by trimmers may lead to carpal tunnel syndrome. Repetitive scissor grip + wrist position + sustained hours = real occupational injury risk.

**Setup for sustainable workflow:**
- **Seated** at proper-height table (elbows at 90°, shoulders relaxed)
- **Lighting from above + side** (no shadows on bud)
- **Trim tray with screen + collection chamber** (kief recovery)
- **Material bins** within easy reach (4 streams: A / B / fan / stem)
- **Hydration + snacks** stations
- **Music / podcast** queued (focus aid for long sessions)

**Break schedule:**
- **Every hour: 5–10 minute break** with hand stretches
- **Every 2 hours: 15 minute break** with full body stretching
- **Every 4 hours: 30 minute meal break**
- **Maximum 6–8 hour shift** before quality drops noticeably

**Hand fatigue prevention:**
- Pay attention to grip tightness (loosest grip that controls scissors)
- Keep wrist neutral (not flexed up or down)
- Alternate hands periodically if ambidextrous-tolerable
- Stretches: wrist flexion, finger extension, thumb stretches, forearm rolls
- Nerve-gliding exercises (median nerve)

**Repetitive strain injury (RSI) flags:**
- Tingling / numbness in fingers (especially thumb, index, middle)
- Pain at wrist or forearm
- Weakness gripping objects after work
- Symptoms persisting overnight
- → Stop, rest, consult medical; switching to machine trim if needed

## TRIM MATERIAL STREAMS — Detailed Workflow Integration

### Trim Tray Mechanics

**Standard setup:**
- Tray with **100 micron screen** (kief collection) over collection chamber
- Magnetic / static-resistant surface (some use tempered glass)
- Trichomes that fall through screen accumulate as kief in chamber
- Sweep weekly with credit card or stiff brush to consolidate

**Screen sizing for kief grades:**
| Micron | Use |
|---|---|
| 60–80μ | Initial sift (catches larger trichome heads) |
| 90–120μ | Mid-range standard kief |
| 130–160μ | Fine sift (smaller trichomes) |
| **100μ** | **Standard trim tray screen size — the default** |

### Bubble Hash Workflow (Wet Trim → Hash)

**Process (Tier 2/3 — multiple bubble hash protocol sources):**
1. A trim collected wet → bag for freezing
2. Freeze A trim 24+ hours (cold makes trichomes brittle)
3. Bubble bag stack: 25μ to 220μ progressive screens in 5-gallon bucket
4. Add ice + frozen trim + cold water; agitate (washing machine, paddle, or hand mix)
5. Trichomes break off, sink, separate by size through screens
6. Collect hash from each bag (220μ = larger trichome heads; 25μ = finest)
7. Press / dry → bubble hash

**Yields (rough):** 1 oz of decent A trim → ~3–7 g bubble hash typical.

### Dry Sift Workflow (Dry Trim → Kief/Hash)

**Process:**
1. A trim or B trim dry → freeze 24+ hours
2. Stack of dry sift screens (60–160μ progressive)
3. Rub frozen trim across screens; trichomes drop through
4. Collect kief from each tier
5. Press → dry sift hash

### Rosin Pressing

**Process:**
- Sugar trim → rosin press at controlled temp/pressure
- Solventless concentrate (no chemicals)
- Higher quality than bubble hash for some uses

### Edible / RSO Destinations

**B trim and lower-grade material:**
- Decarboxylate (heat to convert THCA → THC)
- Infuse into oil / butter / alcohol (RSO)
- Use in edibles, topicals
- Lower trichome density acceptable for these destinations

## LARGE HARVEST WORKFLOW (Home-Grow Safe)

**Workflow staging:**
1. **Cut** — branches from the plant; move cleanly to the trim/dry area
2. **Buck or branch-hang** — remove buds from branches only if that matches the wet/dry path
3. **Sort** (optional) — top buds vs lowers/popcorn for different trim effort
4. **Trim** — hand trim, small-batch trimmer, or hybrid approach based on volume and quality goal
5. **Quality check** — sample jars/bags for leafiness, moisture feel, and contamination
6. **Pack** — direct to cure containers or to drying for dry-trim path

**Trim room environment** (Tier 2):
- Temp 60–70°F
- RH 44–55%
- Good ventilation (resin volatile buildup)
- Dim lighting (UV-resistant)
- Clean surfaces (HLVd-conscious)

**Time planning:**
- Hand trim can take hours even for a small harvest; block time before buds over-dry.
- Small-batch trimmers can reduce time but usually trade away some appearance and trichome preservation.
- Hybrid approach: quick rough trim, then hand-finish the best flower.

**Bottleneck reality** (per `13-harvest.md`): trimming often becomes the harvest bottleneck. Plan trim time before chop day, not after.

## SANITATION (HLVd-Conscious)

(Cross-ref `09-disease.md` for full HLVd protocol.)

Every cut is a potential HLVd transmission vector. For shared tools, mother plants, or multi-plant grows:

- **Glove changes between plants** (or per-batch in high-throughput)
- **Scissor IPA wipe between plants** (10% bleach soak 10 minutes for HLVd-grade sanitation; longer wipe-down for between-batches)
- **Trim bin cleaning** between batches/plants when disease risk exists
- **Trim tray cleaning** between sessions
- **Work-surface cleanup** to keep the area clean and reduce cross-contamination
- **Simple sanitation notes** in the grow log when disease/HLVd risk exists

**Hard rule:** if mother room contains HLVd-positive plants, those tools never touch HLVd-negative plants without thorough sterilization.

## Quick Reference — Trim Method Selection Tree

```
START → Trim path?
├── Wet trim (from harvest decision) → Hand trim immediately
├── Dry trim (from harvest decision) → Wait 7-14 days, then trim
└── Botrytis affected → MUST be wet trim immediately (mold spread risk)

VOLUME?
├── < 1 lb dry per harvest → Hand trim only (ROI doesn't justify machine)
├── 1-10 lbs per harvest → Bowl trimmer optional; hand trim still viable
├── 10-50 lbs per harvest → Throughput trimmer (Twister T2/T4, Triminator) + hand finishing
├── 50+ lbs per harvest → Throughput trimmer (Mobius M108, T-Zero PRO) + bucker integration

QUALITY TARGET?
├── Best-looking jar flower → Hand trim, close style, sharp scissors
├── Standard personal flower → Hand trim or hybrid (machine + hand finish)
├── Lower buds/popcorn → Loose trim or small-batch machine trim
└── Solventless/cooking input where legal → Loose/minimal trim

SCISSORS?
├── Pro / production volume → Chikamasa B-500SF (3.5 hr between cleaning)
├── Hobby / occasional → Fiskars Micro-Tip
└── Backup / spare → Generic curved-tip (rotate frequently)

TRIM MATERIAL?
├── A trim (calyx-attached, trichome-rich) → Bubble hash, rosin
├── B trim (intermediate sugar) → Compost, cooking input where legal, or lower-grade solventless input
├── Fan leaves → Compost
└── Stems → Compost or chipped disposal
```

## Common Mistakes & Misdiagnosis Traps

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Cutting too close to calyxes | Trichome loss; calyx damage | Pull back; calyx surface is most trichome-dense |
| Cutting too loose / not enough | Extra leaf in cure, harsher final feel | Trim more if jar appearance/smoothness matters |
| Mixing material streams | Good sugar trim gets mixed with waste | Use separate labeled bins |
| Dirty scissors | Slow cuts, fatigue, trichome stripping | Rotate every 30-45 min; soak in IPA |
| No HLVd protocol | Cross-contamination across plants | Glove changes + scissor sterilization between plants |
| Long sessions without breaks | Quality drops at fatigue + RSI risk | Hourly breaks; max 6-8 hour shifts |
| Wet trim into bins with no airflow | Mold within hours | Move to drying within 2 hours of cut |
| Direct sun on trim work | Terpene loss, color degradation | Indoor or shaded work area only |
| Crumbling sugar leaves into bud (dry trim) | Degrades final product | Pre-remove fans at hang time; sharper scissors for dry sugar |
| Bowl trimmer expecting hand-trim quality | Disappointed appearance | Bowl trimmer = speed tool; hand-trim best flower |
| Machine trim of best flower | More handling and possible appearance/trichome loss | Hand trim best flower; machine trim lowers if needed |
| No kief screen on trim tray | Lost trichome value | 100μ screen standard; sweep weekly |
| Trim tray sun-exposed | Kief degradation | Indoor work; opaque collection chamber |
| RSI / carpal tunnel ignored | Worker injury, long-term harm | Stretches, breaks, ergonomic intervention; switch to machine if persistent |

## Verification Questions Before Trimming

1. Trim path confirmed (wet from harvest OR dry post-drying)?
2. Trim space prepared (lighting, trays, bins, scissors, gloves, scale)?
3. Hand vs machine decision matches volume + quality target?
4. Material stream separation system ready (4 bins labeled)?
5. Kief collection setup (trim tray with 100μ screen)?
6. HLVd sanitation protocol (gloves + scissor IPA between plants)?
7. Ergonomic setup (proper height, lighting, breaks scheduled)?
8. Time blocked appropriately (~30-90 min per pound depending on path)?
9. Trim destination ready (cure containers for wet path, storage for dry)?
10. Yield tracking (weights at trim stage for grow-log)?
11. Scissors ready (4-6 pairs, sharp, IPA bath for rotation)?
12. Trim material destinations planned (hash, edibles, compost streams)?

If 4+ unknown → don't start trimming yet. Get the data and the plan.
