# 14 — Drying

Drying is where most irreversible quality damage happens. Cure can recover from imperfect dry to a degree, but a botched dry locks in hay smell, terpene loss, harshness, and case hardening that no amount of curing can undo. The single most-cited piece of post-harvest wisdom — "you can't fix a bad dry in cure" — is grounded in chlorophyll degradation kinetics: chlorophyll breakdown happens during dry on a specific timeline; rushing it stalls the process and leaves grass/hay smell locked in. Cross-reference with `13-harvest.md` (drying environment must be ready before the cut), `15-curing.md` (this file's output is cure's input — moisture content matters), `16-trimming.md` (dry-trim path executes after this file completes), `09-disease.md` (Botrytis can appear post-cut during dry; Aspergillus and Penicillium emerge from improper dry environments), `02-environment.md` (dehumidifier sizing, environmental control specs).

## How to Use This File

1. **Confirm drying space ready** — RH/temp/airflow tested with hygrometer 24–48h before cut.
2. **Match method to harvest path** — wet-trim → rack dry; dry-trim → hang whole/branches; premium → Boveda chamber or Cannatrol; specialty → freeze dry.
3. **Load plants/branches/buds** with proper spacing (4–6 inches; airflow critical).
4. **Monitor daily** — hygrometer reading + visual + smell + handling check.
5. **Snap test from day 5–7** — small stems should snap, not bend.
6. **Transition to cure when ready** — don't rush; don't over-dry.
7. **Hand off to `15-curing.md`** when snap test passes and weight has stabilized.

## Critical Concept — What Drying Actually Does

Drying isn't "removing water" — it's a coordinated set of biochemical transitions that happen on a specific timeline driven by environmental conditions.

**The biochemistry:**
- **Water loss** — moisture moves from interior bud tissue outward to dry air via vapor diffusion
- **Chlorophyll degradation** — enzymatic breakdown of green pigment; this is THE process that converts "fresh-cut grassy" to "ready-to-cure"; proceeds at 60–68°F; halts below 55°F or above 75°F
- **Sugar conversion** — starches break down to simpler compounds; affects burn quality and smoke smoothness
- **Limited bacterial activity (early dry)** — small amount of biological activity in first 24–48 hours breaks down cell-wall sugars
- **Terpene volatilization** — terpenes evaporate continuously from cut surfaces; faster at higher temps; this is the loss to minimize
- **Cannabinoid stability** — THCA, CBDA, CBGA largely stable through proper dry; decarboxylation (THC, CBD active forms) doesn't significantly happen until heat applied (combustion or vaping)

**Why this matters operationally:**
- Drying is biochemistry, not just dehydration
- The hay-smell → cure-ready aroma transition is chlorophyll breaking down on a timeline that environmental conditions either enable or block
- Rushing the dry stalls chlorophyll degradation → permanent hay smell
- Curing **does not reverse a rushed dry** (Tier 3 — universal grower consensus, supported by chlorophyll degradation kinetics): flower that smells like hay after drying rarely comes back later

(Tier 1 background — Sutton et al. on chlorophyll degradation in plant tissue; Caplan/Zheng general post-harvest research; Tier 3 — Cervantes, Rosenthal drying chapters as primary practical sources.)

## Critical Concept — Slow & Even Wins (the Master Rule)

Two principles drive all drying-method decisions:

**Slow:** longer drying time = more chlorophyll breakdown = better final product
- Standard: 7–14 days
- Premium: 14–21 days
- Commercial fast-dry: 3–5 days (sacrifices quality for throughput)
- Freeze dry: 1–2 days (preserves quality through different physics — sublimation)

**Even:** uniform moisture loss across each bud prevents case hardening
- Achieved via controlled RH + gentle airflow + appropriate temp
- The bud must lose 60–75% of water content over the dry period
- Lose it too fast = hay smell + terpene loss + harshness + case hardening
- Lose it too slow = mold risk

**The three failure modes:**

| Failure | Cause | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Too fast** | RH too low (<45%), temp too high (>70°F), direct airflow | Hay smell locked in; terpene loss; case hardening; harsh smoke |
| **Too slow** | RH too high (>65%), no airflow, cool stagnant room | Mold (Botrytis, Aspergillus, Penicillium); total loss possible |
| **Uneven** | Poor airflow distribution; some plants in still air zones | Some buds dry, others still wet; per-plant inspection required; rotate positions |

## Critical Concept — Environmental Setpoints (the 60/60 Rule)

| Parameter | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **RH** | **55–62%** | The single most critical variable; below 45% = case hardening risk; above 65% = mold risk |
| **Temp** | **60–68°F (15–20°C)** | Cooler = slower but better quality; below 55°F halts chlorophyll breakdown; above 75°F damages trichomes |
| **Airflow** | Gentle, indirect | No direct fan on plants; leaves should NOT visibly flutter |
| **Light** | Dim or dark | UV degrades cannabinoids; some operations use blacked-out rooms |
| **Air exchange** | Light, periodic | Stale anaerobic air = mold; balance with terpene retention |
| **Duration** | 7–14 days standard | Premium 14–21 days; commercial fast-dry 3–5 days (quality compromise) |

**The "60/60 rule"** (Tier 3 — community-standard, widely-cited): 60°F and 60% RH is the gold-standard slow dry. Cannabis dries over 7–14 days under these conditions, balancing chlorophyll breakdown, terpene retention, and mold prevention.

**Hard rule:** RH below 30% will case-harden every bud, every time. RH above 70% sustained will produce mold within days. Between those extremes, lean toward slower (60% RH range) for quality.

## DRYING METHODS — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Hang Dry Whole Plant

**Principle:** Cut whole plant at base, hang upside-down by stem in controlled environment. Slowest method; best terpene retention.

**Environment:** 60°F / 60% RH; gentle indirect airflow; dark room.

**Equipment:** drying lines or rack with clamps; sufficient ceiling height for plant length; dehumidifier sized for room.

**Timeline:** 10–14 days standard; 14–21 days premium.

**Quality outcome:** highest terpene retention; best chlorophyll breakdown; classic craft dry.

**Pros:**
- Slow, even drying
- Terpene preservation
- Time-tested
- Minimal equipment

**Cons:**
- Requires hanging space (vertical clearance)
- Dry-trim path only (or rough wet-trim then hang)
- Slower throughput

**Common pitfalls:**
- Plants too close → uneven dry
- Floor-level airflow blocked → bottom buds dry slower
- Light leak through tent zippers → cannabinoid degradation

### Hang Dry Branches

**Principle:** Cut into 1–3 foot branches, hang individually. Same as whole-plant but more manageable.

**Environment:** Same as whole-plant (60/60).

**Equipment:** drying lines with clamps; less vertical clearance needed than whole-plant.

**Timeline:** 7–10 days (slightly faster than whole-plant due to smaller mass).

**Quality:** very close to whole-plant; minor terpene loss from extra cut surfaces.

**Pros:**
- More flexible space requirements
- Easier to inspect individual branches
- Easier rotation for even drying

**Cons:**
- More cuts = more terpene loss surfaces
- Slightly faster dry than ideal

### Rack Dry (Mesh Trays)

**Principle:** Wet-trim path standard. Trimmed buds laid horizontally on mesh racks. Fast, high-throughput.

**Environment:** 60–65°F / 55–60% RH (slightly warmer/drier than hang due to faster moisture loss); gentle airflow.

**Equipment:** stacked mesh racks; floor or table-mounted; oscillating fan in corner.

**Timeline:** 5–10 days (faster than hang due to wet-trim removing leaf moisture buffer).

**Quality outcome:** good but lower terpene retention than hang; commercial standard for wet-trim ops.

**Pros:**
- High throughput
- Less space per pound dried
- Easier visual inspection
- Scales to commercial volume

**Cons:**
- Lower terpene retention (sugar leaves removed early = less protection)
- Risk of crushing if buds touch
- Faster dry can locks in hay smell if RH too low

**Common pitfalls:**
- Buds too dense on rack → bottom layer doesn't dry
- Rotation skipped → uneven dry
- Direct fan → case hardening

### Boveda RH-Controlled Chamber

**Principle:** Sealed environment with humidity packs (Boveda 62%) maintaining specific RH automatically. Premium small-batch method.

**Environment:** Boveda 62% packs in sealed container; ambient temp determines exchange rate.

**Equipment:** sealed plastic tote or hard-sided cooler; multiple Boveda 62% packs sized to volume; hygrometer for verification.

**Timeline:** 10–14 days; slower due to controlled RH not letting moisture escape rapidly.

**Quality outcome:** very consistent RH = very consistent dry. Terpene retention excellent.

**Pros:**
- Most consistent RH of any method
- Set-and-forget after initial loading
- Excellent terpene preservation
- Scales to small premium batches

**Cons:**
- Limited capacity per chamber
- Boveda pack cost (replace as exhausted)
- Requires sealed container; not for high-volume
- Initial moisture content must be right (very wet plants overload Boveda)

(Tier 2 — Boveda technical; ASTM standards 55–65% RH ideal; Boveda 62 specifically developed in response to cannabis cultivator demand for cure-target RH.)

### Cannatrol (Controlled Cabinet — Vaportrol Technology)

**Principle:** Temperature + humidity (via dew point control) precisely managed in a sealed cabinet. Combines drying and curing automatically.

**Environment (Tier 2 — Cannatrol official specs):**
- **Drying phase:** 4 days at 68°F temp / 53°F dew point
- **Curing phase:** 4 days at 68°F / 52°F dew point
- **Storage phase:** 68°F / 53°F dew point indefinite

**Equipment:** Cool Cure cabinet — 28.25"H × 17.75"W × 20.5"D, 60 lbs, 2.49 cu ft internal capacity; holds 4–5 lbs dry flower / wet load max 2.2 lbs; 30W average power draw.

**Timeline:** 4 days dry + 4 days cure = 8 days total controlled-environment processing.

**Quality outcome:** consistent results; preserves terpenes well; commercial trend among premium operations.

**Pros:**
- Automated environmental control via Vaportrol patented system
- Combines dry + cure in one unit
- Repeatable results across batches
- Smaller footprint than dedicated dry room

**Cons:**
- Equipment cost ($2k–10k+ depending on model)
- Limited capacity per unit (Cool Cure: 2.2 lbs wet at a time)
- Requires power 24/7 during dry/cure
- Black-box approach loses some of the craft "feel-it-out" element

**Best fit:** premium home growers, small craft operations, R&D facilities. Larger commercial systems also offered.

### Freeze Dry (Lyophilization)

**Principle:** Sublimation — frozen water transitions directly to vapor at low pressure. Bypasses the liquid water phase entirely.

**The three phases:**
1. **Freezing** — material rapidly frozen to lock in volatile compounds
2. **Primary drying (sublimation)** — pressure lowered, slight heat added, frozen water sublimates
3. **Secondary drying (desorption)** — residual bound moisture removed

**Environment:** vacuum chamber; 75°F maximum temperature setting (trichomes damaged at higher); pressure varies by phase.

**Equipment (Tier 2 — Harvest Right, Cryo Cure, Original Resinator):**
- Harvest Right Scientific Model with DigiVac vacuum bleed controller (cannabis-specific recommended)
- Cryo Cure dedicated cannabis freeze dryer
- Original Resinator with lyophilization SOP
- Equipment cost: $5k–50k+

**Timeline:** 1–2 days total processing.

**Quality outcome:** best terpene + cannabinoid preservation of any method; preserves volatile compounds that traditional dry loses.

**Pros:**
- Fastest method (1–2 days vs 7–14)
- Best terpene/cannabinoid preservation
- Prevents mold/insect contamination via low temp
- Used for premium hash/extract destinations

**Cons:**
- Capital cost ($5k–50k+ for production-quality units)
- Operational complexity (vacuum, electrical)
- Doesn't develop the same flavor profile as slow-cured (chlorophyll breakdown different)
- Specialty technique; not for everyday production

**Best fit:** specialty operations, hash/concentrate destinations, premium small batches where terpene retention is paramount.

### Outdoor / Shed Drying

**Principle:** Hang plants in barn, shed, or tent in ambient outdoor conditions. Cheap; high-risk; climate-dependent.

**Environment:** ambient outdoor (depends on harvest season climate).

**Equipment:** drying lines or racks; tarp/cover for weather protection.

**Timeline:** highly variable (5–20+ days depending on climate).

**Quality outcome:** highly variable; can be excellent (cool dry mountain climate) or disastrous (humid lowland fall).

**Pros:**
- Free
- Scales to large outdoor harvests
- Traditional method

**Cons:**
- High mold risk in humid climates
- Pest exposure (insects, rodents, birds)
- Variable conditions = inconsistent quality
- Theft risk
- Not legally permitted in many jurisdictions even where cannabis legal

**Best fit:** outdoor harvests in arid climates; emergency dry when no infrastructure available.

## THE SNAP TEST — Readiness Criteria

Multiple signals confirm dry completion. Don't rely on any single one.

**Primary signals:**
- **Small stems (pencil-thickness) snap when bent** — the canonical test
- **Bud surface "crunchy"** but interior should be slightly springy
- **Larger main stems may still flex slightly** — that's fine; small branches are the test
- **Buds feel light** — water has departed; weight is much lower than wet

**Secondary signals:**
- **Smell transition** — hay/grassy → terpene-forward as chlorophyll breaks down (this is gradual; cure completes the transition)
- **Visual leaf curl** — sugar leaves curl tightly toward bud as moisture leaves
- **Weight stabilization** — daily weight loss <2% indicates approaching equilibrium with environment

**Test multiple plants and bud sites** — uneven dry is common; one snap-test passing doesn't mean all plants are ready.

**Failure mode — case hardening false-positive:** snap test passes but cure jars produce condensation within 24h. The bud was dry on the OUTSIDE only. See Case Hardening section below.

**When the snap test passes and weight has stabilized → transition to cure (cross-ref `15-curing.md`).**

## EQUIPMENT — Sizing and Setup

### Dehumidifier Sizing for Drying Rooms

**Formula approaches:**
- **Cubic feet × 0.75** = required pints per day capacity (rough estimate)
- **0.5–2 pints per day per square foot of canopy** (more accurate for active grow rooms; drying rooms slightly less)
- **For drying specifically:** fresh cannabis is 75–80% water by weight; calculate moisture release based on wet harvest weight + drying duration

**Sizing safety margin:** size 25–30% above calculated load to handle peak conditions. If room produces 40 PPD under normal conditions, choose 50–55 PPD unit.

**AHAM DH-1 standard:** dehumidifier ratings are tested at 80°F / 60% RH; real-world performance at 60°F / 60% RH is **lower** than rated. Typical de-rating: ~50% capacity at 60°F. Size accordingly.

(Tier 2 — Hydrobuilder, Quest Climate, Preair commercial dehumidifier sizing technical resources.)

### AC for Temperature Management

In warm climates, AC required to hold 60–68°F drying room temp. Sizing depends on room volume + heat load (lighting, equipment, ambient).

### Hygrometer Placement

- **At canopy level** primary placement
- **Multiple locations** (corners differ; near intake/exhaust differ)
- **Calibrate** with salt-method or factory-calibrated reference
- **Cheap pens lie** — Bluelab, Sensirion, or similar quality units worth the investment

### Drying Rack/Lines

- **Spacing 4–6 inches** between plants/branches for airflow
- **Height accommodates** plant length (pre-flip plants 2–3 feet need 4+ feet hanging room)
- **Material** — non-galvanized (galvanized off-gases zinc); cotton string, plastic-coated wire, or stainless

### Air Movement

- **Oscillating fan in corner** of room — gentle ambient circulation
- **NOT pointing at plants** — direct airflow causes uneven dry
- **Sealed room preferred** (allows RH control); tent works if dehumidifier sized correctly

## BUD WASHING (Pre-Dry Rinse) — Debated Practice

**Method:** dunk plants/branches in cold water bath before hanging. Optional additives (3% hydrogen peroxide at 10–12.5 ml per liter, or baking soda + lemon juice).

**Claim:** removes dust, debris, residues, surface mold spores, IPM treatment residues.

**Evidence:**
- **Trichomes are oil-based and hydrophobic** — water doesn't dissolve or dislodge them (Tier 2/3 — multiple bud-washing protocol sources)
- Bud washing has no documented negative effect on flavor or potency in mild rinses
- No peer-reviewed cannabis-specific studies on quality outcome
- Widely practiced for outdoor / IPM-treated plants

**When useful:**
- Outdoor plants with visible dust, debris, insects
- Indoor plants treated with IPM products near harvest (residue removal)
- Smoke-quality concerns (some growers report cleaner ash)

**Risks:**
- If drying environment imperfect, washing adds moisture and increases mold risk
- Trichomes shake off if vigorous agitation (bubble hash technique)
- Submerging poorly-shaken plants → bottom buds soaked

**Honest framing:** reasonable practice with some logic; not mandatory; not for poorly-controlled drying environments. If you wash, ensure drying space is dialed in (RH 55–60%, gentle airflow, immediate hang).

**Standard protocol:**
1. Three buckets: clean water + 3% H₂O₂ at 10–12.5 ml/L → clean water rinse → clean water rinse
2. Dunk 30 seconds in each
3. Gentle swirl, no aggressive shake
4. Hang immediately for water shake-off (15–30 min) before formal drying
5. Move to standard drying room

## COMMON DRY FAILURES & RECOVERIES

| Failure | Cause | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Hay smell at end of dry** | Drying too fast (RH low, temp high); chlorophyll didn't break down | Limited recovery via long burp cure (4–8+ weeks); often permanent |
| **Mold (Botrytis, Aspergillus, Penicillium) during dry** | RH too high; poor airflow; contaminated material | Isolate affected; trash; lower RH and run airflow on remaining; do NOT mix moldy with healthy |
| **Over-dry (brittle, harsh)** | RH too low; dry too long; low ambient | Cautious rehydration via Boveda 62% packs in sealed jar; doesn't fully recover |
| **Case hardening (outside dry, inside wet)** | RH too low + direct airflow + temp too high | Back to dry environment with higher RH (60%); re-equilibrate over days; some quality loss permanent |
| **Uneven dry across plants** | Poor airflow distribution | Rotate positions every 2–3 days; check airflow at all canopy points |
| **Strong "wet plant" smell never transitioning** | Dry too slow + temp too low | Raise temp slightly within range (62–66°F); increase airflow gently |
| **Buds smell "off" / chemical** | Dehumidifier exhaust contamination; carbon filter needed; poor drying space | Move to clean space; ventilate; carbon filter |
| **Trichomes look gray/dull** | Light exposure during dry | Block all light; salvage what can be salvaged |
| **Smell weak after dry** | Terpene loss from too-fast or too-warm dry | Permanent loss; learn for next time |

## CASE HARDENING — Specific Failure Mode

The single most diagnostic-worthy failure mode in cannabis drying. Deserves its own section.

**What it is:** Outer bud tissue (calyxes, sugar leaves) dries faster than interior bud mass. Outside feels dry by snap test, inside still has 15–25%+ moisture.

**Cause (the moisture gradient):**
- Surface evaporation outpaces internal moisture migration outward
- Created by RH too low (<45%), temp too high (>70°F), direct airflow on plants, or combination

**Detection:**
- **Snap test passes** but **cure jars produce condensation within 24 hours** of jarring
- Buds feel hard/crunchy outside but compress oddly when squeezed
- Mold risk in cure (interior moisture has nowhere to go)

**Prevention:**
- Maintain 55–62% RH consistently (don't dip below 45% even briefly)
- Temp 60–68°F
- Indirect airflow only — never direct fan on plants
- Minimum 7-day dry; faster than that risks gradient

**Recovery:**
- Open jars more frequently in early cure (every 2–4 hours instead of 1–2× daily for first 3 days)
- Or: move buds back to controlled drying environment with higher RH (60–62%) for 3–5 days
- Re-equilibrate; then re-jar
- Some quality loss (terpene migration, cannabinoid degradation in trapped moisture areas) is permanent

**Hard rule:** never jar buds that pass snap test alone. Verify with weight stabilization + multiple bud-site tests. False positives lead to ruined cures.

## Quick Reference — Drying Method Selection Tree

```
START → Goal?
├── Maximum quality, time available → Hang dry whole/branches at 60/60 (10-14 days)
├── Premium small batch, set-and-forget → Boveda chamber OR Cannatrol
├── Commercial high-throughput, wet-trim path → Rack dry (5-10 days at 60-65°F / 55-60% RH)
├── Specialty / hash destination → Freeze dry (1-2 days lyophilization)
├── Outdoor harvest, no equipment → Shed dry (high-risk; weather-dependent)
└── Botrytis-affected material → Wet-trim immediately + careful rack dry isolated

EQUIPMENT?
├── Dehumidifier sized to room (cubic ft × 0.75 = PPD; +30% safety margin)
├── AC for temp control if warm climate
├── Multiple hygrometers (corners + canopy level)
├── Drying lines/racks 4-6 inches spacing
└── Oscillating fan in corner (NOT on plants)

VERIFY READINESS?
├── Snap test (pencil-thick stems snap, not bend)
├── Weight stabilization (daily loss <2%)
├── Smell transition (hay → terps)
├── Multiple bud sites tested
└── No condensation in test jar within 24 hours
```

## Common Misdiagnosis Traps

| Looks like | Actually | How to tell |
|---|---|---|
| **Snap test passes, ready to cure** | **Case hardening** | Test in jar 24h; if condensation, back to dry |
| **"Hay smell"** | **Drying too fast** | Lower temp, raise RH slightly; usually irreversible |
| **Cure jars moldy** | **Inadequate dry before jarring** | Re-dry; lower starting moisture content |
| **Brittle and harsh** | **Over-dry** | Cautious rehydrate via Boveda; partial recovery only |
| **Drying takes forever (>14 days)** | **RH too high (>65%)** | Mold risk; dehumidifier capacity check |
| **Plants drying unevenly** | **Airflow distribution failure** | Rotate positions; check fan placement |
| **Bud washing left buds soaked** | **Insufficient airflow post-rinse** | Hang in active air for 15–30 min before formal dry |
| **Trichomes look gray/dull** | **Light exposure during dry** | Block all light; UV degrades cannabinoids |
| **Smell weak after dry** | **Terpene loss from too-fast or too-warm dry** | Slower dry next time; can't recover |
| **Mold appearing in dry room** | **RH too high** | Isolate, dispose, lower RH below 55% |
| **Stems still flexible after 14 days** | **RH still too high; dry incomplete** | Check hygrometer accuracy; extend dry; raise temp slightly |
| **Buds smell like dehumidifier exhaust** | **Equipment contamination** | Carbon filter; ventilate; replace dehumidifier filter |

## Verification Questions Before/During Drying

1. Drying space tested (RH and temp at canopy level with calibrated hygrometer)?
2. Drying method matching trim path (wet-trim → rack; dry-trim → hang)?
3. Hygrometer placement validated (multiple points, corners checked)?
4. Plants/branches spaced for airflow (4–6 inches apart)?
5. Light source controlled (dim or dark)?
6. Daily inspection schedule set?
7. Snap-test readiness criteria understood?
8. Cure containers ready (jars, Grove Bags, Boveda 62 packs)?
9. Botrytis risk assessment from harvest carry-over?
10. Dehumidifier sized correctly (capacity 25–30% above calculated load)?
11. AC available if warm climate?
12. Backup plan if dry environment drifts (RH spike or drop)?

If 4+ unknown → don't load the dry room yet. Get the data and the plan in place.
