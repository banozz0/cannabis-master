# 10 — Training

Training is shape and yield management — the physical manipulation of cannabis plants to maximize light interception, control height, and produce more usable flower per square foot. Every training technique works by exploiting the same biology: apical dominance suppresses lateral growth via auxin gradients, and disrupting that gradient (by cutting, bending, or redirecting growth) lets the plant develop multiple equal branches instead of one dominant cola. Cross-reference with `12-flowering.md` (training timing relative to flip; stretch math), `02-environment.md` (training is wasted if VPD/PPFD limit growth elsewhere), `03-lighting.md` (canopy uniformity matters because PPFD falls off inverse-square at edges), `01-genetics-strains.md` (sativa-leaning stretches more, indica branches tighter — different training response), `08-pests.md` and `09-disease.md` (defoliation reduces canopy moisture → lower disease pressure), `11-propagation.md` (clones from flowering mothers = monstercropping).

## How to Use This File

1. **Pick technique by goal + space + plant type** — no one-size-fits-all. Mainline + SCROG suits a 4×4 tent with 4 plants; SOG suits 16+ small plants in the same tent.
2. **Execute on a stage-appropriate schedule** — topping in early veg, LST throughout veg, defoliation at flip and week 3 of flower, nothing major after week 3 of flower.
3. **Allow recovery before next technique** — 5–7 days post-topping; 24–72 hours post-super-crop; 0–48 hours post-LST.
4. **Stop all HST 1+ week before flip** — give the plant time to rebuild its hormone gradient before the stretch demands all available energy.
5. **Use only LST + selective fan removal in flower** — buds don't grow back; a wrong cut in week 4 of flower stays wrong.
6. **Sterile cutting tools (HLVd!)** — every cut is a transmission point; see `09-disease.md`.

## Critical Concept — Apical Dominance: Why Training Works

Cannabis (like most plants) concentrates growth at the apical meristem — the top growing tip. Auxin (a plant hormone) produced at the apex flows downward, suppressing lateral bud development. The result without training: one tall main stem with weaker side branches.

Training works by manipulating this hormone gradient:

| Technique class | What it does to auxin | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Topping / FIM** | Removes the apical source entirely → auxin re-distributes to highest remaining buds | 2 (topping) or 4–6 (FIM) new co-equal apical mains |
| **LST** | Bends apex below the canopy plane → secondary buds become "highest" → they receive apical-equivalent auxin signal | All branches grow as if each were the main, flat horizontal canopy |
| **Super cropping** | Damages vascular tissue locally → auxin transport disrupted at the bend → secondary buds below get redirected hormone flow | Stronger, knotted regrowth; controls height + redistributes vigor |
| **SCROG** | Mechanical version of LST applied to many branches → enforces uniform apex height | Even canopy, all bud sites at same PPFD |
| **Defoliation** | Removes light-blocking leaves → lower buds receive more PPFD → higher photosynthesis at lower nodes | Better lower-bud development (debated — see schwazzing) |

**The plant rebuilds the apex over 5–7 days post-topping** — this is the recovery window. Training schedule design hinges on respecting these recovery times so the next technique starts on a fully-recovered plant.

## Critical Concept — HST vs LST Paradigm

Training techniques split into two stress categories. The right balance depends on time available, plant count, container size, and grower experience.

| Stress class | Examples | Recovery time | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| **HST (High Stress Training)** | Topping, FIM, mainlining cuts, super cropping, schwazzing, full lollipop | 5–14 days | Bigger structural change; growth pause during recovery; risk of mistakes |
| **LST (Low Stress Training)** | Bending, tying, plant yo-yos, gentle selective defoliation, screen weaving | 0–48 hours | Slower change; no growth pause; lower risk |
| **No training** | SOG, autoflowers grown straight up, mother plants kept productive | None | Simpler; maximizes per-plant cycle time; lower per-plant yield |

**Practical principle:** LST works *with* the plant; HST works *on* the plant. Both can produce yield gains. New growers should start with LST + one topping, learn the response, then add techniques. Commercial growers often run mainline + SCROG + lollipop as the standard stack.

## Critical Concept — Stage Timing & Recovery Windows

| Stage | Allowed techniques | Forbidden / risky |
|---|---|---|
| Seedling (week 1–2 from sprout) | Nothing — let the plant establish | Anything stressful — kills seedlings outright |
| Early veg (week 3–4) | First topping at 4th–5th node; gentle LST start at 3rd node | Heavy defoliation, super cropping (stems too tender) |
| Mid-late veg (week 5–8) | Mainline cuts, LST, SCROG screen install, super cropping, defoliation | Final HST cut <7 days before flip |
| Pre-flip (last week of veg) | Final LST adjustments, SCROG weave, light defoliation | All HST cutting (no recovery time before stretch) |
| Stretch (week 1–2 flower) | LST tucking, SCROG weave, light fan-leaf cleanup | Topping (you'll lose the new top buds during their formation) |
| Mid-flower (week 3) | **Schwazzing window 2** (if using), lollipop, fan-leaf cleanup | Anything cutting after this point |
| Late flower (week 4+) | Selective fan-leaf removal only if blocking direct light | All HST, heavy defol, training |

**Why "1 week before flip" matters:** the stretch period (first 2 weeks of flower) doubles to triples plant height. The hormone systems driving stretch need a stable gradient to work with — a topping cut in the last 3 days of veg sends conflicting signals, often producing uneven stretch and weaker top buds. Plan the cut at least 7 days before flip; 10 days is safer.

## Critical Concept — Strain & Plant Type Compatibility

| Plant type | Training compatibility | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Indica-dominant photoperiod** | Loves all training — bushy, recovers fast | Topping at 4th node, mainline-friendly, schwazz-tolerant |
| **Sativa-dominant photoperiod** | LST + topping but plan for stretch | Pure sativas can stretch 3× in flower; ceiling height critical |
| **Hybrid photoperiod** | Test on one plant first | Most modern hybrids (most retail genetics) respond well |
| **Autoflower** | LST + minimal selective defoliation ONLY | Cannot HST — fixed lifecycle; no recovery time |
| **Mother plants** | Light topping for clone harvest only | Goal is healthy long-term plant, not yield |
| **Photoperiod from clone** | Faster veg → tighter training schedule | Same techniques, compressed timeline |
| **Re-veg / monstercrop** | Wait for normal-leaf growth before training | First 4–8 weeks post-flower clones grow weird; don't cut yet |

**The autoflower exception is the single most important strain-type rule in this file.** Autoflowers have a fixed 70–110-day lifecycle and pre-flower regardless of size. They have ~3–4 weeks of veg total. A 5–7 day topping recovery is 25% of their veg life — usually a net yield loss. See the autoflower section below for the exception path.

## TOPPING TECHNIQUES — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Topping (Classic)

**What it is:** Cut the apical meristem (top growing tip), removing the dominant. The two side branches at the next node below take over as new apical mains.

**When to use:**
- Photoperiod plants in early veg
- After 4–5 nodes of vertical growth (typically week 3 from sprout)
- Sativa-leaning strains: top earlier (4th node) to control stretch
- Indica-leaning strains: top later (5th–6th node) to maintain bush density

**How to execute:**
1. Identify the 4th or 5th node (counting up from soil)
2. Cut just above the node, leaving ~5 mm of stem
3. Use sterile sharp scissors or razor blade — clean cut, not crushed
4. Remove only the top growth tip, leaving the leaves at that node intact

**Recovery time:** 5–7 days; first new growth visible 3–4 days; full recovery ~7 days.

**Result:** 1 main → 2 main colas; net flowering sites doubled; height suppressed.

**Pros:**
- Doubles main colas
- Reduces final height (helps with PPFD penetration)
- Standard technique, well-tolerated by most genetics

**Cons:**
- Recovery pause delays grow timeline by 5–7 days
- Wrong-node cuts can produce uneven branching
- Requires precision — crushed cuts heal slowly

**Common pitfalls:**
- Topping too early (<3rd node) → both side branches develop weak; better to wait
- Topping too late (close to flip) → no recovery window; uneven stretch
- Crushed cut from dull scissors → slow heal, infection entry
- Non-sterile tools → HLVd transmission risk (see `09-disease.md`)

### FIM ("F*** I Missed")

**What it is:** Partial topping — cut leaves ~20% of the apical meristem tissue intact. Variant of topping with less precision required, producing 4–6 new mains instead of 2.

**When to use:**
- Same timing as topping
- Faster than mainlining if you want 4+ colas without symmetric structure
- Less precise — useful for grower learning the cut

**How to execute:**
1. Identify the apical bud cluster
2. Pinch/cut 75–80% of the meristem tip, leaving ~20% intact
3. The remaining tissue heals into 4–6 separate growing tips

**Recovery time:** 5–10 days (slightly slower than clean topping due to ragged tissue).

**Result:** 1 main → 4–6 new mains; messier than topping but more colas per cut.

**Pros vs Cons vs topping:**
- Pro: more colas per cut (4–6 vs 2)
- Con: messier branching, often asymmetric
- Con: slightly slower recovery
- Best fit: growers who prefer one cut over multiple toppings; not ideal for SCROG (asymmetric)

### Mainlining / Manifolding

**What it is:** Symmetric repeat-topping protocol producing a uniform 8 (or 16) cola "manifold." Term **"Mainlining"** credited to grower **Nugbuckets**; **"Manifold"** variant credited to **Nebula Haze (Grow Weed Easy)** — produces 8 mains in 2 cuts instead of 3 (Tier 3 — primary source community consensus).

**When to use:**
- Any photoperiod plant where you want maximum uniform canopy
- 4–8 weeks of veg available
- Pairs perfectly with SCROG (the manifold lays flat through the screen naturally)
- Best for max yield per plant in a small grow

**How to execute (Nugbuckets 8-cola version, 3 cuts):**
1. **First top:** at 3rd node — cut just above the 3rd node, remove ALL growth below the 3rd node (lower branches and leaves come off; just keep the 3rd node and its 2 side shoots). The plant now looks like a "Y" — 2 branches.
2. **Recovery:** 5–7 days. Each side shoot becomes a main.
3. **Second top:** when each of the 2 mains has grown 3 nodes, top each above the 3rd node. Now you have 4 mains.
4. **Recovery:** 5–7 days.
5. **Third top (optional, for 8-cola):** repeat on each of the 4 mains. Now you have 8.
6. **LST:** train all 8 outward symmetrically while they grow — flat manifold ready for SCROG.

**Manifold (Nebula Haze) variant — 2 cuts for 8 mains:**
1. First top above 3rd node (same as Nugbuckets cut #1)
2. Wait for each branch to develop 3+ nodes
3. Top each above the 3rd node
4. The two side branches at THAT node + the original branch's growth = 8 total when laid out

**Recovery time per cut:** 5–7 days; total mainline cycle 4–6 weeks.

**Result:** Symmetric 8-cola manifold, uniform canopy, ideal for flat SCROG.

**Pros:**
- Most uniform canopy of any technique
- Maximum cola count without sacrificing individual cola size
- Pairs perfectly with SCROG

**Cons:**
- Time-intensive (4–6 weeks of veg required)
- Demands grower attention (symmetric cuts)
- Wrong-cut errors propagate through the manifold

**Common pitfalls:**
- Asymmetric cuts → uneven 8 mains → uneven canopy
- Skipping the "remove all growth below 3rd node" step on first cut → bottom-heavy plant, hard to LST
- Mainlining autoflowers → almost always net yield loss

## BENDING / SPATIAL TRAINING — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### LST (Low Stress Training)

**What it is:** Bend the main stem horizontal and tie it down. Side branches all become "tops" (each receives apical-equivalent auxin signal once the original apex is no longer highest).

**When to use:**
- Any photoperiod or autoflower plant (LST is the autoflower-safe technique)
- Start at 3rd node — when the plant has 3+ pairs of true leaves
- Throughout veg + first 2 weeks of flower (during stretch)

**How to execute:**
1. Soft plant ties (rubber-coated wire, plant velcro, soft string) — never bare wire on stems
2. Hold-down anchors: tent grommets, fabric pot edge, stake in pot
3. Bend the main stem ~90° down/sideways and tie at the bend point
4. As side branches grow up, tie those down too — keep canopy flat
5. Continue weekly throughout veg and stretch

**Recovery time:** 0–48 hours (gentle bending = effectively no recovery).

**Result:** Wide flat plant; all branches at same height; uniform PPFD across canopy.

**Pros:**
- Lowest-stress technique
- Compatible with autoflowers
- Combines with everything else
- No timeline cost

**Cons:**
- Slower visible change than HST
- Tying labor adds up across many plants
- Stems can break if bent too fast; gradual is the rule

**Common pitfalls:**
- Tying too tight → cuts circulation → branch dies (use soft ties, check weekly)
- Bare wire on stems → cuts into stem → pathogen entry
- Bending too fast → snap (gradual bend over 24–48 hours; pre-bend with finger pressure)
- Stopping LST mid-stretch → upper canopy explodes vertical, defeats purpose

### Super Cropping

**What it is:** Pinch and bend stem until inner tissue collapses but outer skin stays intact. Plant heals with knot of stronger tissue at the bend; auxin redirects to lower branches; height controlled.

**When to use:**
- Vigorous mid-late veg (stems flexible, plant strong)
- To save a too-tall plant pre-flip
- To redirect a dominant branch back to canopy plane
- NOT for autoflowers (HST — recovery time the autos don't have)

**How to execute (Tier 3 — community consensus on technique):**
1. Locate the bend point (typically just below where you want height controlled)
2. With thumb and forefinger, pinch the stem firmly and roll back-and-forth
3. The internal tissue collapses ("squish" feeling) but outer skin stays intact
4. Bend the stem at the pinched point to ~90°
5. If the outer skin tears, tape with electrical tape or plant tape and the plant heals through it

**Recovery time:** 24–72 hours; first sign of recovery (turgor returning) within 24 hours; full healing 5–7 days; "knuckle" hard knot forms within a week.

**Result:** Lowered canopy point; stronger stem at bend; redirected auxin → secondary buds get growth signal.

**Pros:**
- Powerful height control without removing growth
- Stronger plant overall (knot tissue more rigid than original stem)
- Works on woody mid-late-veg stems
- No tools required — fingers only

**Cons:**
- HST — recovery pause
- Wrong technique = snapped stem (use tape if torn)
- Not for tender early-veg stems
- Not for autoflowers

**Common pitfalls:**
- Pinching too hard / rolling too long → snap
- Bending without pinching first → snap
- Super cropping in early veg → stems too tender, snap
- Super cropping in flower → too late, no recovery

### SCROG (Screen of Green)

**What it is:** Install a horizontal screen above plants; weave branches through the screen as they grow; force all branches to flat canopy at screen height.

**When to use:**
- Photoperiod plants with 4–8 weeks of veg
- Any technique that creates multiple co-equal mains (mainline, topping, FIM)
- Mature plants ready to fill the screen
- Combined with topping or mainlining for best results

**How to execute:**
1. **Screen install:** 8–12 inches above the medium / plant base, before plants reach screen
2. **Material:** garden trellis netting (4–6 inch squares is typical), bamboo + string, or rigid metal grid
3. **Pre-flip:** when shoots reach 50–60% of screen height (or just penetrate the net), start tucking — pull each branch through the screen and weave it horizontally to fill empty squares
4. **Flip timing (Tier 3 — community consensus):** flip to 12/12 when screen is 50–75% full
   - 50% full = stretchy strain (sativa) — they'll fill the rest in stretch
   - 75% full = indica-leaning — less stretch, want more pre-filled
5. **Continue weaving** through stretch; stop tucking around day 10 of flower (buds too delicate to manipulate after that)

**Result:** Flat horizontal canopy at screen height; every cola in the same PPFD plane; uniform bud development.

**Pros:**
- Maximum light interception per plant
- Levels out per-cola yield (no popcorn lower buds)
- Combines with mainlining for the gold-standard hobby setup

**Cons:**
- Requires structural setup (screen + tent or framework)
- Hard to move plants once SCROG'd
- Demands attentive weaving for 3–4 weeks
- Flowering plants underneath the screen are effectively immobile

**Common pitfalls:**
- Screen too high → plants stretch above and you're back to vertical canopy
- Flipping with screen <50% full → SCROG benefit lost; vertical stretch dominates
- Flipping too late (>75% with indica) → screen overcrowded; airflow issue
- Forgetting to defoliate below screen → wasted energy on lower-canopy "popcorn"

### Trellis Layers (Flower-Stage Support)

**What it is:** 1–3 horizontal trellis nets stacked above the SCROG screen during flower. Provides bud support as flowers develop weight, prevents branch sag and snap.

**When to use:**
- Late stretch (week 2–3 of flower) when buds start to fatten
- Heavy-yielding strains where unsupported branches will snap
- Any commercial SCROG setup

**How to execute:**
1. First trellis layer: 8–12 inches above SCROG screen, install at flip
2. Second layer: 8–12 inches above first, install week 2 of flower
3. Third layer (optional): top of canopy by week 3
4. Tuck developing buds through each layer

**Result:** Branches supported through bud development; no snapping; clean canopy plane.

### SOG (Sea of Green)

**What it is:** Opposite philosophy from SCROG — many small plants, no training, single cola each. Faster cycle; less per-plant work; relies on plant count for total yield.

**When to use:**
- Commercial / multi-plant grows where space is more valuable than per-plant yield
- Fast-flowering indica genetics
- Tight cycle scheduling (continuous harvest setup)
- Small containers (1–2 gal) with short veg (1–2 weeks)

**How to execute:**
1. 12–16+ small plants per square meter
2. Veg only 1–2 weeks (just root establishment)
3. Flip to 12/12 immediately after
4. Each plant produces 1 single main cola
5. No training; harvest staggered for continuous flow

**Pros:**
- Fastest cycle time
- No training labor
- Scales linearly with plant count
- Great for commercial automation

**Cons:**
- Many plants = many transplants, many waterings
- Lower per-plant yield (intentional trade-off)
- Less flexibility per genetic variation

## DEFOLIATION & CANOPY MANAGEMENT — Per-Item Deep Profiles

### Selective Defoliation (Fan-Leaf Removal)

**What it is:** Ongoing removal of fan leaves that block light or airflow to bud sites. The safe default — every grower should be doing this throughout veg and into early flower.

**When to use:**
- Throughout veg + first 3 weeks of flower
- Whenever a fan leaf shades a future bud site
- When lower canopy isn't getting visible light penetration

**How to execute:**
- Snap or scissor-cut at petiole base
- Target: leaves directly shading bud sites; oversized fan leaves >9 inches across; leaves with damage / age
- Leave: smaller leaves directly attached to budding sites (these feed the bud)
- Pace: gradual; remove ~10–20% of fan leaves at a time, repeat in 5–7 days if more needed
- Sterile tools (HLVd!)

**Result:** Better light penetration to lower canopy; better airflow; reduced disease pressure.

### Schwazzing (Heavy Strategic Defoliation)

**What it is:** Aggressive defoliation at two specific points in flower. Originated in Joshua Haupt's *Three a Light* book (2015) — claims 3 lbs per 1000W light vs industry standard 1.5–2 lbs (Tier 3 — Haupt is the primary source; published trial data outside the book is limited).

**The protocol:**
1. **First schwazz:** at flip (day 1 of 12/12) — remove almost all fan leaves, leaving only the top 2–3 sets of leaves on each branch
2. **Second schwazz:** at week 3 of flower — repeat the heavy fan-leaf removal
3. After week 3: stop; only selective fan removal, no further heavy cuts

**Why people do it:**
- Massive light penetration to lower canopy
- Reduced humidity in canopy
- Reported yield gains in commercial trials (within Haupt's network)
- Bigger lower buds (less popcorn)

**Why it's controversial:**
- Independent peer-reviewed yield gains haven't replicated the *Three a Light* claim
- Heavy defoliation in flower stresses the plant during stretch (when it needs all leaves for stretch metabolism)
- Genetic-dependent — some strains tolerate, others stall significantly
- Recovery costs days of growth; net effect varies

**When NOT to use:**
- First-time growers (high risk if technique is wrong)
- Sativa-dominant or stretchy strains (already need leaves for stretch)
- Autoflowers (cannot tolerate)
- Stressed plants (any pre-existing issue compounds)
- New strain — test on one plant first

**Decision:** treat schwazzing as an experimental option for advanced growers with healthy indica-leaning genetics, not a default protocol. Start by trying it on a single plant in a multi-plant tent and compare A/B against your own past yields.

### Lollipopping

**What it is:** Remove the lower 25–33% of the plant's growth — branches and leaves that won't produce quality flower because they're too far below canopy PPFD to develop dense buds. Concentrates plant energy upward into the productive canopy.

**When to use:**
- At flip OR week 1 of flower (after stretch begins)
- Standard in commercial grows
- Photoperiod plants of any structure

**Why it works (Tier 1 — Folina et al. 2020, Fischedick et al. 2010):** cannabinoid concentrations are significantly higher in upper-canopy inflorescences than lower; lower-canopy "popcorn" buds produce less-potent flower. Removing them concentrates the plant's resources on the high-quality positions and reduces post-harvest trim labor.

**How to execute:**
1. Identify the canopy plane (where most of your buds will develop — the SCROG screen height, or the height of your dominant tops)
2. Mark a horizontal line ~1/3 down from canopy plane
3. Remove all branches and leaves below that line
4. Cut at the main stem cleanly
5. Lower-third buds and leaves all go

**Result:** Concentrated bud production at top canopy; less popcorn; less trim labor.

**Pros:**
- Backed by cannabinoid distribution research
- Reduces trim time at harvest
- Concentrates yield into high-quality positions
- Standard practice in commercial — lots of accumulated wisdom

**Cons:**
- Looks dramatic — beginners often hesitate
- Wrong-line cuts (too high) can sacrifice productive buds
- Combines with SCROG; less applicable to SOG (no lower canopy to remove)

## SPECIALTY / ADVANCED TECHNIQUES

### Monstercropping

**What it is:** Take a clone from a *flowering* mother plant. The clone re-vegs into a low-spreading bushy "monster" with weird leaf shapes (rounded single-finger leaves at first), eventually returning to normal leaf morphology. Photoperiod plants only — autoflowers can't be cloned this way.

**Why people do it:**
- Stretches a single mother's genetics across multiple cycles without keeping a vegetative mom
- Produces unusually bushy plants ideal for SCROG
- Useful in micro-grows where one heavy-yielding plant > many small plants

**How to execute:**
1. Select a lower branch from a flowering mother (3rd–6th week of flower works)
2. Cut diagonally across stem (max surface area for water uptake)
3. Remove all flower bud material (don't try to root flowers)
4. Strip lower leaves, root in cloning medium under 18+ hour photoperiod
5. Initial growth is weird — single-finger rounded leaves, ground-hugging form
6. Wait 4–8 weeks for normal vegetative growth resumption
7. Once normal, train as standard photoperiod plant

**Recovery time:** 4–8 weeks before normal training compatibility.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Cutting too late in flower (week 7+) → clone won't re-veg
- Trying on autoflower → won't work, autos can't re-veg
- Training during the weird-leaf phase → confused plant, stalled growth

### Re-vegging

**What it is:** Flowering plant reverted to vegetative growth by switching back to 18+ hour photoperiod. Useful for:
- Saving genetics from a finished plant (after harvest, leave a few small lower buds, switch to 18/6)
- Recovering from accidental light leaks during dark cycle
- Extending a single plant across multiple harvest cycles

**How to execute:**
1. Leave 2–3 small lower buds intact at harvest (don't strip the plant)
2. Switch photoperiod to 18/6 or 24/0
3. Wait 4–8 weeks (sometimes 12+ weeks) for normal growth resumption
4. Initial regrowth is weird (same as monstercrop)
5. Train as normal once normal-leaf growth resumes

**Pros:** preserves genetics without keeping a vegetative mother; recovery from accidents.

**Cons:** slow; some plants don't re-veg well; weird leaf phase is mandatory.

### Stem Tying / Staking

**What it is:** Physical support for heavy buds in late flower. Bamboo stakes, soft plant ties, or plant yo-yo systems.

**When to use:** late flower (week 4+) when bud weight starts pulling branches down. Heavy-yielding genetics may need support before week 4.

**Result:** prevents branch snap; keeps buds in canopy plane; reduces airflow disruption from sagging branches.

## AUTOFLOWER TRAINING

Autoflowers are different enough that they get their own section. The fundamental constraint: 70–110-day fixed lifecycle, 3–4 weeks of veg, and the plant pre-flowers regardless of size. There is no recovery time available.

### What works on autoflowers

| Technique | Notes |
|---|---|
| **LST** | The default for autos; start at 3rd node, gentle bending, recovery <48h |
| **Selective defoliation** | Light fan-leaf removal only; never schwazzing |
| **SCROG (gentle)** | Install screen low; weave gently; flip when 60–70% full |
| **Tying / staking late flower** | Same as photoperiod |

### What doesn't work (or is risky)

| Technique | Why |
|---|---|
| **Topping** | 5–7 day recovery = 25% of veg lost; usually net yield loss |
| **FIM** | Same recovery problem |
| **Mainlining** | Multiple toppings = whole veg burned in recovery |
| **Super cropping** | HST recovery; rarely worth the loss |
| **Schwazzing** | Catastrophic; autos don't have leaf reserves to spare |
| **Monstercropping** | Autos don't re-veg from clones |

### The autoflower exception

Some "autoflower 2.0" / strong modern autoflower genetics tolerate **a single early topping at week 3 from sprout**, but only in experienced hands. Test on one plant first; expect the topped plant to yield similar or slightly less than untopped, with more colas of smaller individual size.

For most growers: **autoflower = LST + maybe gentle SCROG + selective defoliation. Done.**

## WEEK-BY-WEEK MASTER TIMELINE

The integrated picture for a typical photoperiod grow:

| Week | Stage | Training actions |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Seedling | Nothing — establish |
| 2 | Seedling → early veg | Transplant if needed; nothing else |
| 3 | Early veg (4–5 nodes) | **First topping** at 4th–5th node OR start LST at 3rd node |
| 4 | Mid veg | Tie down side branches; gentle LST; install SCROG screen if planned |
| 5 | Mid veg | **Second topping** for mainline OR continue LST + screen weaving |
| 6 | Late veg | **Third topping** if doing 8-cola manifold; or finalize LST canopy |
| 7 | Pre-flip prep | Final LST adjustments; defoliation light pass; super crop tall outliers |
| 8 / Pre-flip | Pre-flip | All HST cuts done at least 7–10 days ago; final canopy organize |
| Flip + Week 1 | Stretch | **Schwazz #1** if doing; tuck SCROG; gentle LST |
| Week 2 flower | Stretch ends | Continue tucking until day ~10; install first trellis net |
| **Week 3 flower** | **Last training window** | **Schwazz #2** if doing + **lollipop** + general fan cleanup |
| Week 4–6 flower | Bud development | Selective fan removal only; install second trellis net |
| Week 7+ flower | Late flower / ripening | Hands off; only remove dying / heavily shading leaves |
| Last 2 weeks | Pre-harvest | No training of any kind |

## Quick Reference — Technique Selection Decision Tree

```
START → Plant type?
├── Photoperiod → continue
└── Autoflower → LST-only path (skip the rest)

Goal?
├── Max yield single plant in tent → mainlining + SCROG + lollipop
├── Max yield multi-plant tent → SOG (no training) OR LST + topping
├── Max yield commercial → topping + trellis layers + lollipop + selective defol
├── Stealth / short height → super cropping + heavy LST + topping
├── Genetics preservation → monstercropping for unique pheno expression
└── Save a single plant from harvest → re-veg

Space constraints?
├── Tight ceiling → super cropping + heavy LST + early topping
├── Wide flat → SCROG + trellis + mainline
└── Tall column / chimney → SOG single cola or no training

Time available?
├── 4+ weeks veg → mainlining (3 cuts) or manifold (2 cuts)
├── 2–4 weeks veg → topping + LST
├── 1–2 weeks veg → SOG no training
└── Autoflower (fixed) → LST only
```

## Common Mistakes & Misdiagnosis Traps

| Looks like | Actually | How to tell |
|---|---|---|
| "Plant looks tired after topping" | Normal recovery pause | 5–7 days of slowed growth is expected; recovery starts day 3 |
| "Branches snapped during super cropping" | Too aggressive pinch + bend | Pinch 5+ seconds without bending first; let stem soften |
| "Topping killed my auto" | Yes, that's what topping does to most autos | Avoid topping autoflowers |
| "SCROG screen too high" | Install at canopy level, not above | Should be 8–12" above plant base |
| "Schwazzing yielded less" | Strain-dependent; many genetics don't tolerate | Test on 1 plant before adopting wholesale |
| "Re-veg taking forever" | 4–12 weeks is normal | Patience; some clones take longer |
| "LST tied too tight" | Circulation cut off | Loosen and re-tie weekly; soft ties only |
| "Defoliation in late flower stalled buds" | Yes — late-flower defol stresses | Selective only after week 3 of flower |
| "FIM cut produced uneven mains" | That's normal for FIM | Use clean topping if symmetric outcome desired |
| "Plant grew over the SCROG screen" | Flipped too late OR didn't tuck enough | Flip at 50–75% screen full; tuck weekly |
| "Single-finger rounded leaves" after monstercrop | Normal re-veg phase | Wait 4–8 weeks for normal growth resumption |
| "Lollipop felt too aggressive" | If buds are still developing fine, you cut right | Lower-canopy popcorn would have been low-quality anyway |
| "Mainline cuts produced weak side branches" | Cut too early (before 3rd node) | Wait for clear 3rd node before first topping |

## Verification Questions Before Cutting

1. Plant type — photoperiod or autoflower?
2. Stage and node count from soil?
3. Last training event and recovery status (full or in-progress)?
4. Strain compatibility verified (sativa-stretch / indica-bushy / known training response)?
5. Sterile cutting tools? (HLVd transmission risk — see `09-disease.md`)
6. Recovery time available before flip (7+ days minimum after HST)?
7. Plant healthy and not stressed from other factors (deficiency, pest, environment)?
8. Container size accommodating planned canopy spread?
9. Light height adjustable for new canopy shape?
10. Backup plan if technique fails (extra plants, time buffer)?

If 4+ unknown → don't cut yet. Get the data and the plan.
